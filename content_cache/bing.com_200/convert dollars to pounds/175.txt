       <h3><a href="#p61174">Re: pennies into pounds</a></h3> 
       <p class="author"><a href="./viewtopic.php?p=61174&amp;sid=bd9fec7a42effd3e8c5d855f55cd696e#p61174"><img src="./styles/convert-me/imageset/icon_post_target.gif" width="11" height="9" alt="Post" title="Post"></a>by <strong>PHXinArizona</strong> » Sun Nov 20, 2011 6:11 pm </p> 
       <div class="content">
        It depends on the date of the penny. Lincoln pennies minted from 1909 to 1982 are 95% copper and weigh 3.11 grams each. (Except the 1943 steel pennies which weigh less)
        <br>
        <br>In mid 1982 pennies were switched from being made mostly of copper to zinc with a very thin coating of copper. These zinc pennies (including ones made today) weigh 2.5 grams.
        <br>
        <br>These weights are for pennies that are in new condition and haven't been worn down by lots of handling.
        <br>
        <br>1000 grams = 1 kilogram
        <br>
        <br>1 kilogram = 2.2 pounds
        <br>
        <br>1000/2.2 = 454.54 grams to a pound
        <br>
        <br>454.54/16 = approx. 28.41 grams per ounce (16 ounces = 1 pound)
        <br>
        <br>3.11/28.41 = approx. 0.11 Weight in ounces of one pre-1982 Lincoln penny
        <br>
        <br>2.5/28.41 = approx. 0.088 Weight in ounces of one post-1982 Lincoln penny
        <br>
        <br>It would take approx. 146 pre-1982 pennies to equal one pound. (28.41/3.11 x 16)
        <br>
        <br>And approx. 182 post-1982 pennies to equal one pound (28.41/2.5 x 16)
        <br>
        <br>The composition of the Lincoln penny was changed in mid-1982. Some 1982's are copper, some are zinc. You would have to weigh each individually to determine without actually cutting into it to get the composition of the coin.
