       <h1> <span style="font-size:32px;">How Much Does a Gallon of Propane Weigh?</span></h1>

       <h2 style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt;"> <span style="font-size:24px;">How Many Gallons in a 20 Pound Propane Tank?</span></h2> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt;"> 
        <br> 
        <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt;"> 
         <span style="font-size:18px;"><img alt="20 pound propane tank" src="/images/stories/Sng9kgDE190.png" style="margin: 10px 55px;" title="20 Pound Propane Tank"></span>
        </div> 
       </div> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt;"> 
        <span style="font-size:16px;">A 'full' 20 pound propane tank would contain 4.73 gallons of propane.</span>
       </div> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt;"> 
        <span style="font-size:16px;">Note that in some US tank exchange programs, the fill is something less than 20 pounds and is labelled accordingly.</span>
       </div> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt;"> 
        <span style="font-size:16px;">The image above is a typical 20 pound (9kg) propane tank.</span>
       </div> 
       <h2 style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt;"> <span style="font-size:24px;">How Many Gallons in a 100 Pound Propane Tank?</span></h2> 
       <h2 style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt;"> <span style="font-size:24px;"><img alt="100 pound propane tank" src="http://www.elgas.com.au/images/stories/Cylinder45kg250px.png" style="float: right; margin: 10px;"></span></h2> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt;"> 
        <span style="font-size:16px;"><span style="font-size:16px;">Based on the conversion chart above,</span> there are 23.64 gallons in a 100 pound propane tank.</span>
       </div> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt;"> 
        <span style="font-size:16px;">A 100 pound tank is roughly the same size as a 45kg gas bottle.</span>
       </div> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt;"> 
        <span style="font-size:16px;">They are small enough to be exchanged or tanker filled.</span>
       </div> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt;"> 
        <span style="font-size:16px;">The image to the right is of a typical 100 pound propane tank.</span>
       </div> 
       <h3 style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt;"> <span style="font-size:28px;">Background Information</span></h3> 
       <h2 style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt;"> <span style="font-size:20px;">Propane Specific Gravity - Density</span></h2> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt"> 
        <span style="font-size: 16px">Unlike water, <span style="font-size: 16px">1 pint of propane does not weigh ? </span>1lb.&nbsp; It actually weighs 0.51lbs.</span>
       </div> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt"> 
        <span style="font-size: 16px">This is because the density or specific gravity of propane is only about half that of water.&nbsp; </span>
       </div> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt"> 
        <span style="font-size: 16px">Converting from weight to volume, 1lb of propane <span style="font-size: 16px">@ 60ºF</span> has a volume of 0.24 US gallons.</span>
       </div> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt"> 
        <span style="font-size: 16px">Conversely, 1 US gallon of propane <span style="font-size: 16px">@ 60ºF</span> only weighs 4.23lbs, instead of the 8.34lbs it would weigh if it was water.</span>
       </div> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt"> 
        <span style="font-size: 16px">Important Note:</span>
       </div> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt"> 
        <span style="font-size:18px;">All volumes are quoted in US gallons, as opposed to imperial gallons.</span>
       </div> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt"> 
        <span style="font-size:18px;"><span style="font-size:18px;">Imperial gallons are 20% larger.</span></span>
       </div> 
       <h2 style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt;"> <span style="font-size:20px;">Propane Gas Energy Content in BTUs</span></h2> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt"> 
        <span style="font-size: 16px">BTU is the acronym for British thermal unit.&nbsp; It is a unit of energy. </span>
       </div> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt"> 
        <span style="font-size: 16px">One BTU is the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of 1lb of water by 1°F, at sea level.</span>
       </div> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt"> 
        <span style="font-size: 16px">Appliances are rated in BTU based on the required energy input.&nbsp; </span>
       </div> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt"> 
        <span style="font-size: 16px">The relative efficiency of the appliance determines the heat output.</span>
       </div> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt"> 
        <span style="font-size: 16px">BTU's are used by propane companies to determine total gas load on a system when everything is running on maximum.</span>
        <span style="font-size: 16px"> </span>
       </div> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt"> 
        <span style="font-size: 16px">More propane energy content facts:</span>
       </div> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt"> 
        <span style="font-size: 16px">1 US gallon of propane = 91,502 BTU @ 60ºF</span>
       </div> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt"> 
        <span style="font-size: 16px">1ft<sup>3</sup> propane gas = 2,488 BTU of Gas @ 60ºF</span>
       </div> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt"> 
        <span style="font-size: 16px"><span style="font-size: 16px">1 US gallon propane = 1.1 therm </span></span>
       </div> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt"> 
        <span style="font-size: 16px"><span style="font-size: 16px"><span style="font-size: 16px">1 therm = 100,000 BTU</span></span></span>
       </div> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt"> 
        <span style="font-size: 16px">1 watt = 3.41214 BTU/h</span>
       </div> 
       <h2 style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt;"> <span style="font-size:20px;">Propane Liquid to Gas Volume Expansion</span></h2> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt"> 
        <span style="font-size: 16px">Propane expands about 270 times in volume when it goes from liquid to gas.&nbsp; </span>
       </div> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt"> 
        <span style="font-size: 16px">So, 1 US gallon of liquid propane equals 36.4 ft<sup>3 </sup>of gaseous propane</span>
       </div> 
       <h2 style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt;"> <span style="font-size:20px;">Gas Bottle Tare Weight</span></h2> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt"> 
        <span style="font-size: 16px">Tare weight is the empty weight of the gas bottle-cylinder. &nbsp;</span>
       </div> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt"> 
        <span style="font-size: 16px">Along with the gas bottle dimensions, they can vary greatly by manufacturer and date of manufacture.&nbsp; </span>
       </div> 
       <div style="margin: 0in 0in 10pt"> 
        <span style="font-size: 16px">For example, older bottles tend to be heavier than more recent versions.</span>
       </div>
