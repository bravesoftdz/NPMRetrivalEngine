               Displaying financial amount in respective currency is common requirement in Java based E-commerce applications. For example if you are selling products on-line globally, you will show price of product in their local currency rather than USD or some other currency. Storing price in every currency is not a good option because of maintenance, and more realistically fluctuation in exchange rates. That's why many of these application prefer stores price of books, electronic goods or whatever product they are selling in USD, and responsibility of converting that price to local currency and displaying is left to client side code. If your client is Java based client e.g. Swing GUI , Java FX client, or a 
              <a href="http://javarevisited.blogspot.sg/2014/02/how-to-create-tab-ui-using-html-css-jQuery-JavaScript-JSP.html">JSP web page</a>, you can use 
              <span style="font-family: &quot;courier new&quot; , &quot;courier&quot; , monospace;">java.text.NumberFormat</span> class to 
              <i>format currency in Java</i>.
              <br> 
              <a name="more"></a>
              <br> NumberFormat class allows you to display price in multiple currency depending upon Locale supported by your Java version. All you need to do is to get correct Currency Instance based upon Locale, for example to display amount in US dollar, call 
              <span style="font-family: &quot;courier new&quot; , &quot;courier&quot; , monospace;">NumberFormat.getCurrencyInstance()</span> method with Locale as 
              <span style="font-family: &quot;courier new&quot; , &quot;courier&quot; , monospace;">Locale.US</span>, similarly to display currency as UK pound sterling, pass 
              <span style="font-family: &quot;courier new&quot; , &quot;courier&quot; , monospace;">Locale.UK</span> and for displaying money in Japanese Yen, pass 
              <span style="font-family: &quot;courier new&quot; , &quot;courier&quot; , monospace;">Locale.JAPAN</span>.
              <br> 
              <br> Once you get the correct instance of currency formatter, all you need to do is call their 
              <span style="font-family: &quot;courier new&quot; , &quot;courier&quot; , monospace;">format()</span> method by passing your number. We will see an example of converting one currency to other and displaying amount in multiple currency in Java.
              <br> 
              <br> It's actually very much similar to 
              <a href="http://java67.blogspot.sg/2013/01/how-to-format-date-in-java-simpledateformat-example.html" rel="nofollow">formatting date in Java</a>, so if you are familiar to that part, it would be easy to format currency as well.
              <br> 
              <br>

              <h3 style="text-align: left;"> <u>How to format &nbsp;Currency in Java</u></h3> Here is our sample program to format a number e.g. 
              <span style="font-family: &quot;courier new&quot; , &quot;courier&quot; , monospace;">double </span>or 
              <span style="font-family: &quot;courier new&quot; , &quot;courier&quot; , monospace;">int </span>to Currency in Java e.g. USD or GBP with dollar and pound sign. When we say formatting currency or formatting number as currency, what we mean is to 
              <i>show respective currency sign</i> in front of price/amount. For example to display prince in USD, you will show something like 
              <span style="font-family: &quot;courier new&quot; , &quot;courier&quot; , monospace;">$100.25</span>.
              <br> 
              <br> Similarly to show same price to UK customer in Pound, you will show 
              <span style="font-family: &quot;courier new&quot; , &quot;courier&quot; , monospace;">£60.15</span> and if you are doing business in Asia, and have &nbsp;Japanese customers, you will show prince in their local currency as well e.g. Japanese Yen, like&nbsp;
              <span style="font-family: &quot;courier new&quot; , &quot;courier&quot; , monospace;">?10,279</span>. Did you notice currency sign in front of amount or price? This is what makes that number and amount rather than just a number.
              <br> 
              <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;"> 
               <a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-eYxPVfEJllg/UwnnEtaU-aI/AAAAAAAABOI/F1HsaNl6ImU/s1600/Currency+Conversion+in+Java.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img alt="how to format Currency in Java NumbeFormat Example" border="0" height="499" src="https://3.bp.blogspot.com/-eYxPVfEJllg/UwnnEtaU-aI/AAAAAAAABOI/F1HsaNl6ImU/s640/Currency+Conversion+in+Java.jpg" title="" width="640"></a>
              </div> 
              <br> Java also put comma, just like the way write a bigger amount. This type of Locale specific issue becomes really tricky if you are doing this by hard-coding. Suppose you started your business with USD, and then start selling your product on England, if you keep storing price in database in multiple currency you will face hell lot of issue. Instead of that, just keep price in one currency in database, get the latest FX rate and covert price in real time for display.
              <br> 
              <br> Java API has rich support for formatting currency by 
              <span style="font-family: &quot;courier new&quot; , &quot;courier&quot; , monospace;">java.text.NumberFormat</span> class, and good knowledge of it will save lot of your time, so do invest some time on this class.
              <br> 
              <br> Also this program is compiled using JDK 1.7, did you notice 
              <a href="http://javarevisited.blogspot.sg/2011/08/string-switch-case-jdk7-example.html">use of String in Switch case</a>, though I am not a big fan of it in production code, it works perfectly in short tutorials.

                <span style="color: #008800; font-weight: bold;">return</span> <span style="color: #0000dd; font-weight: bold;">0.60</span>;
            <span style="color: #008800; font-weight: bold;">case</span> <span style="background-color: #fff0f0; color: #dd2200;">"EURO"</span>:
                <span style="color: #008800; font-weight: bold;">return</span> <span style="color: #0000dd; font-weight: bold;">0.73</span>;
            <span style="color: #008800; font-weight: bold;">default</span>:
                <span style="color: #008800; font-weight: bold;">throw</span> <span style="color: #008800; font-weight: bold;">new</span> <span style="color: #0066bb; font-weight: bold;">IllegalArgumentException</span>(String.<span style="color: #336699;">format</span>(<span style="background-color: #fff0f0; color: #dd2200;">"No rates available for currency %s %n"</span>, currency));
        }
    }

}


Output
Price in USD : $100.<span style="color: #0000dd; font-weight: bold;">25</span> 
Price in GBP : <span style="background-color: #e3d2d2; color: #a61717;">£</span><span style="color: #0000dd; font-weight: bold;">60.15</span> &nbsp;</pre> 
              <span style="color: #333333; line-height: 16.25px;">Price in JPY : </span>
              <span style="background-color: #e3d2d2; color: #a61717; line-height: 16.25px;">?</span>
              <span style="color: #0000dd; font-weight: bold; line-height: 16.25px;">10</span>
              <span style="color: #333333; line-height: 16.25px;">,</span>
              <span style="color: #0000dd; font-weight: bold; line-height: 16.25px;">279</span>
              <br> 
              <br> 
              <br> That's all about 
              <b>how to display currency in Java</b>, or 
              <i>how to format a number like integer or double to currency in Java</i>. Remember, 
              <span style="font-family: &quot;courier new&quot; , &quot;courier&quot; , monospace;">NumberFormat </span>class is on 
              <span style="font-family: &quot;courier new&quot; , &quot;courier&quot; , monospace;">java.text</span> package and not on 
              <span style="font-family: &quot;courier new&quot; , &quot;courier&quot; , monospace;">java.util</span> package. I have instance when people searching for this class, thinking that by importing 
              <span style="font-family: &quot;courier new&quot; , &quot;courier&quot; , monospace;">java.util.*</span> they should get 
              <span style="font-family: &quot;courier new&quot; , &quot;courier&quot; , monospace;">NumberFormat </span>class as well. Let me know if you face any issue while formatting currency or displaying amount in multiple currency to Java clients e.g. Swing, Java FX or JSP pages.
