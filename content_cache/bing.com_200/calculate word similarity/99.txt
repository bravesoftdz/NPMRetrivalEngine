          <h1 class="entry-title"> How to measure the similarity between two words </h1>
          <!-- .entry-title --> 
         </header> 
         <div class="entry-content clearfix">  
          <div class="at-above-post addthis_tool" data-url="http://mynixworld.info/2012/09/24/how-to-measure-the-similarity-between-two-words/"></div>
          <div class="pf-content"> 
           <p style="text-align: justify;">One problem that I have encountered these days is related to "how to measure the similarity between two words".</p> 
           <p style="text-align: justify;">The problem is not new (in fact its solution is older than any person I know alive) and one solution could be a <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hash_function#Finding_similar_substrings" target="_blank">hash function</a> that will calculate a score for each word then you compare the both scores and if they are "close enough" then you can decide whether or not they are alike. It sounds like <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soundex" target="_blank">soundex</a> function, which in fact is a hashing system for English words.</p> 
           <p style="text-align: justify;">There are also other alternatives which seems to be even better. One is <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levenshtein_distance" target="_blank">Levenshtein edit distance</a>, a method that measure how many edit operations (such as insert/delete/substitute of a single char) should be done to transform one word into another. Another is the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Needleman-Wunsch" target="_blank">Needleman-Wunsch</a> algorithm which tries to find the optimal score when aligning two sequences (words). For two words with the same length one could use even the <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamming_distance" target="_blank">Hamming distance</a> (which counts how many chars are different, so it measures only the necessary substitutions that are required to change one word to another).</p> 
           <p style="text-align: justify;">Even if the Needleman-Wunsch algorithm is helping us because it provides the optimal alignment of the two sequences, for my problem the Levenshtein distance seems to be 1% better.</p> 
           <h3 style="text-align: justify;">Levenshtein distance</h3> 
           <div id="attachment_875" style="width: 505px" class="wp-caption aligncenter">
            <a href="http://mynixworld.info/wp-content/uploads/2012/09/levenshtein_sample1.png"><img class="size-full wp-image-875 " title="Levenshtein_sample" alt="measure the similarity between two words" src="http://mynixworld.info/wp-content/uploads/2012/09/levenshtein_sample1.png" width="495" height="681" srcset="http://mynixworld.info/wp-content/uploads/2012/09/levenshtein_sample1.png 495w, http://mynixworld.info/wp-content/uploads/2012/09/levenshtein_sample1-218x300.png 218w" sizes="(max-width: 495px) 100vw, 495px"></a>
            <p class="wp-caption-text">source: http://www-igm.univ-mlv.fr/~lecroq/seqcomp/node2.html</p>
           </div> 
           <p style="text-align: justify;">In the example above we have tried to find the similarity between the word "LEVENSHTEIN" and "LEVENSHMIT". So using the Levenshtein disntace algorithm you will get a number (bottom-right in matrix) that will show you how much it will cost you if you want to change first word into the second one:</p> 
           <ul>
            <li style="text-align: justify;">if we choose the first path/way then you have to delete the "T" letter then to substitute the "E" letter with a new one "M" (which does not exists in our source word) then to come up with another one "N" (which replaces the last "T"); so 3 edit operations</li> 
            <li style="text-align: justify;">if we choose the second path/way then you have to substitute the "T" with "M", delete the "E" and insert the "T" at the end of the word; also 3 edit operations</li> 
           </ul>
