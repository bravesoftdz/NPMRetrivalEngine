       <div itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/Article" class="content-wrap col-md-8 clearfix"> 
        <span class="hide" itemprop="name headline">Nutrition: How to Read a Nutrition Facts Label</span> 
        <meta itemprop="mainEntityOfPage" content="https://familydoctor.org/nutrition-how-to-read-a-nutrition-facts-label/"> 
        <div class="featured-image"> 
         <span itemprop="image" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject"> <img itemprop="contentUrl" src="https://familydoctor.org/wp-content/uploads/2007/10/shutterstock_269127809-705x469.jpg" height="469" width="705" alt="Young couple reading a nutrition label in the supermarket"> 
          <meta itemprop="url" content="https://familydoctor.org/wp-content/uploads/2007/10/shutterstock_269127809-705x469.jpg"> 
          <meta itemprop="height" content="469"> 
          <meta itemprop="width" content="705"> </span> 
        </div> 
        <div class="body-content"> 
         <div class="body-text" itemprop="articleBody"> 
          <div class="pf-content">
           <p>The nutrition facts label tells you what’s in the food you’re eating. It helps you determine if you have a healthy, balanced diet. Every packaged, or processed, product should have a label. Some restaurants also have nutrition facts information available. The label includes amounts in the areas below. They are listed per serving and as a percentage of the daily value. <a href="https://familydoctor.org/condition/43358/" title="Read more about ."></a><br>Here is an example of what’s listed on the nutrition facts label:<img class="size-medium wp-image-26111 alignright" src="https://familydoctor.org/wp-content/uploads/2007/10/nutrition-facts-label-148x300.png" alt="Example of a nutrition facts label" width="148" height="300" srcset="https://familydoctor.org/wp-content/uploads/2007/10/nutrition-facts-label-148x300.png 148w, https://familydoctor.org/wp-content/uploads/2007/10/nutrition-facts-label.png 165w" sizes="(max-width: 148px) 100vw, 148px"></p>
           <ul>
            <li>calories</li>
            <li>total fat</li>
            <li>saturated fat</li>
            <li>trans fat</li>
            <li>cholesterol</li>
            <li>sodium (salt)</li>
            <li>total carbohydrate</li>
            <li>dietary fiber</li>
            <li>sugars</li>
            <li>protein</li>
            <li>vitamins and minerals.</li>
           </ul>
           <h3><strong>Path to improved health</strong></h3>
           <p>Serving size is the first piece of information listed on the label. This is the amount of food that is typically eaten at one time. The size is in a basic household measurement, such as pieces, cups, or ounces. For example, a serving may be 7 potato chips or 1 cup of cereal.</p>
           <p>Serving size is an important factor in your diet. You should compare the amount of that food you normally eat to the serving size listed on the label. Eating large servings or portions can lead to weight gain. The larger your portions are, the more calories you eat. For example, the label may list a serving size as 7 potato chips. If you eat 14 chips, you are consuming twice the amount of calories and nutrients.</p>
           <p>The label provides a column of percentages called the percent daily value. It compares how much of a nutrient is in one serving of food to how much of that nutrient you should consume in one day. The percentages are based on a daily <abbr class="icon-tooltip" title="diet" content="The total food consumed by an individual. A therapeutic diet is a prescription of required or restricted foods." rel="tooltip">diet</abbr> of 2,000 calories. You’ll need to adjust the amounts if you eat more or less than 2,000 calories per day. Everyone has different <abbr class="icon-tooltip" title="calorie" content="The amount of energy needed to raise the temperature of one kilogram of water one degree. Commonly used to express the amount of energy-producing value in food." rel="tooltip">calorie</abbr> needs. These depend on your gender, age, and activity level.</p>
           <p>You get your daily calories from what you eat and drink. A healthy person should consume nutrients from the five basic food groups. Some ingredients, such as saturated and trans fats, are unhealthy. You only should eat these in small amounts. A serving with 5% or less of the daily value is low. A serving with 20% or more of the daily value is high.</p>
           <h3><strong>Things to consider</strong></h3>
           <p>Not all nutrients, or ingredients, listed on a label are equal. Some are good and should be eaten more. These include:</p>
           <ul>
            <li><strong>Fiber.&nbsp;</strong>Fiber helps your body digest the food you eat. It also can help lower your risk of <a href="https://familydoctor.org/condition/diabetes/" title="Read more about diabetes.">diabetes</a> and <abbr class="icon-tooltip" title="heart" content="The hollow, muscular organ responsible for pumping blood through the circulatory system." rel="tooltip">heart</abbr> disease. Food is high in <abbr class="icon-tooltip" title="fiber" content="1. A slender, thread-like structure of organ tissue. 2. In the diet, strands of complex carbohydrates (cellulose) that aren't digestible." rel="tooltip">fiber</abbr> if it contains 5 grams or more per serving. Men 50 years of age or younger should get at least 38 grams of fiber per day. Women 50 years of age or younger should get at least 25 grams of fiber per day. Fiber is found in fruits, vegetables, and whole grains. Look for the words “whole grain” on the package and ingredient list.</li>
            <li><strong>Vitamins and minerals. </strong>The main types include, <abbr class="icon-tooltip" title="vitamin" content="An organic substance found in food and essential in small quantities for good health." rel="tooltip">vitamin</abbr> A, vitamin C, calcium, and iron. Vitamin D and potassium also are important. Talk to your doctor about what vitamins and minerals you need and how much.</li>
           </ul>
           <p>Others nutrients are bad and should be eaten less. These include:</p>
           <ul>
            <li><strong>Saturated fat.</strong> This type of <abbr class="icon-tooltip" title="fat" content="A major energy source for animals and humans. Fat contains nine calories per gram." rel="tooltip">fat</abbr> can increase your risk of heart disease and high cholesterol. The average adult should consume less than 20 grams of saturated fat per day.</li>
            <li><strong>Trans fat.</strong> This type of fat also increases your risk of heart disease. Ideally, you should get 0 grams of trans fat per day. Keep in mind, companies can list 0 grams if it contains less than 0.5 grams of trans fat per serving. This means that your food may have trans fat even if the nutrition label says 0. Check the ingredient list for trans fat products. This includes any hydrogenated vegetable oils. Trans fat often is found in baked goods, fried foods, snack foods, and margarine.</li>
            <li><strong>Cholesterol.&nbsp;</strong>You should eat less than 300 milligrams of <a href="https://familydoctor.org/condition/cholesterol/" title="Read more about cholesterol.">cholesterol</a> per day. If you have heart disease, aim for less than 200 milligrams per day.</li>
           </ul>
           <h3><strong>Questions to ask your doctor</strong></h3>
           <ul>
            <li>How do I know how many calories I need?</li>
            <li>Are all products labeled “fat-free” good for me?</li>
            <li>If the nutrition facts label lists 0 grams of cholesterol, is it good for me?</li>
            <li>What if the serving size listed seems low?</li>
           </ul>
           <h3><strong>Resources</strong></h3>
           <p><a href="https://familydoctor.org/nutrition-determine-your-calorie-needs/">American Academy of Family Physicians, Nutrition: Determine Your Calorie Needs</a></p>
           <p><a href="https://familydoctor.org/vitamins-and-minerals-how-to-get-what-you-need/">American Academy of Family Physicians, Vitamins and Minerals: How to Get What You Need</a></p>
           <p><a href="https://www.fda.gov/food/guidanceregulation/guidancedocumentsregulatoryinformation/labelingnutrition/ucm385663.htm">U.S. Food and Drug Administration, Nutrition Facts Label</a></p>
