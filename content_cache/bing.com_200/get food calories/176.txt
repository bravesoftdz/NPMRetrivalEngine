      <p>A <b>snack</b> is a portion of <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a>, smaller than a regular <a href="/wiki/Meal" title="Meal">meal</a>, generally eaten between meals.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Snacks come in a variety of forms including packaged snack foods and other processed foods, as well as items made from fresh ingredients at home.</p> 
      <p>Traditionally, snacks are prepared from ingredients commonly available in the home. Often <a href="/wiki/Cold_cuts" class="mw-redirect" title="Cold cuts">cold cuts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruits</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leftovers" title="Leftovers">leftovers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nut_(fruit)" title="Nut (fruit)">nuts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sandwich" title="Sandwich">sandwiches</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Candy" title="Candy">sweets</a> are used as snacks. The <a href="/wiki/Dagwood_sandwich" title="Dagwood sandwich">Dagwood sandwich</a> was originally the humorous result of a cartoon character's desire for large snacks. With the spread of <a href="/wiki/Convenience_store" title="Convenience store">convenience stores</a>, packaged snack foods became a significant business. Snack foods are typically designed to be portable, quick, and satisfying. <a href="/wiki/Food_processing" title="Food processing">Processed</a> snack foods, as one form of <a href="/wiki/Convenience_food" title="Convenience food">convenience food</a>, are designed to be less perishable, more durable, and more portable than prepared foods. They often contain substantial amounts of <a href="/wiki/Sugar_substitute" title="Sugar substitute">sweeteners</a>, <a href="/wiki/Preservative" title="Preservative">preservatives</a>, and appealing ingredients such as <a href="/wiki/Chocolate" title="Chocolate">chocolate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">peanuts</a>, and specially-designed flavors (such as flavored <a href="/wiki/Potato_chip" title="Potato chip">potato chips</a>).</p> 
      <p>Beverages, such as <a href="/wiki/Coffee" title="Coffee">coffee</a>, are not generally considered snacks though they may be consumed along with or in lieu of snack foods.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p> 
      <p>A snack eaten shortly before going to bed or during the night may be called a (mid)night snack.</p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snack&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>In the United States, the first snack food was the <a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">peanut</a>. Peanuts first arrived from <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> via <a href="/wiki/South_american_slavery" class="mw-redirect" title="South american slavery">slave ships</a>, and became incorporated into African-inspired cooking on southern <a href="/wiki/Plantation" title="Plantation">plantations</a>. After the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a>, the taste for peanuts spread north, where they were incorporated into the culture of baseball games and <a href="/wiki/Vaudeville_theater" class="mw-redirect" title="Vaudeville theater">vaudeville theaters</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup></p> 
      <p>Along with popcorn (also of South American origin), snacks bore the stigma of being sold by unhygienic street vendors. The middle-class etiquette of the Victorian era (1837–1901) categorized any food that did not require proper usage of utensils as lower-class.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup></p> 
      <p>Pretzels were introduced to North America by the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> via <a href="/wiki/New_Amsterdam" title="New Amsterdam">New Amsterdam</a> in the 17th century. In the 1860s, the snack was still associated with immigrants, unhygienic street vendors, and saloons. Due to loss of business during the <a href="/wiki/Prohibition_in_the_United_States" title="Prohibition in the United States">Prohibition era</a> (1920-1933), pretzels underwent rebranding to make them more appealing to the public. <a href="/wiki/Packaging_and_labeling" title="Packaging and labeling">Packaging</a> revolutionized snack foods, allowing sellers to reduce contamination risk while making it easy to advertise brands with a <a href="/wiki/Logo" title="Logo">logo</a>. Pretzels boomed in popularity, bringing many other types of snack foods with it. By the 1950s, snacking had become an all-American pastime, becoming an internationally recognized emblem of middle American life.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup></p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Snacks_and_health">Snacks and health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snack&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Snacks and health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p><a href="/wiki/Healthy_diet" title="Healthy diet">Healthy snacks</a> include those that have significant <a href="/wiki/Vitamin" title="Vitamin">vitamins</a>, are low in <a href="/wiki/Saturated_fat" title="Saturated fat">saturated fat</a> and added <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugars</a>, and have a low <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> content. <sup id="cite_ref-healthy_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-healthy-4">[4]</a></sup> Examples of healthy snacks include:</p> 
      <ul> 
       <li>Foods that have <a href="/wiki/Whole_grain" title="Whole grain">whole grains</a><sup id="cite_ref-healthy_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-healthy-4">[4]</a></sup></li> 
       <li><a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">Fruits</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vegetable" title="Vegetable">vegetables</a><sup id="cite_ref-healthy_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-healthy-4">[4]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>Nuts and <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seeds</a><sup id="cite_ref-healthy_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-healthy-4">[4]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>Low-fat <a href="/wiki/Dairy_product" title="Dairy product">dairy products</a><sup id="cite_ref-healthy_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-healthy-4">[4]</a></sup></li> 
       <li>Lean <a href="/wiki/Meat" title="Meat">meats</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-healthysnacks_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-healthysnacks-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Egg_(food)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (food)">eggs</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Boiled_egg" title="Boiled egg">hard-boiled eggs</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-healthysnacks_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-healthysnacks-5">[5]</a></sup> and lean <a href="/wiki/Cheese" title="Cheese">cheese</a></li> 
      </ul> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutritional_concerns">Nutritional concerns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snack&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nutritional concerns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Government bodies such as <a href="/wiki/Health_Canada" title="Health Canada">Health Canada</a> recommend that people make a conscious effort to eat more healthy, natural snacks - such as <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vegetable" title="Vegetable">vegetables</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nut_(fruit)" title="Nut (fruit)">nuts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">cereal grains</a> – while avoiding high-calorie, low-nutrient <a href="/wiki/Junk_food" title="Junk food">junk food</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p> 
      <p>A 2010 study showed that children in the United States snacked on average six times per day, approximately twice as often as American children in the 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This represents consumption of roughly 570 calories more per day than U.S. children consumed in the 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Snacks_and_cognition">Snacks and cognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snack&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Snacks and cognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>A <a href="/wiki/Tufts_University" title="Tufts University">Tufts University</a> Department of Psychology empirical study titled "Effect of an afternoon <a href="/wiki/Confectionery" title="Confectionery">confectionery</a> snack on <a href="/wiki/Cognition" title="Cognition">cognitive</a> processes critical to learning" found that a consumption of a confectionery snack in the afternoon improved <a href="/wiki/Spatial_memory" title="Spatial memory">spatial memory</a> in the study's <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(statistics)" title="Sampling (statistics)">sample group</a>, but in the area of <a href="/wiki/Attention" title="Attention">attention</a> performance it had a mixed effect.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
