        <p class="body-el-text standard-body-el-text">Many frozen fruits and veggies are actually more nutritious than their fresh counterparts. (Studies show that heat and light destroy nutrients; frozen fruits and vegetables get around this problem because they are flash frozen, right after picking.) As for taste and texture, you'll have to go through a little trial and error. Certain brands, and certain vegetables or fruits within brands, are better than others. My personal favorites: Trader Joe's Haricots Verts (thin green beans), all the Cascadian Farm berries, boxed Green Giant Spinach, and Birds Eye New Baby Peas.</p>

        <p class="body-el-text standard-body-el-text"> Here, the cold truth about nutritious frozen foods:</p>
        <h3 class="body-el-subtitle standard-body-el-subtitle article-sub-title"><b>Frozen Fruit (Unsweetened)</b></h3>
        <p class="body-el-text standard-body-el-text"><b>Blackberries </b><br> Calories per cup: 97<br> Nutrition highlights per cup: An excellent source of fiber (8 g), frozen blackberries help you reach your daily requirement (25 g for women and 38 g for men).</p>
        <p class="body-el-text standard-body-el-text"> <b>Blueberries </b><br> Calories per cup: 79<br> Nutrition highlights per cup: A good source of fiber (4 g). Plus, they're a rich source of antioxidants, linked to reducing blood clots and improving memory.</p>
        <p class="body-el-text standard-body-el-text"> <b>Peaches </b><br> Calories per cup: 107<br> Nutrition highlights per cup: Plenty of vitamin C (236 mg), plus 4 g fiber and 443 mg potassium.</p>
        <p class="body-el-text standard-body-el-text"> <b>Strawberries </b><br> Calories per cup: 77<br> Nutrition highlights per cup: A good source of fiber (4 g), with 91 mg of vitamin C.</p>
        <h3 class="body-el-subtitle standard-body-el-subtitle article-sub-title"><b>Vegetables</b></h3>
        <p class="body-el-text standard-body-el-text"><b>Baby Lima Beans</b><br> Calories per cup: 217<br> Nutrition highlights per cup: A generous 10 g of fiber and 741 mg potassium (21% of the daily recommended amount). Plus, 57 mg calcium.</p>
        <p class="body-el-text standard-body-el-text"> <b>Broccoli</b><br> Calories per cup: 41<br> Nutrition highlights per cup: 5 g fiber, 1,605 IU of vitamin A (a third of the daily value), 87 mg calcium. Plus, it's a good source of sulforaphane, a compound that may help prevent cancer.</p>
        <p class="body-el-text standard-body-el-text"> <b>Brussels sprouts</b><br> Calories per cup: 53<br> Nutrition highlights per cup: 5 g fiber, 96 mg vitamin C, and cancer-fighting compounds called glucosinolates.</p>
        <p class="body-el-text standard-body-el-text"> <b>Butternut squash</b><br> Calories per cup: 148<br> Nutrition highlights per cup: Double the daily value for vitamin A,* with 551 mg potassium.</p>
        <p class="body-el-text standard-body-el-text"> <b>Cauliflower</b><br> Calories per cup: 32<br> Nutrition highlights per cup: Offers the full daily value for vitamin C and contains a cancer-fighting compound called S-methylmethane thiosulfonate.</p>
        <p class="body-el-text standard-body-el-text"> <b>Peas</b><br> Calories per cup: 111<br> Nutrition highlights per cup: 5 g fiber and 50% of the recommended daily value for vitamin C. Plus, it's an excellent source of lutein and zeaxanthin (antioxidants linked to the prevention of eye diseases such as cataracts and macular degeneration).</p>
        <p class="body-el-text standard-body-el-text"> *Vitamin A in fruits and vegetables comes from safe sources, beta and alpha carotene, so it's okay to have more than the recommended daily value.</p>
