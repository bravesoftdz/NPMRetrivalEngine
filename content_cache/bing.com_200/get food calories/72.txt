     <h2>Abstract</h2> Have you ever wondered how nutritionists know how many 
     <i>Calories</i> a certain food contains? In this project you will learn a method for measuring how many Calories (how much chemical energy) is available in different types of food. You will build your own 
     <i>calorimeter</i> to capture the energy released by burning a small food item, like a nut or a piece of popcorn. This project gives a new meaning to the phrase "burning calories!" 
     <h2>Objective</h2> 
     <p>The goal of this science project is to determine which food items store more chemical energy; you will determine this by burning the items and capturing the heat given off in a homemade calorimeter.</p>

     <h2>Introduction</h2> 
     <p>You know that the energy that keeps your brain and body going comes from the food you eat. Your digestive system and the cells in your body break down the food and gradually oxidize the resulting molecules in a series of chemical reactions to release energy that your cells can use and store. </p> 
     <p>In this project you will learn a method for measuring how much chemical energy is stored in different types of food and express your result in <b>Calories</b> (note the capital "C"), as this is the unit of energy widely used to quantify food energy. To measure the chemical energy stored in food, you will <b>oxidize</b> the food much more rapidly than the cells in our body do by burning it in air. To do this, you will use a homemade bomb calorimeter that captures and measures the heat energy released by burning food. The basic idea of a <b>calorimeter</b> is to release all the stored energy at once and capture all the released heat energy with a reservoir of water. Measuring the temperature of the water at the beginning and at the end of the trial will allow you to calculate the energy put into heating up the water. As all this energy comes from the chemical reaction, the calculation will reveal the actual amount of energy released during the reaction, or the energy originally stored in the food. However, this is only true if <i>all</i> the energy released during the reaction is used to heat the water, and none of it gets "lost." </p> 
     <p>Bomb calorimeters that are used by scientists are made such that practically <i>all</i> of the energy released during the chemical reaction (like burning food) is captured by the water. Your homemade version will not reach the same <b>efficiency</b> to catch the heat energy released while burning the food; in other words, only a fraction of energy stored in the food will be converted to heat energy of the water and measured by your calorimeter. For example, some of the energy might go into heating up the surrounding air instead of the water. Even catching half of the energy released (an efficiency of 0.5) is acceptable for a homemade calorimeter, as it is very difficult to transfer all the chemical energy stored in the food and released during oxidation into heat energy of the water in the calorimeter. Even with low efficiencies, this project will allow you to rank different kinds of food from more caloric to less caloric and will allow you to predict, with reasonable accuracy, the ratio of caloric content of different types of foods. </p> 
     <p>Now, let us see how to calculate the energy stored in the water for a measured increase in the temperature (in °C). The temperature difference times the mass of the water (in grams) will give you the amount of energy captured by the calorimeter, in <i>calories</i>, a unit of chemical energy. We can write this in the form of an equation: </p> 
     <span style="" class="math"> <script type="math/tex;mode=display"> Q_{water} = m_{water} \:c  \Delta T  </script> 
      <noscript style="color:red;">
       [Please enable JavaScript to view equation]
      </noscript> </span> 
     <script src="/Dirs/9/4/MathJax.js?config=sb" type="text/javascript"></script> 
     <p>where:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><b>Q<sub>water</sub></b> is the energy in the form of heat captured by the water, expressed in calories (cal); </li> 
      <li><b>m<sub>water</sub></b> is the mass of the water, expressed in grams (g); </li> 
      <li><b>c</b> is the <i>specific heat capacity</i> of water, which is 1 cal/(g °C) (1 calorie per gram per degree Celsius); and </li> 
      <li><b>?T</b> is the change in temperature, or the final temperature of the water minus the initial temperature of the water, expressed in degrees Celsius (°C). </li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>The unit <b>calorie (cal)</b> (lowercase "c") is defined by the heat capacity of water. One calorie is the amount of energy that will raise the temperature of 1&nbsp;g of water by 1&nbsp;°C. When we talk about food energy, we also use the word Calorie (Cal) (note uppercase "C"), but it is a different unit. It is the amount of energy needed to raise the temperature of 1&nbsp;kilogram (kg) (which equals 1,000&nbsp;g) of water by 1&nbsp;°C. So one Calorie (abbreviated as 1 Cal) is the same as 1,000 calories, also called 1&nbsp;<b>kilo calorie (kcal)</b>. In this project, for food Calories, we will be careful always to use an uppercase "C". Can you verify that the specific heat capacity of water equals 0.001&nbsp;Cal/(g °C)? </p> 
     <p>Now we will work through an example to make sure that the equation is clear. (We will use made-up numbers for the example; you will have to do the project for yourself to get actual measurements.) So let us say that we start out with 100&nbsp;milliliters (mL) of water in the calorimeter. Since 1&nbsp;mL of water has a mass of exactly 1&nbsp;g, this water will have a mass of 100&nbsp;g (<i>m<sub>water</sub></i> = 100&nbsp;g). The initial temperature of the water is 20&nbsp;°C. After burning up a small piece of food, we measure the water temperature again, and find that the final temperature is 24&nbsp;°C. Now we have all of the information we need to calculate the amount of heat captured by the calorimeter: </p> 
     <span style="" class="math"> <script type="math/tex;mode=display"> Q_{water} = m_{water} \:c  \Delta T  </script> 
      <noscript style="color:red;">
       [Please enable JavaScript to view equation]
      </noscript> </span> 
     <span style="" class="math"> <script type="math/tex;mode=display"> Q_{water} = 100 \:g  \times 1  \frac{cal}{g ^{\circ} C} \times \left( 24 ^{\circ} C - 20 ^{\circ} C  \right)  </script> 
      <noscript style="color:red;">
       [Please enable JavaScript to view equation]
      </noscript> </span>

     <p>Can you see why the specific heat capacity of water has such strange units (cal/(g °C))? Notice that the grams (g) from the mass of the water and the degrees Celsius (°C) from the change in temperature cancel out with the grams (g) and degrees Celsius (°C) in the denominator of the units for specific heat. That way you are left with units of calories, a measure of energy, which is what you want. </p> 
     <p>Eating a balanced diet is fundamental to good health. This project will give you a chance to learn about how much energy your cells can extract from different types of food. It is important to remember, though, that energy is only one measure of nutritional value. As you are doing your background research on this project, try to find out about other measures of a balanced diet in addition to food energy. </p>

     <h2>Experimental Procedure</h2> 
     <div class="note"> 
      <b>Safety Note</b>: Adult supervision is required! As with any project involving open flame, there is a fire hazard with this project. Make sure you work on a non-flammable surface. Keep long hair tied back. Be careful handling the items used in this experiment as they may be hot! Wear safety glasses.

     <h3> Assembling Your Calorimeter </h3> 
     <p>Use the diagram shown in Figure 1, to guide you through building your calorimeter.</p> 
     <div class="content-image page-break-avoid" style="text-align: center;"> 
      <img src="/Files/3357/5/Chem_img016.gif" alt="Homemade Calorimeter Diagram" width="330" height="339" class="search-thumbnail">
      <br> 
      <b>Figure 1.</b> Diagram of homemade calorimeter. This diagram labels the support as a glass rod, but you can use a wooden dowel, a pencil, or a metal rod for the support. 
     </div> 
     <ol> 
      <li>Use the can opener to remove the bottom from the larger can, so that you have a cylinder that is open on both ends. Be careful after opening as the edges might be sharp. 
       <ul> 
        <li><i>Note:</i> If you are not using the kit, you will need to select two cans that nest inside one another. The smaller can needs to sit high enough so that you can place the cork, needle, and food item beneath it. </li> 
       </ul></li> 
      <li>Cover the inside of the large can with aluminum foil. Folding the edge of the aluminum foil over the edge of the can will keep it in place.</li> 
      <li>As a helper holds the can in place for you, use a hammer and nail to make holes around one edge of the cylinder. Space the holes about 4–5&nbsp;cm apart. This edge will become the bottom edge when the can is standing vertically. The holes are shown as black spots in Figure 1 (the holes are there to allow air to come in and sustain the flame). </li> 
      <li>Put aside the large can and pick up the small can. As a helper holds the small can in place for you, use a hammer and nail to punch holes on opposite sides of the smaller can, about 1–4&nbsp;cm from the top (open end). These holes will be used to attach the support. Depending on the relative height of the cans, you can choose to punch bigger holes and to put the support through them as shown in Figure 1, or smaller holes and attach the support on top of the can with metal wire, as shown in Figure 2. <i>Note</i>: The diagram in Figure 1 labels the support as a glass rod, but you can use a wooden dowel, a pencil, or a metal rod for the support. Your support needs to be longer than the width of your large can.</li> 
     </ol> 
     <div class="content-image page-break-avoid" style="text-align: center;"> 
      <img src="/Files/6705/8/calorimeter-attach-rod.jpg" alt="To create a calorimeter using cans, a rod needs to be attached on top of the smaller can." width="325" height="433">
      <br> 
      <b>Figure 2.</b> The rod can be attached on top of the small can with metal wire as shown here, or threaded through the holes as shown in the diagram of Figure 1. 
     </div> 
     <ol start="7"> 
      <li>Put the supporting rod in place and attach it firmly. <i>Note</i>: It is not a good idea to use glue, as the glue might melt when the calorimeter gets hot.</li> 
      <li>Grasp the three needles and push the blunt ends into the cork, as shown in Figure 3. You will impale the food to be tested on the sharp ends of the needles. If needed, you can cut the cork lengthwise and let it rest on the cut side to reduce its height.</li> 
     </ol> 
     <div class="content-image page-break-avoid" style="text-align: center;"> 
      <img src="/Files/6706/8/calorimeter-cork.jpg" alt="A cork with needles will hold the food items to be burned in the calorimeter." width="250" height="360">
      <br> 
      <b>Figure 3.</b> A cork with needles will hold the food items to be burned. 
     </div> 
     <ol start="9"> 
      <li>To construct the calorimeter: 
       <ol type="a"> 
        <li>Place the aluminum pie pan on a heat resistant surface. </li> 
        <li>Put the cork with needles sticking up in the middle of the pan. </li> 
        <li>Place the larger can over the cork on the aluminum pie pan.</li> 
        <li>Hang the smaller can inside the big cylinder.</li> 
       </ol> <p>The final result is shown in Figure 4.</p> </li> 
     </ol>

         </ol></li> 
        <li>Have your calorimeter pieces close at hand, and ready for use. </li> 
        <li>Place the cork with food already impaled in the middle of the aluminum pie pan and light the food items with the long matches. Try the following if you have trouble igniting the food: 
         <ol type="i"> 
          <li>A big flame on your match will help ignite the food. A slight breeze helps create bigger flames. If you cannot find a fire-safe place with a breeze, consider using a fan (at safe distance).</li> 
          <li>Be patient, some food items like nuts might take a while to catch fire.</li> 
          <li>Definitely use <i>long</i> matches. This will make it safer and easier to keep the food item in the flame for a longer period of time.</li> 
         </ol></li> 
        <li>When at least one food item catches fire, place the <i>large</i> can around the cork, then carefully place the smaller can in place above the flame.</li> 
        <li>Allow the food item to burn itself out. Use smoke coming out of the top as an indicator to evaluate if the burning is still in process. Try the following if you have trouble keeping the fire alive in the calorimeter: 
         <ol type="i"> 
          <li>Some food items might keep burning when they were put in the calorimeter just smoldering, but others will need a real flame to keep the burning active in the calorimeter. Experiment a bit with whether the food items burn better with a vibrant flame. Figure 6 might give you some ideas.</li> 
          <li>Enlarge the holes at the bottom of your cylinder to allow more air to pass through.</li> 
         </ol> <p>If the food in your calorimeter burns well, you might opt to tent the calorimeter with aluminum foil so less heat is lost to the environment, leaving a small opening at the top to allow air circulation. </p> </li> 
        <li>Shortly after the food stops burning, carefully stir the water and measure the final temperature (<i>T<sub>f</sub></i>). Make sure the thermometer has reached a steady level before recording the value. </li> 
        <li>When the burnt food item has cooled, carefully remove it from the needles and weigh the remains. Record your value in the column "Food: <i>M<sub>f</sub></i>". Ideally, all the food should have burned up. If it is not, you will correct for this during your analysis by subtracting the final mass from the initial mass.</li> 
        <li>This will complete one trial for this food item.</li> 
       </ol> </li> 
     </ol> 
     <div class="content-image page-break-avoid" style="text-align: center;"> 
      <img src="/Files/6708/6/calorimeter-attach-food.jpg" alt="Food items place in the calorimeter are held in place by needles." width="500" height="493">
      <br> 
      <b>Figure 5.</b> Food items held in place by needles are placed so they touch each other. 
     </div>

      <br> 
      <b>Figure 6.</b> Some food items will need to be in flames before being put under the calorimeter. 
     </div> 
     <ol start="4"> 
      <li>Repeat step 3 for two additional trials of this food item.</li> 
      <li>Repeat steps 2 and 3 for the other food items you have on your list.</li> 
     </ol> 
     <h3> Analyzing Your Data </h3> 
     <ol> 
      <li>To analyze your data, you will first calculate the energy captured by the water for each trial. As explained in the <a href="#background">Introduction</a>, the energy captured by the water (<i>Q<sub>water</sub></i>) can be calculated from the mass of the water in your calorimeter (<i>m<sub>water</sub></i>), the change in temperature of the water (<i>T<sub>f</sub> - T<sub>i</sub></i>) and c, the <i>specific heat capacity</i> of water, which is 1 cal/(g °C) or 1/1,000 Cal/(g °C) using this equation: <span style="" class="math"> <script type="math/tex;mode=display"> Q_{water} = m_{water} \:c  \left(T_{f} - T_{i} \right)  </script> 
        <noscript style="color:red;">
         [Please enable JavaScript to view equation]
        </noscript> </span> <p>Use the data in your data table to calculate the heat energy captured by the water for each trial and record your result in the column labeled <i>Q<sub>water</sub></i>. </p> </li> 
      <li>The energy you just calculated (<i>Q<sub>water</sub></i>) reflects energy released by the total amount of food burned, or (<i>M<sub>f</sub> - M<sub>i</sub></i>) grams of food burned. Can you calculate how much <i>Q<sub>water</sub></i> would be if 1&nbsp;g of food was burned? We call this the energy per unit weight, and it is expressed in Cal/g. Write your number down in the column "<i>Q<sub>water</sub></i> for 1&nbsp;g food". </li> 
      <li>Average the energy per unit weight released per individual food item over all three trials and write your value in the last column of your data table. </li> 
      <li>Since in a homemade calorimeter, <i>only part</i> of the energy contained in the food and released during burning transfers into heat energy of the water, the measured values only reflect a fraction of the chemical energy contained in the food. Some of the energy will get lost to, for example, heating up the surrounding air. Still, you can learn a lot from your obtained values. Below are some ideas to get you started: 
       <ol type="a"> 
        <li>Order your food items from more caloric to less caloric. </li> 
        <li>Create a graph, listing the food items on the x-axis and the caloric content of 1&nbsp;g of food on the y-axis. Remember to label your axes, and add the units and a title to the graph.</li> 
        <li><i>Normalize</i> the caloric content of food items to the caloric content of one food item. To do this, choose one food item from your list (e.g. Cheerios) and fill in the values, as directed by the equations in Table 2. <br> <br> 
         <div class="content-table page-break-avoid" style="width: Auto;"> 
          <table> 
           <thead> 
            <tr> 
             <th>Food Item</th> 
             <th>Average Q for 1 g of Food <br>(Cal/g)</th> 
             <th>Average Caloric Content<br> Normalized to the<br> Caloric Content of Cheerios</th> 
            </tr> 
           </thead> 
           <tbody> 
            <tr>
             <td>Cheerios</td> 
             <td><i>Q<sub>Cheerios</sub></i></td> 
             <td>1</td>
            </tr> 
            <tr>
             <td>Food item 1</td> 
             <td><i>Q<sub>item 1</sub></i></td> 
             <td><i>Q<sub>item 1</sub> / Q<sub>Cheerios</sub></i></td>
            </tr> 
            <tr>
             <td>Food item 2</td> 
             <td><i>Q<sub>item 2</sub></i></td> 
             <td><i>Q<sub>item 2</sub> / Q<sub>Cheerios</sub></i></td>
            </tr> 
           </tbody> 
          </table> 
          <b>Table 2.</b> Table containing the average caloric values as measured for all food items and the normalized caloric content relative to the caloric content of Cheerios. 
         </div> <p>These relative values show you how much more or less caloric food items are with respect to the item chosen (here Cheerios). For example, if the normalized caloric content for nuts would be 2, it would inform you that these nuts contain double as many calories per gram of food than Cheerios do. </p> </li> 
        <li>Create a graph listing the food items on the x-axis and the normalized caloric content on the y-axis. Remember to label your axes, and add the units and a title to the graph.</li> 
        <li>Do you think the amount of Calories you measured is likely to be higher or lower than the true value for each food item? Why? If you can, look up the caloric content of your food items <i>per gram of food</i>. Does this confirm your hypothesis? </li> 
       </ol></li> 
      <li>The <a href="#background">Introduction</a> states that, unfortunately, in homemade calorimeters, part of the energy stored in the food does not make it to heat energy of the water. Can you suggest areas for improvement?</li> 
     </ol>
