         <div class="views-field views-field-title"> 
          <span class="field-content"><a id="faq-node-[nid]">How can I find the potassium content of certain foods? </a></span> 
         </div> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-body"> 
          <div class="field-content">
           <p>According to <a class="ext" href="http://www.fda.gov/Food/GuidanceRegulation/GuidanceDocumentsRegulatoryInformation/LabelingNutrition/ucm064894.htm#declare" id="anch_177" target="_blank">FDA regulations </a>, labeling potassium content on the Nutrition Facts Panel of food products is voluntary for manufacturers. The USDA provides a <a href="https://fnic-u.nal.usda.gov/food-composition/usda-nutrient-data-laboratory" id="anch_178" target="_blank">list</a> of the potassium content of selected foods per common measure. After clicking on the link, go to USDA Nutrient Lists. Select potassium as your first nutrient, sort by food name or nutrient content.&nbsp;</p> 
           <p>For more information on the nutrient content of a specific food or to find the potassium content of foods not listed in the chart, search the USDA <a class="ext" href="http://ndb.nal.usda.gov/ndb/search/list" id="anch_179" target="_blank">food composition database</a>.</p> 
           <p>From the search page:</p> 
           <ol>
            <li>Enter the food as a keyword (e.g., "strawberries") and click submit.</li> 
            <li>On the resulting page, click the button next to the description of the food you want information on or that is most similar to your food (e.g., "strawberries, raw") and click submit.</li> 
            <li>On the resulting page, select the serving size(s) (e.g., "1 cup, halves") and click submit. Note: 100 grams is usually automatically selected, but you can un-check it to select only the serving sizes you are interested in.</li> 
            <li>Once you click submit, the nutrient content quantities in the food youve chosen will be listed.</li> 
           </ol>
           <p>Finally, food labeling regulations are governed by the FDA. The FDA is evaluating the Nutrition Facts Panel to determine ways to help consumers make smart choices about their diet. Consumers may submit comments regarding labeling directly to the FDA. A general contact phone number for the FDA is 1-888-INFO-FDA (1-888-463-6332). You can also contact the FDA via e-mail at <a class="mailto" href="mailto:ConsumerInfo@fda.hhs.gov" id="anch_180">ConsumerInfo@fda.hhs.gov (link sends e-mail)</a> or via mail at:</p> 
           <p>Consumer Health Information Staff<br> HFI-40<br> 5600 Fishers Lane<br> Rockville, MD 20857</p>

         <div class="views-field views-field-title"> 
          <span class="field-content"><a id="faq-node-[nid]">Is the USDA Database for the Added Sugars Content of Selected Foods still available? </a></span> 
         </div> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-body"> 
          <div class="field-content">
           <p>The USDA NDL has removed the USDA Database for the Added Sugars Content of Selected Foods from the NDL website. This is due to constant changes in formulations for commercial, multi-ingredient foods, the primary contributor of added sugars to the diet. NDL is not recalculating added and intrinsic sugars at this time, in part, because brand name market shares and ingredients are changed so rapidly that these estimates are more a temporary cross-section in time than fixed values. No method can analyze for added sugars so their amounts must be extrapolated or supplied by food companies, many of which are not willing to make public such proprietary information. The <a class="ext" href="http://www.ars.usda.gov/Services/docs.htm?docid=12107" id="anch_184" taget="new" target="_blank">Agricultural Research Service</a>&nbsp;provides additional information about this decision.</p>

         <div class="views-field views-field-title"> 
          <span class="field-content"><a id="faq-node-[nid]">What is the difference between calories and kilocalories? </a></span> 
         </div> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-body"> 
          <div class="field-content">
           <p>The "calorie" we refer to in food is actually kilocalorie. One (1) kilocalorie is the same as one (1) Calorie (uppercase C). A kilocalorie is the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of one kilogram of water one degree Celsius. Visit USDA's <a class="ext" href="http://www.ars.usda.gov/Main/docs.htm?docid=6233" id="anch_186" target="_blank">NDL </a>&nbsp;for additional information.</p>
          </div>

         <div class="views-field views-field-title"> 
          <span class="field-content"><a id="faq-node-[nid]">Where can I find how much omega-3 and omega- 6 fatty acids are in different foods? </a></span> 
         </div> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-body"> 
          <div class="field-content">
           <p>Fatty acids are listed in the <a class="ext" href="http://www.ars.usda.gov/Services/docs.htm?docid=8964" id="anch_191" target="_blank"> Nutrient Data Laboratory (NDL) database </a>&nbsp;by their molecular name, such as 18:4 or 16:0. The most common omega-3 fatty acids are linolenic (18:3), EPA (20:5), DHA (22:6). The most common omega-6 fatty acids are linoleic (18:2) and arachidonic (20:4). In order to see the content of omega- 3 and omega- 6 fatty acids:</p> 
           <ul>
            <li>Click on Full Report (All Nutrients) at the top of the screen, underneath the name of the food.</li> 
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>Scroll down to the next to last header on the page and click on the green button with a white plus mark next to Lipids. A list of common fatty acids will appear. As noted above:</li> 
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>Common Omega-3 fatty acids are: linolenic (18:3), EPA (20:5), DHA (22:6)</li> 
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>Common Omega-6 fatty acids are: linoleic (18:2) and arachidonic (20:4)</li> 
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>A detailed listing of systematic scientific and common names of fatty acids is available at the <a class="ext" href="http://nutritiondata.self.com/topics/fatty-acids" id="anch_192" target="_blank">Fats &amp; Fatty Acids page </a>&nbsp;of the Nutrition Data website. Omega-3 and omega-6 isomers include "n-3" and "n-6" markings.</li> 
           </ul>
           <ul>
            <li>Add the amounts of all Omega 3 fatty acids to get a total amount for Omega 3s. Add up the amounts of all Omega 6 fatty acids to get a total amount for Omega 6s.</li> 
           </ul>
           <p>Please be aware of the serving sizes listed for each food (i.e. 100 grams of coconut oil, 1 tablespoon of flaxseed oil, etc.). You may need to adjust these values to accurately determine the fatty acid content of a portion of food.</p>
          </div> 
         </div>

        <h3><span id="faq-category-10880">Health and Wellness Events</span></h3>

         <div class="views-field views-field-body"> 
          <div class="field-content">
           <p>&nbsp;For reliable, vetted nutrition information, start your research at <a href="https://www.nal.usda.gov/fnic" id="anch_245" target="_blank">FNIC</a>.            <p>However, we are unable to provide speakers for events or grant interviews for student assignments, including research papers.         <h3><span id="faq-category-10881">Meal Planning, Shopping and Cooking</span></h3> 
        <div class="views-row views-row-1 views-row-odd views-row-first"> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-title"> 
          <span class="field-content"><a id="faq-node-[nid]">Do the fatty acids in olive oil convert to trans fatty acids when heated? </a></span> 
         </div> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-body"> 
          <div class="field-content">
           <p>&nbsp;The hydrogenation process that results in the formation of trans fatty acids is a drastic procedure involving the use of hydrogen, high pressure, a catalyst and sometimes high temperatures. These conditions are more extreme than the heating that takes place in normal cooking. Therefore, it is not likely that the fatty acids in olive oil are converted to trans fatty acids upon heating.</p>
          </div> 
         </div> 
        </div>

         <div class="views-field views-field-title"> 
          <span class="field-content"><a id="faq-node-[nid]">What are Dietary Reference Intakes (DRIs) and how are they determined? </a></span> 
         </div> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-body"> 
          <div class="field-content">
           <p>The DRIs are set by the IOM's FNB and can be accessed from FNIC's <a href="https://www.nal.usda.gov/fnic/dietary-reference-intakes">Dietary</a><a href="https://www.nal.usda.gov/fnic/dietary-reference-intakes"> Reference Intakes page</a>. The DRIs are a common set of reference values for a healthy population based on the relationships between nutrient intakes and health or the prevention of disease. DRI is a generic term for a set of nutrient reference values that include the EAR, the RDA, the AI and the UL.</p> 
           <p></p>
           <ul>
            <br>
            <li>The EAR is the average daily nutrient intake level estimated to meet the requirement of half the healthy individuals in a particular life stage and gender group. In the case of energy, an EER is provided. The EER is the average dietary energy intake that is predicted to maintain energy balance in a healthy adult of a defined age, gender, weight, height and level of physical activity consistent with good health.</li>
            <br>
            <li>The RDA is the average daily dietary nutrient intake level sufficient to meet the nutrient requirement of nearly all (97 to 98 percent) healthy individuals in a particular life stage and gender group. The process for setting the RDA depends on being able to set an EAR and estimating the variance of the requirement itself. Note that if an EAR cannot be set due to limitations of the data available, no RDA will be set.</li>
            <br>
            <li>The AI is used when an RDA cannot be determined. The AI amount is a recommended average daily intake level based on observed or experimentally determined estimates of nutrient intake by apparently healthy people. There is much less certainty about an AI value than about an RDA value.</li>
            <br>
            <li>The UL is the highest level of daily nutrient intake that is likely to pose no risk of adverse effects for almost all people. As intake increases above the UL, the potential risk of adverse effects may increase.</li>
           </ul>
          </div>

        <h3><span id="faq-category-10884">Weight Management</span></h3> 
        <div class="views-row views-row-1 views-row-odd views-row-first"> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-title"> 
          <span class="field-content"><a id="faq-node-[nid]">How can I burn off my stored body fat? </a></span> 
         </div> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-body"> 
          <div class="field-content">
           <p>&nbsp;The best strategy for losing excess weight and stored body fat combines a plan for behavior change with calorie reduction and increased physical activity. See <a class="ext" href="http://www.nutrition.gov/weight-management/strategies-success/interested-losing-weight" id="anch_305" target="_blank">Interested in Losing Weight?</a>&nbsp;from Nutrition.gov and <a href="https://www.nal.usda.gov/weight-and-obesity/practical-healthy-weight-control" id="anch_306" target="_blank">Weight and Obesity &gt; Practical, Healthy Weight Control</a> for more information.We all need some body fat, but if stored fat is excessive it may increase risk of diet-related diseases such as heart disease, diabetes and some cancers. This is particularly true if excess fat is in the abdominal area. According to the CDC, a BMI of 25 or higher indicates that your weight may be unhealthy. If BMI is 25 or higher, a waist circumference of more than 40 inches in men and 35 inches in women indicates excess abdominal fat. CDC's <a class="ext" href="http://www.cdc.gov/healthyweight/assessing/index.html" id="anch_307" target="_blank">Assessing Your Weight</a>&nbsp;can help you calculate your BMI and measure your waist circumference.</p>
          </div> 
         </div> 
        </div> 
        <div class="views-row views-row-2 views-row-even"> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-title"> 
          <span class="field-content"><a id="faq-node-[nid]">How can I get enough nutrients without consuming too many calories? </a></span> 
         </div> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-body"> 
          <div class="field-content">
           <p>&nbsp;The <a class="ext" href="http://health.gov/dietaryguidelines/" id="anch_309" target="_blank">Dietary Guidelines for Americans</a>encourages you to choose a variety of nutrient-dense foods and beverages to help achieve recommended nutrient intakes. Foods like vegetables, fruits, whole grains, low-fat dairy products and lean proteins can help you get the nutrients you need without excess calories.You can avoid excess calories by limiting your consumption of foods high in added sugars and solid fats, and alcoholic beverages; these provide calories but are poor sources of essential nutrients. See <a class="ext" href="http://www.choosemyplate.gov/" id="anch_310" target="_blank">USDA's MyPlate</a>&nbsp;to learn more about choosing nutrient-dense foods.</p> 
           <p>Finally, because calorie intake must be balanced with physical activity to control weight, stay active. If you are thinking about being more active, the <abbr title="Weight-control Information Network's">WIN's</abbr> <a class="ext" href="http://www.niddk.nih.gov/health-information/health-topics/diet/changing-habits/Documents/Changing_Your_Habits_Flyer.pdf" id="anch_311" target="_blank">Climb These Steps to a Healthier You!</a>&nbsp;offers four steps to help you make healthy habits a part of your daily routine.</p>
          </div> 
         </div> 
        </div> 
        <div class="views-row views-row-3 views-row-odd"> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-title"> 
          <span class="field-content"><a id="faq-node-[nid]">I am on a diet to lose weight. Do I still need to exercise? </a></span> 
         </div> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-body"> 
          <div class="field-content">
           <p>&nbsp;When you are on a diet to reach a healthier weight, physical activity may support your weight loss efforts by helping you achieve an appropriate calorie balance. The CDC's <a class="ext" href="http://www.cdc.gov/healthyweight/calories/index.html" id="anch_313" target="_blank">Balancing Calories page</a>&nbsp;can help you find your own calorie balance. In addition, individuals who exercise regularly may be less likely to regain the weight they lost.</p> 
           <p>These resources provide more information about physical activity:</p> 
           <ul>
            <li>FNIC's <a class="ext" href="https://fnic.nal.usda.gov/lifecycle-nutrition/fitness-and-sports-nutrition" id="anch_314" target="_blank">Lifecycle Nutrition</a></li> 
            <li><a class="ext" href="http://health.gov/paguidelines/blog/post/How-much-daily-exercise-is-best-for-weight-loss.aspx" id="anch_315" target="_blank">Be Active Your Way: A Guide for Adults from the Physical Activity Guidelines for Americans</a></li> 
            <li><a class="ext" href="http://origin-www.choosemyplate.gov/physical-activity-what-is" id="anch_316" target="_blank">MyPlate Physical Activity</a></li> 
            <li><a class="ext" href="http://www.cdc.gov/healthyweight/physical_activity/index.html" id="anch_317" target="_blank">CDC's Physical Activity for a Healthy Weight</a></li> 
           </ul>
          </div> 
         </div> 
        </div> 
        <div class="views-row views-row-4 views-row-even"> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-title"> 
          <span class="field-content"><a id="faq-node-[nid]">I want to lose a pound of weight. How many calories do I need to burn? </a></span> 
         </div> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-body"> 
          <div class="field-content">
           <p>&nbsp;You need to burn 3,500 calories more than you take in to lose one pound of weight. This translates into a reduction of 500 calories per day to lose one pound in a week, or a reduction of 1,000 calories per day to lose two pounds in a week. A calorie reduction can be achieved by either eating fewer calories or burning more calories through physical activity. A combination of both is best. See CDC's <a class="ext" href="http://www.cdc.gov/healthyweight/calories/index.html" id="anch_319" target="_blank">Balancing Calories</a>to learn more.</p> 
           <p>Note: It is generally considered safe to lose weight at a rate of one to two pounds per week, but you should check with your healthcare provider or an RD before starting any weight loss program. RDs are health professionals who are trained to provide counseling on nutrition and eating habits. They can provide personalized dietary advice, taking into consideration your health status, lifestyle and food preferences. The Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics can help you locate an RD who specializes in your health condition with <a class="ext" href="http://www.eatright.org/find-an-expert" id="anch_320" target="_blank">Find a Registered Dietitian</a>.</p>
          </div> 
         </div> 
        </div> 
        <div class="views-row views-row-5 views-row-odd"> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-title"> 
          <span class="field-content"><a id="faq-node-[nid]">I would like to gain weight. How can I do this in a healthy manner? </a></span> 
         </div> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-body"> 
          <div class="field-content">
           <p>Losing, gaining or staying at the same weight all depend on how many calories you eat and how many calories your body uses over time. If you eat more calories than you use, you will gain weight; conversely, if you eat fewer calories than you use, you will lose weight. The <a class="ext" href="http://www.eatright.org/" id="anch_322" target="_blank">Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics</a>&nbsp;<a class="ext" href="http://www.eatright.org/" id="anch_323" target="_blank">Healthy Weight Gain</a>&nbsp;webpage provides some information and advice on how to gain weight and remain healthy.</p> 
           <p>Because many Americans are overweight, there are many resources geared toward losing weight. Some of these resources explain the principles of weight balance and can provide guidance for you to gain weight in a healthy manner; you will just need to focus on portion sizes for weight gain, rather than weight loss. One such resource is <a class="ext" href="http://www.nhlbi.nih.gov/files/docs/public/heart/AIM_Pocket_Guide_tagged.pdf" id="anch_324" target="_blank">Aim for a Healthy Weight</a>&nbsp;from the National Institute of Healths <a class="ext" href="http://www.nhlbi.nih.gov/" id="anch_325" target="_blank">National Heart Lung and Blood Institute</a>. There are many other weight control resources on the <a class="ext" href="http://fnic.nal.usda.gov/weight-and-obesity/practical-healthy-weight-control" id="anch_326" target="_blank">Practical, Healthy Weight Control </a>page of the <a class="ext" href="http://fnic.nal.usda.gov/weight-and-obesity" id="anch_327" target="_blank">Weight and Obesity </a>section of FNIC.</p> 
           <p>If you would like personalized advice, or you want to know how many calories or what types of foods are best for you, Registered Dietitians (RD) are health professionals who can physically assess you and your needs. In the United States, the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics has a referral service to registered dietitians. You can find a dietitian in your area by using the <a class="ext" href="http://www.eatright.org/find-an-expert" id="anch_328" target="_blank">Find a Registered Dietitian</a>&nbsp;referral service on their website.</p>
          </div> 
         </div> 
        </div> 
        <div class="views-row views-row-6 views-row-even"> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-title"> 
          <span class="field-content"><a id="faq-node-[nid]">What is a healthy diet? </a></span> 
         </div> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-body"> 
          <div class="field-content">
           <p>A healthy eating pattern is one that provides enough of each essential nutrient from nutrient-dense foods, contains a variety of foods from all of the basic food groups, and focuses on balancing calories consumed with calories expended to help you achieve and sustain a healthy weight. This eating pattern limits intake of solid fats, sugar, salt (sodium) and alcohol.</p> 
           <p>The Dietary Guidelines for Americans consumer pamphlet, <a class="ext" href="http://www.cnpp.usda.gov/sites/default/files/dietary_guidelines_for_americans/DG2010Brochure.pdf" id="anch_330" target="_blank">Let's Eat For the Health of It</a>&nbsp;(PDF | 968KB), provides guidance for creating a healthy eating pattern to reduce the risk of chronic diseases such as diabetes, cancer and osteoporosis. Go to the <a class="ext" href="http://health.gov/dietaryguidelines/" id="anch_331" target="_blank">Dietary Guidelines for Americans</a>&nbsp;for more information.</p>
          </div> 
         </div> 
        </div> 
        <div class="views-row views-row-7 views-row-odd"> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-title"> 
          <span class="field-content"><a id="faq-node-[nid]">When I eat more than I need, what happens to the extra calories? </a></span> 
         </div> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-body"> 
          <div class="field-content">
           <p>&nbsp;Consuming extra calories results in an accumulation of stored body fat and weight gain. This is true whether the excess calories come from protein, fat, carbohydrate or alcohol. See CDC's <a class="ext" href="http://www.cdc.gov/healthyweight/calories/index.html" id="anch_333" target="_blank">Balancing Calories</a>&nbsp;for more information about the calorie balance equation.</p>
          </div>

        <div class="views-row views-row-8 views-row-even"> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-title"> 
          <span class="field-content"><a id="faq-node-[nid]">Where can I find information about popular weight-loss diets? </a></span> 
         </div> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-body"> 
          <div class="field-content">
           <p>&nbsp;For reliable information, see <a class="ext" href="http://www.nutrition.gov/weight-management/what-you-should-know-about-popular-diets" id="anch_335" target="_blank">What You Should Know About Popular Diets</a>. Weight-loss diets have been popular for many years. In fact, many people have followed a weight-loss diet at one time or another. Unfortunately, most results are not permanent and some pose serious health risks. The popular low-carbohydrate, high-protein diet is an example of a strict weight-loss program that may carry potentially serious health risks.</p> 
           <p>Before you begin any weight-loss program, it is wise to speak to a qualified healthcare provider such as an RD for advice on a program that is right for you. RDs are health professionals who are trained to provide counseling on nutrition and eating habits. They can provide personalized dietary advice, taking into consideration your health status, lifestyle and food preferences. The Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics can help you locate an RD who specializes in weight management with <a class="ext" href="http://www.eatright.org/find-an-expert" id="anch_336" target="_blank">Find a Registered Dietitian</a>.</p> 
           <p>You may find some useful information on the web about weight loss and healthy eating. This is only for educational purposes and does not take the place of personalized advice from a qualified healthcare provider who is familiar with your particular situation. Although the web can provide useful information, some of the information can be inaccurate or misleading. Many sites devoted to weight loss are marketing special products.</p>

        <div class="views-row views-row-2 views-row-even"> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-title"> 
          <span class="field-content"><a id="faq-node-[nid]">Is it true that I can get all the vitamins and minerals I need from the food I eat? </a></span> 
         </div> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-body"> 
          <div class="field-content">
           <p>There is insufficient evidence to recommend or not recommend the use of multivitamin/mineral supplements for the prevention of chronic diseases for healthy Americans. Aim to get all the vitamins and minerals you need by eating nutrient-dense forms of foods, while balancing calorie intake with energy expenditure. Nutrient-dense foods contain essential vitamins and minerals, fiber and other naturally occurring substances that may have health benefits.</p> 
           <p>For more information, see <abbr title="Food and Nutrition Information Center">FNIC</abbr>'s <a href="https://www.nal.usda.gov/fnic/dietary-guidance-0">Dietary Guidance</a><a href="https://www.nal.usda.gov/fnic/dietary-guidance-0">&nbsp;page</a>&nbsp;and the <a href="https://www.cnpp.usda.gov/dietary-guidelines">Dietary Guidelines for Americans</a>.</p>
          </div> 
         </div> 
        </div> 
        <div class="views-row views-row-3 views-row-odd">

            <li><a class="ext" href="https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/list-VitaminsMinerals/" id="anch_139" target="_blank">Fact Sheets on Vitamins and Minerals&nbsp;</a></li> 
            <li><a class="ext" href="https://ods.od.nih.gov/factsheets/list-Botanicals/" id="anch_140" target="_blank">Fact Sheets on Botanicals&nbsp;</a></li> 
            <li><a class="ext" href="http://www.fda.gov/Food/DietarySupplements/UsingDietarySupplements/ucm480069.htm" id="anch_141" target="_blank">Dietary Supplements Q &amp; A </a>&nbsp;provides an overview of the FDA and its role in dietary supplement labeling.</li> 
            <li><a class="ext" href="http://www.fda.gov/Food/DietarySupplements/UsingDietarySupplements/ucm110567.htm" id="anch_142" target="_blank">Tips for the Dietary Supplement User: Making Informed Decisions and Evaluating Information&nbsp;</a></li> 
            <li><a class="ext" href="http://www.fda.gov/Safety/MedWatch/HowToReport/default.htm" id="anch_143" target="_blank">Reporting Serious Problems to the FDA</a>&nbsp;links to MedWatch, the FDA Safety Information and Adverse Event Reporting Program, which the FDA maintains to keep track of adverse reactions from dietary supplements.</li>

        <h3><span id="faq-category-10877">Food Labeling</span></h3> 
        <div class="views-row views-row-1 views-row-odd views-row-first"> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-title"> 
          <span class="field-content"><a id="faq-node-[nid]">Is there a law that requires food labels to list ingredients that commonly cause food allergies? </a></span> 
         </div> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-body"> 
          <div class="field-content">
           <p>The <a class="ext" href="http://www.fda.gov/Food/GuidanceRegulation/GuidanceDocumentsRegulatoryInformation/Allergens/ucm106187.htm" id="anch_208" target="_blank">Food Allergen Labeling and Consumer Protection Act of 2004</a>, which went into effect in January 2006, requires that food labels identify in plain English if the product contains any of the eight major food allergens: milk, eggs, fish, crustacean shellfish, peanuts, tree nuts, wheat and soybeans. For more information, see <a class="ext" href="http://www.fda.gov/Food/GuidanceRegulation/GuidanceDocumentsRegulatoryInformation/ucm059116.htm" id="anch_209" target="_blank">Guidance for Industry: Questions and Answers Regarding Food Allergens</a>.</p>
          </div> 
         </div> 
        </div> 
        <div class="views-row views-row-2 views-row-even"> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-title"> 
          <span class="field-content"><a id="faq-node-[nid]">What are the regulations for creating food product and nutrition labels? </a></span> 
         </div> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-body"> 
          <div class="field-content">
           <p>The Federal government provides regulations for food product and nutrition labeling which you, as the producer or manufacturer, are responsible for adhering to. The FDA of the DHHS is the Federal agency responsible for the majority of food products and their labeling. The USDA is responsible for fresh meat, poultry and eggs.</p> 
           <p>Neither department provides nutrient analysis or creates the Nutrition Facts label for a product. As the producer or manufacturer, you may select any company or entity to do your analysis and create your Nutrition Facts label, provided they follow the Federal regulations.</p> 
           <p>You may also first want to check the <a href="https://www.fda.gov/Food/GuidanceRegulation/GuidanceDocumentsRegulatoryInformation/LabelingNutrition/ucm2006867.htm">Small Business Nutrition Labeling Exemption</a>. The FDA provides food labeling guidance for food producers and manufacturers, including the <a href="https://www.fda.gov/Food/GuidanceRegulation/GuidanceDocumentsRegulatoryInformation/LabelingNutrition/ucm2006828.htm">Food Labeling Guide</a>. Also available is <a class="ext" id="anch_213" href="http://www.fda.gov/Food/GuidanceRegulation/GuidanceDocumentsRegulatoryInformation/LabelingNutrition/ucm063113.htm" target="_blank">Guidance for Industry: Nutrition Labeling Manual - A Guide for Developing and Using Data Bases</a>.</p>
          </div> 
         </div> 
        </div>

         <div class="views-field views-field-title"> 
          <span class="field-content"><a id="faq-node-[nid]">Why dont restaurants provide nutrition information? </a></span> 
         </div> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-body"> 
          <div class="field-content">
           <p>Current Federal regulations do not require restaurants to provide nutrition information unless they are making health or nutrient-content claims about food or menu items (e.g., low fat or low sodium) on menus, signs or placards. When such claims are made, nutrient information must be available upon request. Many restaurants provide nutrition information voluntarily for all items, but not all of them do.</p> 
           <p>For more information, visit FDA's <a class="ext" href="http://www.fda.gov/Food/IngredientsPackagingLabeling/LabelingNutrition/ucm248731.htm" id="anch_225" target="_blank">Questions and Answers on the New Menu and Vending Machines Nutrition Labeling Requirements</a>.&nbsp;If you have additional questions, you may want to <a class="ext" href="http://www.fda.gov/AboutFDA/CentersOffices/OfficeofFoods/CFSAN/default.htm" id="anch_226" target="_blank">contact</a>&nbsp;the CFSAN, the division of the FDA that regulates labeling.</p>

        <h3><span id="faq-category-10875">Food Allergies and Intolerances</span></h3> 
        <div class="views-row views-row-1 views-row-odd views-row-first"> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-title"> 
          <span class="field-content"><a id="faq-node-[nid]">Is there a law that requires food labels to list ingredients that commonly cause food allergies? </a></span> 
         </div> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-body"> 
          <div class="field-content">
           <p>The <a class="ext" href="http://www.fda.gov/Food/GuidanceRegulation/GuidanceDocumentsRegulatoryInformation/Allergens/ucm106187.htm" id="anch_160" target="_blank">Food Allergen Labeling and Consumer Protection Act of 2004</a>, which went into effect in January 2006, requires that food labels identify in plain English if the product contains any of the eight major food allergens: milk, eggs, fish, crustacean shellfish, peanuts, tree nuts, wheat and soybeans. For more information, see <a class="ext" href="http://www.fda.gov/Food/GuidanceRegulation/GuidanceDocumentsRegulatoryInformation/ucm059116.htm" id="anch_161" target="_blank">Guidance for Industry: Questions and Answers Regarding Food Allergens</a>.</p>
          </div> 
         </div> 
        </div> 
        <div class="views-row views-row-2 views-row-even"> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-title"> 
          <span class="field-content"><a id="faq-node-[nid]">What are the most common foods that people are allergic to? </a></span> 
         </div> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-body"> 
          <div class="field-content">
           <p>A consumer resource from the FDA, <a target="_blank">Food Allergies: What You Need to Know</a>, lists the foods that most commonly cause allergies as milk, eggs, fish, crab, lobster, shrimp, almonds and other tree nuts, and peanuts. Peanuts are one of the chief foods responsible for severe anaphylaxis.</p> 
           <p>Children will typically outgrow their allergies to milk, egg, soy and wheat, but will not typically outgrow their allergies to peanuts, tree nuts, fish and shrimp. In contrast, adults will not typically outgrow any of their allergies.</p>
          </div> 
         </div> 
        </div> 
        <div class="views-row views-row-3 views-row-odd"> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-title"> 
          <span class="field-content"><a id="faq-node-[nid]">What is the difference between a food allergy and a food intolerance? </a></span> 
         </div> 
         <div class="views-field views-field-body"> 
          <div class="field-content">
           <p>The NIAID provides<a href="https://www.niaid.nih.gov/diseases-conditions/food-allergy-characterizing">&nbsp;</a>a pamphlet:&nbsp;<a href="https://www.niaid.nih.gov/diseases-conditions/food-allergy-guidelines-patients">The&nbsp;<em>Guidelines for the Diagnosis and Management of Food Allergy in the United States: Summary for Patients, Families, and Cargivers</em></a>&nbsp;&nbsp;which discusses the difference between a food allergy and a food intolerance. In addition, it explains that individuals who suspect they have food allergies should have them identified by a healthcare provider because unidentified food allergies can potentially cause serious reactions.</p> 
           <p>For more information, see the resources at FNIC's <a href="https://www.nal.usda.gov/fnic/allergies-and-food-sensitivities" id="anch_167" target="_blank">Allergies and Food Sensitivities</a>.</p>
          </div>
