     <h4>U. S. Food and Drug Administration<br> Center for Food Safety and Applied Nutrition<br> June 2000</h4> 
     <p> </p>
     <div align="CENTER"> 
      <font size="+3"> <b>Guidance on How to Understand<br>and Use the Nutrition Facts Panel on Food Labels</b> </font> 
     </div> 
     <p>People look at food labels for different reasons. But whatever the reason, many consumers would like to know how to use this information more effectively and easily. The following guidance is intended to make it easier for you to use nutrition labels to make quick, informed food choices that contribute to a healthy diet.</p> 
     <p><b><font size="+2">The Nutrition Facts panel has two parts:</font></b><br> The main or top section (see #1-5 on the sample nutrition label below), which contains product-specific information (serving size, calories, and nutrient information) that varies with each food product; and the bottom part (see #6 on the sample nutrition label below), which contains a footnote. This footnote is only on larger packages and provides general dietary information about important nutrients.</p>

        <td valign="TOP"> (#1 on sample label):<br> The first place to start when you look at the Nutrition Facts panel is the serving size and the number of servings in the package. Serving sizes are provided in familiar units, such as cups or pieces, followed by the metric amount, e.g., the number of grams. Serving sizes are based on the amount of food people typically eat, which makes them realistic and easy to compare to similar foods.<p></p> </td>

     <p><b>Pay attention to the serving size, including how many servings there are in the food package, and compare it to how much YOU actually eat</b>.</p> 
     <p>The size of the serving on the food package influences all the nutrient amounts listed on the top part of the label. In the sample label above, one serving of macaroni and cheese equals one cup. If you ate the whole package, you would eat <u>two</u> cups. That doubles the calories and other nutrient numbers, including the %Daily Values as shown below (see Calories and %Daily Value for more information).</p>

     <p><b>Remember: You can not only use the food label to help limit those nutrients you want to cut back on, but also to increase those nutrients you want to consume in greater amounts.</b></p> 
     <p><b><u><font size="+2">The Percent Daily Value (%DV):</font></u></b><br> <img SRC="http://img.cooks.com/web/nutrition/images/fl_cooks18.gif" WIDTH="48" HEIGHT="278" ALT="%DV section of label." VSPACE="3" HSPACE="3" ALIGN="LEFT" BORDER="0"><br> </p>
     <p>This part of the Nutrition Facts panel tells you whether the nutrients (fat, sodium, fiber, etc) in a serving of food contribute a lot or a little to your total daily diet. By diet we mean all the different foods you eat in a day.</p> 
     <p><img SRC="http://img.cooks.com/web/nutrition/images/fl_cooks19.gif" WIDTH="347" HEIGHT="172" ALT="Footnote of label with header circled." VSPACE="3" HSPACE="3" ALIGN="RIGHT"><br> <b>%DVs are based on recommendations for a 2,000 calorie diet. </b>For labeling purposes, FDA set 2,000 calories as the reference amount for calculating %DVs. The %DV shows you the percent (or how much) of the recommended daily amount of a nutrient is in a serving of food. By using the %DV, you can tell if this amount is high or low. You, like most people, may not know how many calories you consume in a day. But you can still use the %DV as a frame of reference, whether or not you eat more or less than 2,000 calories each day.</p> 
     <p>It's not hard to follow nutrition experts' advice for a healthy diet. Try to limit your total daily intake of fat, saturated fat, sodium, and cholesterol (shown in yellow on the chart) to <b>less</b> than 100%DV.</p> 
     <p>Likewise, you should try to get enough essential nutrients like calcium, iron, and vitamins A and C as well as other components such as dietary fiber (shown in blue on the chart). Try to average 100% for each one of these nutrients each day.</p> 
     <p><b><font size="+2">%DVs are easy to use.</font></b><br> Do you need to know how to calculate percentages to follow this advice? No, the label (the %DV) does the math for you. It helps you interpret the numbers (grams and milligrams) by putting them all on the same scale (0-100%DV), much like a ruler. This way you can tell high from low and know which nutrients contribute a lot, or a little, to your daily recommended limit (upper or lower).</p> 
     <ul> 
      <p><img SRC="http://img.cooks.com/web/nutrition/images/fl_cooks20.gif" WIDTH="261" HEIGHT="116" VSPACE="3" HSPACE="3" ALIGN="RIGHT" ALT="Total Fat section of sample label with %DVs hidden."><br> <i>Example of %DV for Total Fat</i>: If you cover up the %DVs on the sample label, can you tell if 12g of Total Fat is high or low? Another way of asking this question is, does one serving (containing 12g of fat) contribute a lot or a little Total Fat to your daily diet? <br CLEAR="ALL"> </p>
      <p>&nbsp;</p> 
      <p><img SRC="http://img.cooks.com/web/nutrition/images/fl_cooks21.gif" WIDTH="262" HEIGHT="119" VSPACE="3" HSPACE="3" ALIGN="right" ALT="Total Fat section of sample label with %DVs shown and 18% circled."><br> Now look at the %DVs on the label example: 12g fat equals 18%DV. When one serving of macaroni and cheese contains 18%DV for Total Fat, that means you have 82% of your fat allowance left for all the other foods you eat that day (100%-18%=82%).</p> 
     </ul> 
     <br CLEAR="ALL"> 
     <p align="CENTER"><img SRC="http://img.cooks.com/web/nutrition/images/fl_cooks22.gif" WIDTH="452" HEIGHT="127" ALT="Illustration of previous sentence concerning % fat allowance."><br> <a name="see5"></a>&nbsp; <font size="+2"><u>Quick Guide to %DV</u></font><br><a href="#seeimage5"><img SRC="http://img.cooks.com/web/nutrition/images/fl_cooks18.gif" WIDTH="48" HEIGHT="278" alt="%DV section of label." VSPACE="3" HSPACE="3" ALIGN="LEFT" BORDER="0"></a> (#5 on sample label):<br><b> This general guide tells you that 5%DV or less is low and 20%DV or more is high.</b> This means that <b>5%DV or less is low</b> for all nutrients, those you want to limit (e.g., fat, saturated fat, cholesterol, and sodium), and those that you want to consume in greater amounts (fiber, calcium, etc). As the <b>Quick</b> <b>Guide </b>shows, <b>20%DV or more is high</b> for all nutrients.</p> 
     <ul> 
      <p><i>Example</i>: Look again at the amount of Total Fat in one serving listed on the sample nutrition label for macaroni and cheese. Is 18%DV contributing a lot or a little to your maximum fat limit of 100% DV? Check the <b>Quick Guide to %DV</b>. You see that 18%DV, which is below 20%DV, is not yet high, but what if you ate the whole package (two servings)? You would double that amount, eating 36% of your daily maximum for Total Fat. That amount, coming from just one food, would contribute a lot of fat to your daily diet. It would leave you 64% of your fat allowance (100%-36%=64%) for <i>all</i> of the other foods you eat that day, snacks and drinks included.</p> 
     </ul> 
     <p align="CENTER"><img SRC="http://img.cooks.com/web/nutrition/images/fl_cooks23.gif" WIDTH="452" HEIGHT="127" ALT="Illustration of previous sentence concerning % fat allowance."><br> <b><font size="+1">Comparisons:</font></b> The %DV also makes it easy for you to make comparisons. You can compare one product or brand to a similar product. It's easy to see which one is higher or lower in a nutrient because the serving sizes are generally consistent for similar types of foods. See <a href="#compare">comparison example #1</a>. </p>
     <p><b><font size="+1">Nutrient Content Claims:</font></b> You can quickly distinguish one claim from another, such as "reduced fat" vs. "light" or "nonfat." Just compare the %DVs for Total Fat in each food product to see which one is higher or lower in that nutrient--<b>there is no need to memorize definitions.</b> This works when comparing all nutrient content claims, e.g., less, light, low, free, more, high, etc. See <a href="#compare">comparison example #1 and #2</a></p> 
     <p><b><font size="+1">Dietary Trade-Offs:</font></b> You can <b>use the %DV to help you make dietary trade-offs</b> with other foods throughout the day. You don't have to give up a favorite food to eat a healthy diet. When a food you like is high in fat, balance it with foods that are low in fat at other times of the day. Also, pay attention to how much you eat so that the total amount of fat for the day stays below 100%DV.</p> 
     <br CLEAR="ALL"> 
     <p><img SRC="http://img.cooks.com/web/nutrition/images/fl_cooks24.gif" WIDTH="250" HEIGHT="362" VSPACE="3" HSPACE="3" ALIGN="RIGHT" ALT="Sample food label with sugars and protein circled."><br> <b><font size="+1">Sugars and Protein:</font></b> Note that neither Sugars nor Protein lists a %DV on the Nutrition Facts panel.<br><br> <b>Sugars</b>: No daily reference value has been established because no recommendations have been made for the total amount of sugars to eat in a day. Keep in mind, the sugars listed on the Nutrition Facts panel include naturally occurring sugars (like those in fruit and milk) as well as those added to a food or drink. Check the ingredient list for specifics on added sugars.<br><br> <b>Protein</b>: A %DV is required to be listed if a claim is made for protein, such as "high in protein". Otherwise, unless the food is meant for use by infants and children under 4 years old, none is needed. Current scientific evidence indicates that protein intake is not a public health concern for adults and children over 4 years of age.</p> 
     <br CLEAR="ALL"> 
     <p><b><font size="+1">Calcium:</font></b> Experts advise consumers to consume adequate amounts of calcium in their daily diet. This advice is given in milligrams (mg), but the Nutrition Facts panel only lists a %DV for calcium. For consumers to know how the calcium they consume relates to expert advice, they need to do some simple math. (This applies to calcium only).</p> 
     <p>&nbsp;</p> 
     <ul> 
      <p><i>Example</i>: Experts advise adolescents, especially girls, to consume 1,300mg and post-menopausal women 1,200mg of calcium daily. To find the %DV that corresponds with 1,300mg and 1,200mg, just divide the number of mg by 10. (The DV for calcium on food labels is 1,000mg). When converted to a percent, this gives a factor of 10. Thus, the daily target for teenage girls, 1,300mg , equals 130%DV, and the daily target for post menopausal women, 1,200mg, equals 120%DV. </p> 
      <br CLEAR="ALL"> 
      <p align="CENTER"><img SRC="http://img.cooks.com/web/nutrition/images/fl_cooks25.gif" WIDTH="515" HEIGHT="89" ALT="Illustration of above sentence."></p>
      <br> 
      <br CLEAR="ALL"> 
      <p>If you want to convert the %DV for calcium into milligrams, just multiply by 10. A container of yogurt might list 30%DV for calcium. To convert this to milligrams, multiply by 10, which equals 300mg of calcium for the yogurt.</p>

     <p><a name="see6"></a><font size="+2"><u>The Footnote, or lower part<br>of the Nutrition Facts Panel</u></font><br> <a href="#seeimage6"><img SRC="http://img.cooks.com/web/nutrition/images/fl_cooks27.gif" WIDTH="301" HEIGHT="158" ALT="Foootnote section of label, indicating values for 2000 and 2500 calorie diets. " VSPACE="3" HSPACE="3" ALIGN="LEFT" BORDER="0"></a> (#6 on sample label)<br> Note the * used after the heading "%Daily Value" on the Nutrition Facts panel. It refers to the Footnote in the lower part of the nutrition label, which tells you that "<b>%DVs are based on recommendations for a 2,000 calorie diet</b>". This statement must be on all food labels. But the remaining information in the full footnote may not be on the package if the size of the label is too small. When the full footnote does appear, it will always be the same. It doesn't change from product to product, because it shows dietary advice for all Americans--it is not about a specific food product.</p> 
     <p><b>The Daily Values</b> are based on expert dietary advice about how much, or how little, of some key nutrients you should eat each day, depending on whether you eat 2,000 or 2,500 calories a day.</p> 
     <ul> 
      <p><img SRC="http://img.cooks.com/web/nutrition/images/fl_cooks28.gif" WIDTH="307" HEIGHT="159" VSPACE="3" HSPACE="3" ALIGN="RIGHT" ALT="Footnote section of food label with total fat circled."> <i>Example</i>: look at the Total Fat information in the footnote. It tells you that if you eat a 2,000 calorie diet, you should eat <i>less than 65g of fat in <b>all</b> the foods</i> you eat in a day. By doing this, you will follow nutrition experts' advice to consume no more than 30 percent of your daily calories from fat. Because the DV for total fat is "less than 65g," this is the same thing as saying, to keep your total fat intake for the day below 100%DV.</p> 
      <br CLEAR="ALL"> 
      <p><img SRC="http://img.cooks.com/web/nutrition/images/fl_cooks29.gif" WIDTH="363" HEIGHT="161" VSPACE="3" HSPACE="3" ALIGN="RIGHT" ALT="Footnote section of food label with cholesterol and sodium circled."> If you consume 2,500 calories per day, the Footnote shows you how your daily values would change for some nutrients but not for others. The Daily Values for Cholesterol (300mg) and Sodium (2,400mg sodium) remain the same no matter how many calories you eat. But recommended levels of intake for other nutrients do depend on how many calories you consume.</p> 
      <br CLEAR="ALL"> 
     </ul> 
     <p><b>Remember: %DVs listed on the top half of the food label are based on recommendations for a 2,000 calorie diet, not a 2,500 calorie diet.</b> </p>
