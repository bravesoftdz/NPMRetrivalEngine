        <div></div> 
        <p><a href="https://whatscookingamerica.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/FoodLabel25.jpg"><img class=" size-full wp-image-150283 alignright" src="https://whatscookingamerica.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/FoodLabel25.jpg" alt="How to read food labels" width="432" height="307"></a></p> 
        <p>Article by&nbsp;<a href="https://whatscookingamerica.net/info.htm" target="_blank">Linda Stradley</a>&nbsp;of&nbsp;<a href="https://whatscookingamerica.net" target="_blank">What’s Cooking America</a>.</p> 
        <p>&nbsp;</p> 
        <p><strong><span style="color: #800000;">Health claims on food packages can be confusing, and it&nbsp;is&nbsp;sometimes hard to reconcile the information of the front of the package with what’s on the nutrition facts panel on the back of the package.</span></strong></p> 
        <p><strong><span style="color: #800000;">Have you ever been&nbsp;confused by the information on the labels on the food you buy to eat?</span></strong> &nbsp;Especially when you are trying to cut calories, fat, and carbohydrates.</p> 
        <p><strong><span style="color: #800000;">Do you read the labels on the food you buy for yourself and your family?</span></strong> &nbsp;If you don’t, it is time to educate yourself and start. &nbsp;What are those food labels really saying? &nbsp;A food label is like a recipe – It tells you what&nbsp;is&nbsp;in the food you eat. &nbsp;You do want to know what you&nbsp;are&nbsp;putting into your body? &nbsp;In order to plan a healthy diet, you must know how to read a food label.</p> 
        <p>This article will help you make quick, informed choices that that can help contribute to healthy eating through more informed label reading.</p> 
        <p>&nbsp;</p> 
        <hr> 
        <p>&nbsp;</p> 
        <div> 
         <p><a href="https://whatscookingamerica.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/NutritionLabel35.jpg"><img class=" size-full wp-image-150285 alignleft" src="https://whatscookingamerica.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/NutritionLabel35.jpg" alt="How to read food labels" width="213" height="463"></a><strong><span style="color: #800000;">When trying to figure out what the food you are thinking of buying actually contains, ignore the front of the package hype! &nbsp;It is just that – marketing hype. &nbsp;Manufacturers can not actually lie on labeling, but they can stretch the true when trying to get your attention to buy their product.</span></strong></p> 
         <p><strong><span style="color: #800000;">Every packaged food must include a list of ingredients. &nbsp;The ingredient in largest quantity is listed first, while the one in smallest quantity is listed last.</span></strong></p> 
         <p>&nbsp;</p> 
         <p><strong>Recommended serving size/Calories per serving.</strong> &nbsp;The first items, at the top of the label, you&nbsp;will&nbsp;notice are Serving Size and Servings Per Container. &nbsp;Serving Size is a standard measure of food. &nbsp;Servings Per Container represents the number of servings found in the food package. &nbsp;Serving size can be expressed in kitchen terms – cups, spoons, slices, ounces, and also in grams. &nbsp;Serving size tells how much food makes up a single serving. All data on the label is based on the serving size stated.</p> 
         <p><strong>Amount Per Serving</strong> – Shows the number of calories found in a single food serving. &nbsp;Multiply this number by the serving size and it should equal, or come close to, the total volume of the package.</p> 
         <p><span style="color: #800000;"><strong>Sample Food Label on left:</strong>&nbsp; Always remember that the calories listed are based on one (1) individual serving from the container. 4 servings x 280 calories = 1,230 total calories in the container/box/bottle on the sample food label.</span></p> 
         <p><strong>Calories from fat</strong> – Food labels show Calories from Fat so you can limit the amount of fat you eat for a healthier diet. The rule of thumb is that no more than 30% of your daily calories should come from fat. Higher fat foods should be eaten in smaller portions.</p> 
         <p><strong>% Daily Value</strong> -This section tells you what percentage of the total recommended daily amount of each nutrient (fats, carbs, proteins, major vitamins, and minerals) is in each serving, based on a 2,000 calorie per day diet.</p> 
         <p><strong>Total Fat</strong>– This equals the number of grams of fat per serving of the food. &nbsp;A heart-healthy diet limits foods containing saturated fats, trans fats, cholesterol, and sodium.</p> 
         <p><strong>Saturated Fat</strong> – A fat that is solid at room temperature and comes chiefly from animal food products and some plants. &nbsp;Some examples of saturated fat include foods such as beef, lamb, pork, lard, butter, cream, whole milk and high fat cheese. &nbsp;Plant sources include coconut oil, cocoa butter, palm oil and palm kernel oil. &nbsp;Saturated fat causes high LDL cholesterol levels — a risk for cardiovascular disease.</p> 
         <p><strong>Trans Fat</strong> – Also known as also known as trans fatty acid. &nbsp;Trans fat is a specific type of fat formed when liquid fats are made into solid fats by the addition of hydrogen atoms, in a process strangely enough known as hydrogenation. &nbsp;Hydrogenation solidifies liquid oils and increases the shelf life and the flavor stability of oils and foods that contain them. &nbsp;Trans fat is found in vegetable shortenings and in some margarines, crackers, cookies, snack foods and other foods. &nbsp;Small amounts of trans fats are found naturally in certain animal based foods. Trans fat is what is considered unhealthy fat.</p> 
         <p><strong>Cholesterol</strong> – This line tells you how many milligrams of cholesterol and what percent this is of the recommended daily value.</p> 
         <p><strong>Sodium/salt</strong> -The latest recommendation for sodium is less than 2,400 mg of sodium per day, or about a teaspoon of table salt.</p> 
         <p><strong>Total carbohydrates</strong> – Tells you how many grams of carbohydrates are in each serving and the percentage of the Daily Value this represents. &nbsp;This number includes starches, complex carbohydrates, dietary fiber, added sugar sweeteners, and non-digestible additives.</p> 
         <p><strong>Fiber</strong> – Fiber is an indigestible carbohydrate and aids in elimination. &nbsp;At least 15 grams of fiber per day is recommended.</p> 
         <p><strong>Protein</strong> – Many foods contain some protein but meat, fish, poultry and dairy foods are highest. &nbsp;Protein needs average between 50-100 grams per day.</p> 
         <p><strong>Percent Daily Values</strong> -This section gives some estimated nutrients per 2000 and 2500 calories.</p> 
         <p>&nbsp;</p> 
         <hr> 
         <p>&nbsp;</p> 
         <p><strong><span style="color: #800000;">The FDA also regulates the use of certain words or phrases on food packaging. &nbsp;Before May 8, 1994, a food product claiming to be “light” could just be light in color, texture or taste. &nbsp;But now such creative, and often misleading, labeling jargon is regulated. &nbsp;Terms used on the new food labels must adhere to the following FDA definitions:</span></strong></p> 
         <p>&nbsp;</p> 
         <p style="padding-left: 30px;"><strong>Light</strong>– Means that the food has half the fat, one-third the calories or half the salt of its regular counterpart. &nbsp;It can still be used to describe other properties like color or texture as long as the label makes the distinction clear (for example, “light brown sugar” or “light and fluffy”).</p> 
         <p style="padding-left: 30px;"><strong>Fat-Free or Sugar-Free</strong> – Indicates that none of the substance cited (or only a negligible amount) is in the product. &nbsp;For instance, a calorie-free product must have fewer than five calories per serving, while fat-free and cholesterol-free foods should have less than half a gram per serving. Related words, such as “without,” “no” and “zero,” must meet the same standards. &nbsp;For example, suppose a food is labeled 95 percent “fat-free.” This means that five percent of the total weight of the food is fat, (which may not seem like much), yet a single gram of fat contains nine calories compared to four calories in a gram of protein or carbohydrates.</p> 
         <p style="padding-left: 30px;"><strong>Fresh</strong> – Means unprocessed, uncooked, unfrozen (for example, fresh or freshly-squeezed orange juice). &nbsp;Washing and coating of fruits and vegetables are allowed. &nbsp;If a food has been quickly frozen, it can be described as fresh-frozen, which is commonly done with fresh fish.</p> 
         <p style="padding-left: 30px;"><strong>Healthy</strong> – Means the food may contain no more than 3 grams of fat (including one gram of saturated fat) and 60 milligrams of cholesterol per serving.</p> 
         <p style="padding-left: 30px;"><strong>High</strong> – As in high-fiber, means the product has 20 percent or more of the daily value for the nutrient cited.</p> 
         <p style="padding-left: 30px;"><strong>Good Source</strong> – Indicates that one serving of the food contains 10 to 19 percent of the daily value for a particular nutrient.</p> 
         <p style="padding-left: 30px;"><strong>Lean</strong> – Can be used to describe the fat content of meat, poultry and seafood. &nbsp;To be called “lean,” a serving of the product must have less than 10 grams of fat, 4.5 grams or less of saturated fat, and less than 95 milligrams of cholesterol. &nbsp;“Extra lean” has also been defined to mean less than 5 grams of fat, less than 2 grams of saturated fat and less than 95 milligrams of cholesterol.</p> 
         <p style="padding-left: 30px;"><strong>Less and fewer</strong> – Can be used to describe foods that have a nutrient or calorie content which is reduced by at least 25 percent.</p> 
         <p style="padding-left: 30px;"><strong>Low Fat</strong> – &nbsp;May be used on products that do not exceed the dietary guidelines for fat, saturated fat, cholesterol, sodium or calories. &nbsp;The criteria they must meet are:</p> 
         <p style="padding-left: 60px;"><span style="color: #339966;">Low-fat:</span> 3 grams or less per serving<br> <span style="color: #339966;">Low-saturated fat:</span> 1 gram or less per serving<br> <span style="color: #339966;">Low-sodium:</span> 140 milligrams or less per serving<br> <span style="color: #339966;">Low-cholesterol:</span> 20 milligrams or less of cholesterol and 2 grams or less of saturated fat per serving<br> <span style="color: #339966;">Low-calorie:</span> 40 calories or less per serving</p> 
         <p style="padding-left: 30px;"><strong>Natural flavors</strong>– The Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act defines “natural flavors” as: “the essential oil, oleoresin, essence or extractive, protein hydrolysate, distillate, or any product of roasting, heating, or enzymolysis, which contains a flavoring constituent derived from a spice, fruit, fruit juice, vegetable, vegetable juice, edible yeast, herb, bark, bud, root, leaf, or similar plant material; meat, seafood, poultry, eggs, dairy products, or fermentation products thereof whose significant function in food is flavoring rather than nutritional. &nbsp;This broad definition simply means that “natural flavors” are extracts from these non-synthetic foods.</p> 
         <p style="padding-left: 30px;"><strong>Reduced</strong> – Means that a nutritionally altered product contains at least 25 percent less of a nutrient or of calories than the regular product.</p> 
        </div> 
        <div> 
         <hr> 
         <p>&nbsp;</p> 
         <p><strong><span style="color: #800000;">Did You Know</span></strong> – In the nineteenth century, food labels barely identified what was inside a box or container. &nbsp;People who lived at that time just had to eat the food and hope for the best!</p> 
         <p>First, the <strong>Federal Food and Drug Act</strong> allowed the federal government to regulate the safety and quality of food. &nbsp;By 1924, the FDA no longer allowed untrue health claims and statements on food labels that might mislead people. &nbsp;After that, the net weight of the food produced and names and addresses of the food manufacturer or distributor had to be printed on labels as well. &nbsp;Ingredient lists also became common on labels.</p> 
        </div>
