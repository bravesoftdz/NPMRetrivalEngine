      <p><b>Calories</b> are <a href="/wiki/Unit_of_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Unit of energy">units of energy</a>. Various <a href="#Definitions">definitions</a> exist but fall into two broad categories. The first, the <b>small calorie</b>, or <b>gram calorie</b> (symbol: <b>cal</b>), is defined as the approximate amount of energy needed to raise the temperature of one <a href="/wiki/Gram" title="Gram">gram</a> of water by one degree <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">Celsius</a> at a pressure of one <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">atmosphere</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Webster_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Webster-1">[1]</a></sup> The second is the <b>large calorie</b> or <b>kilogram calorie</b> (symbol: <b>Cal</b>), also known as the <b>food calorie</b> and similar names,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> is defined in terms of the kilogram rather than the gram. It is equal to <span class="nowrap"><span style="display:none" class="sortkey">7003100000000000000?</span>1000</span> small calories or 1 <b>kilocalorie</b> (symbol: <b>kcal</b>).<sup id="cite_ref-Webster_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Webster-1">[1]</a></sup></p> 
      <p>Although these units relate to the <a href="/wiki/Metric_system" title="Metric system">metric system</a>, all of them have been considered obsolete in science since the adoption of the SI system.<sup id="cite_ref-Hist_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hist-3">[3]</a></sup> The unit of energy in the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">joule</a>. One small calorie is approximately 4.2 joules (so one large calorie is about 4.2 <a href="/wiki/Joule#Kilojoule" title="Joule">kilojoules</a>). The factor used to <a href="/wiki/Conversion_of_units_of_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Conversion of units of energy">convert</a> calories to joules at a given temperature is numerically equivalent to the <a href="/wiki/Specific_heat_capacity" class="mw-redirect" title="Specific heat capacity">specific heat capacity</a> of water expressed in joules per kelvin per gram or per kilogram. The precise conversion factor depends on the definition adopted.</p> 
      <p>In spite of its non-official status, the large calorie is still widely used as a unit of <a href="/wiki/Food_energy" title="Food energy">food energy</a>. The small calorie is also often used for measurements in <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, although the amounts involved are typically recorded in kilocalories.</p> 
      <p>The calorie was first defined by <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Cl%C3%A9ment" title="Nicolas Clément">Nicolas Clément</a> in 1824 as a unit of <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> energy<sup id="cite_ref-Hist_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hist-3">[3]</a></sup> and entered French and English dictionaries between 1841 and 1867. The word comes from <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a> <i><span lang="la" xml:lang="la">calor</span></i>, meaning 'heat'. The large calorie was introduced to the American public by <a href="/wiki/Wilbur_Olin_Atwater" title="Wilbur Olin Atwater">Wilbur Olin Atwater</a> in 1887.<sup id="cite_ref-Hist_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hist-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calorie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>The energy needed to increase the temperature of a given mass of water by 1&nbsp;°C depends on the atmospheric pressure and the starting temperature. Accordingly, several different precise definitions of the calorie have been used.</p> 
      <p>The pressure is usually taken to be the <a href="/wiki/Standard_atmospheric_pressure" class="mw-redirect" title="Standard atmospheric pressure">standard atmospheric pressure</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span style="display:none" class="sortkey">7005101325000000000?</span>101.325&nbsp;kPa</span>). The temperature increase can be expressed as one <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">kelvin</a>, which means the same as an increment of one degree Celsius.</p>

      <p>The two definitions most common in older literature appear to be the <i>15&nbsp;°C calorie</i> and the <i>thermochemical calorie</i>. Until 1948, the latter was defined as 4.1833 international joules; the current standard of 4.184&nbsp;J was chosen to have the new thermochemical calorie represent the same quantity of energy as before.<sup id="cite_ref-Rossini_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rossini-7">[6]</a></sup></p> 
      <p>The calorie was first defined specifically to measure energy in the form of heat, especially in experimental <a href="/wiki/Calorimetry" title="Calorimetry">calorimetry</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[9]</a></sup></p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutrition">Nutrition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calorie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nutrition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Main article: 
       <a href="/wiki/Food_energy" title="Food energy">Food energy</a>
      </div> 
      <p>In a nutritional context, the <a href="/wiki/Kilojoule" class="mw-redirect" title="Kilojoule">kilojoule</a> (kJ) is the <a href="/wiki/SI" class="mw-redirect" title="SI">SI</a> unit of food energy, although the kilocalorie is still in common use.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[10]</a></sup> The word <i>calorie</i> is popularly used with the number of kilocalories of nutritional energy measured. As if to avoid confusion, it is sometimes written <i>Calorie</i> (with a capital "C") in an attempt to make the distinction, although this is not widely understood. Capitalization contravenes the rule that the initial letter of a <a href="/wiki/Unit_name_(SI)" class="mw-redirect" title="Unit name (SI)">unit name</a> or <a href="/wiki/Derived_unit" class="mw-redirect" title="Derived unit">its derivative</a> shall be lower case in English.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[11]</a></sup></p> 
      <p>To facilitate comparison, <a href="/wiki/Specific_energy" title="Specific energy">specific energy</a> or energy density figures are often quoted as "calories per serving" or "kilocalories per 100&nbsp;g". A nutritional requirement or consumption is often expressed in calories per day. One gram of fat in food contains nine kilocalories, while a gram of either a <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrate</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Protein_(nutrient)" title="Protein (nutrient)">protein</a> contains approximately four kilocalories.<sup id="cite_ref-SciAm_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SciAm-14">[12]</a></sup> Alcohol in a food contains seven kilocalories per gram.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[13]</a></sup></p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calorie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>In other scientific contexts, the term <i>calorie</i> almost always refers to the small calorie. Even though it is not an SI unit, it is still used in chemistry. For example, the energy released in a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reaction</a> per <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mole</a> of reagent is occasionally expressed in <a href="/wiki/Kilocalorie_per_mole" title="Kilocalorie per mole">kilocalories per mole</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Zvi_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zvi-16">[14]</a></sup> Typically, this use was largely due to the ease with which it could be calculated in laboratory reactions, especially in <a href="/wiki/Aqueous_solution" title="Aqueous solution">aqueous solution</a>: a volume of reagent dissolved in water forming a solution, with concentration expressed in moles per liter (1 liter weighing 1&nbsp;kg), will induce a temperature change in degrees Celsius in the total volume of water solvent, and these quantities (volume, molar concentration and temperature change) can then be used to calculate energy per mole. It is also occasionally used to specify energy quantities that relate to reaction energy, such as <a href="/wiki/Bond_energy" title="Bond energy">enthalpy of formation</a> and the size of <a href="/wiki/Activation_energy" title="Activation energy">activation barriers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bhagavan_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bhagavan-17">[15]</a></sup> However, its use is being superseded by the SI unit, the joule, and multiples thereof such as the kilojoule.</p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement_of_calories_in_food">Measurement of calories in food</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calorie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Measurement of calories in food">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>In the past a <a href="/wiki/Calorimeter" title="Calorimeter">bomb calorimeter</a> was utilised to determine the energy content of food by burning a sample and measuring a temperature change in the surrounding water. Today this method is not commonly used in the USA and has been succeeded by calculating the energy content indirectly from adding up the energy provided by energy containing nutrients of food (such as protein, carbohydrates and fats). The fibre content is also subtracted to account for the fact fibre is not digested by the body.<sup id="cite_ref-SciAm_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SciAm-14">[12]</a></sup></p>
