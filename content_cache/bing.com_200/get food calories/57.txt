     <h1 id="wb-cont">
      <!-- WCMS:MANUAL-CHAPTER-TITLE BEGIN --> <small>Information within the Nutrition Facts Table</small> <br> Mandatory Information and Serving Size 
      <!-- WCMS:MANUAL-CHAPTER-TITLE END --></h1> 
     <!-- WMCS:GDR-NOTICE --> 
     <!-- WCMS:MANUAL-ARCHIVED --> 
     <!-- WCMS:MANUAL-UNDERREVIEW --> 
     <!-- WCMS:MANUAL-COMPLETE-CHAPTER-LINK --> 
     <!-- WCMS:MANUAL-TC --> 
     <!-- WCMS:MANUAL-CHAPTER-PAGE BEGIN --> 
     <section> 
      <!-- WCMS:MANUAL-INDEX BEGIN --> 
      <nav> 
       <ul class="list-unstyled col-md-offset-1 mrgn-tp-md mrgn-bttm-md"> 
        <!-- WCMS:MANUAL-TC-ITEM BEGIN --> 
        <li> <a href="#s1c1">Nutrition Facts Tables from Other Countries</a> 
         <!-- WCMS:MANUAL-TC-L2 --> </li> 
        <!-- WCMS:MANUAL-TC-ITEM END -->
        <!-- WCMS:MANUAL-TC-ITEM BEGIN --> 
        <li> <a href="#s2c1">Serving Size</a> 
         <!-- WCMS:MANUAL-TC-L2 --> </li> 
        <!-- WCMS:MANUAL-TC-ITEM END -->
        <!-- WCMS:MANUAL-TC-ITEM BEGIN --> 
        <li> <a href="#s3c1">Core Nutrition Information</a> 
         <!-- WCMS:MANUAL-TC-L2 --> </li> 
        <!-- WCMS:MANUAL-TC-ITEM END -->
        <!-- WCMS:MANUAL-TC-ITEM --> 
       </ul> 
      </nav> 
      <!-- WCMS:MANUAL-INDEX END --> 
      <p>The <i>Food and Drug Regulations</i> (FDR) states under section B.01.401 that the label of a pre-packaged product shall carry a Nutrition Facts table (NFT) that contains only the information as set out in the <abbr title="Food and Drug Regulations">FDR</abbr>. The <abbr title="Food and Drug Regulations">FDR</abbr> also sets out in the tables following sections B.01.401 and B.01.402 the manner in which energy and nutrient values must be declared in the Nutrition Facts Table.</p> 
      <figure> 
       <figcaption class="text-center">
        Figure 3.1 (Bilingual)
       </figcaption> 
       <img alt="This is an example of a Nutrition Facts table displaying the core mandatory information. Description Follows" class="img-responsive center-block" src="/DAM/DAM-food-aliments/STAGING/images-images/lble_industry_nutrition_label_table_information_img1_1388773949057_eng.gif"> 
       <details> 
        <summary class="mrgn-tp-lg mrgn-bttm-lg text-center">Description of Image - Figure 3.1 (Bilingual)</summary> 
        <p>General information for the content within the <abbr title="Nutrition Facts table">NFT</abbr>:</p> 
        <ul> 
         <li>a normal width font is to be used along with an 8 point type except as indicated</li> 
         <li>Thin rule lines (0.5 point) centred between text separate each row unless otherwise indicated.</li> 
        </ul> 
        <p>Going down the <abbr title="Nutrition Facts table">NFT</abbr> vertically from top to bottom, the contents are as follows:</p> 
        <ul> 
         <li>English title: Nutrition Facts, 13 point bold type</li> 
         <li>French title: <span lang="fr">Valeur nutritive</span>, 13 point bold type</li> 
         <li>Serving Size: per 125 <abbr title="millilitres">mL</abbr> (87 <abbr title="grams">g</abbr>) / <span lang="fr">par 125</span> <abbr title="millilitres">mL</abbr> (87 <abbr title="grams">g</abbr>)</li> 
         <li>2 point rule (horizontal line)</li> 
         <li>Left side title 'Amount' (with '<span lang="fr">teneur</span>' underneath) and right side title '% Daily Value' (with '% <span lang="fr">valeur quotidienne</span>' underneath) – 6 point bold type</li> 
         <li>1 point rule (horizontal line)</li> 
         <li>'Calories / <span lang="fr">Calories</span> 80' on left side</li> 
         <li>'Fat / <span lang="fr">Lipides</span> 0.5 <abbr title="grams">g</abbr>' on left side and '1%' on right side</li>

       <h2 id="s1c1">Nutrition Facts Tables from Other Countries</h2> 
       <!-- WCMS:MANUAL-H3-TITLE-LINK END --> 
       <p>Since the nutrition information requirements of other countries do not match the Canadian requirements, labels and advertisements with nutrition information other than that permitted by the <abbr title="Food and Drug Regulations">FDR</abbr> are considered to be labelled or advertised contrary to Canadian legislation. Therefore, only the Canadian <abbr title="Nutrition Facts Table">NFT</abbr> may be used to provide nutrition information in Canada, and nutrition labelling systems from other countries are not acceptable in Canada. Hence, the use of both the Canadian <abbr title="Nutrition Facts Table">NFT</abbr> and a nutrition information table from another country together is also not permitted (<abbr title="for example">e.g.</abbr> a food product with both the Canadian and American <abbr title="Nutrition Facts Table">NFT</abbr> is not allowed).</p> 
       <p>One objective of Canada's nutrition labelling regulations is to provide a standardized system for conveying information about the nutrient content of foods. Mandatory declarations, reference values and formats which differ from the one adopted by Canada make it difficult for consumers to compare foods at the point of purchase. These, therefore do not support an informed consumer choice for Canadians.</p> 
       <p><a href="/food/labelling/food-labelling-for-industry/nutrition-labelling/presentation/eng/1387664849974/1387664998059?chap=1#s1c1">Language</a> requirements are available in the <a href="/food/labelling/food-labelling-for-industry/nutrition-labelling/presentation/eng/1387664849974/1387664998059">Presentation of the Nutrition Facts Table</a> section.</p>

       <h2 id="s2c1">Serving Size</h2> 
       <!-- WCMS:MANUAL-H3-TITLE-LINK END --> 
       <p>The nutrient information presented in a Nutrition Facts table is based on a specific amount of food (edible portion). The amount is indicated under the Nutrition Facts heading using the phrase "Serving (naming the serving size)", "Serving Size (naming the serving size)" or "Per (naming the serving size)". The serving size is a quantity of food that can be reasonably consumed at a single eating occasion.</p> 
       <p>Serving sizes set out in the table under <a href="/food/labelling/food-labelling-for-industry/nutrition-labelling/information-within-the-nutrition-facts-table/eng/1389198568400/1389198597278?chap=5">Reference Amounts and Serving Sizes</a> are usually presented as a range. This allows manufacturers some flexibility when determining serving sizes for products of varying density and size, such as cookies or slices of bread. In order not to mislead consumers, the same serving size should be used whenever a serving size is mentioned on the label (<abbr title="for example">e.g.</abbr>, in the Nutrition Facts table, the directions for use, <abbr title="et cetera">etc.</abbr>). For example, if a box of pudding mix says that it makes 6 servings, the Nutrition Facts table should be based on one-sixth of the box and the directions for use should indicate how to make six servings.</p> 
       <h3 id="a1b1">General Requirements</h3> 
       <p>The serving size is based on the edible portion of the food as offered for sale [B.01.002A(1), <abbr title="Food and Drug Regulations">FDR</abbr>]. It must be expressed in the Nutrition Facts table as a consumer friendly measure (first) and in metric units (Second, in brackets, in the same units as the net quantity declaration).</p> 
       <h4 id="a1b1a">Consumer Friendly Measures</h4> 
       <p>For the purposes of serving sizes, the expression "consumer friendly measure" means:</p> 
       <ul> 
        <li class="mrgn-bttm-md">a fraction of food; <abbr title="for example">e.g.</abbr> foods usually divided into pieces before consumption, such as ? cake or ¼ pizza;</li> 
        <li class="mrgn-bttm-md">a common visual measure of food - <abbr title="for example">e.g.</abbr>, household measures such as cups, tablespoons, teaspoons, 250&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr>, 125&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr>, 15&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr>, 5&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr>, <abbr title="et cetera">etc.</abbr>;</li> 
        <li class="mrgn-bttm-md">a unit of food - <abbr title="for example">e.g.</abbr>, square of chocolate; slice of bread, 1 cookie, pat of butter, X&nbsp;<abbr title="millimetre">mm</abbr> slice, <abbr title="et cetera">etc.</abbr>;</li> 
        <li class="mrgn-bttm-md">a single serving, if it meets the criteria described in the section Single Serving Containers below [B.01.002A(2), <abbr title="Food and Drug Regulations">FDR</abbr>]; <abbr title="for example">e.g.</abbr> an entire container.</li> 
       </ul> 
       <h4 id="a1b1b">Metric Serving Sizes</h4> 
       <p>Most formats of the Nutrition Facts table require that the serving size be declared in both a consumer friendly measure and in a metric measure. The metric measure is declared in:</p> 
       <ul> 
        <li>Grams (g) when the net quantity of the product is declared by weight or by count, or</li> 
        <li>Millilitres (ml) when the net quantity of the product is declared by volume.</li> 
       </ul> 
       <p>There are three exceptions to this rule. Although the net quantity of olives, pickles and fruit used for garnish or flavour, such as maraschino cherries, is declared by volume, a serving must be expressed in grams [items 78, 149, and 150 in the <a href="/english/reg/jredirect2.shtml?drgr"><i>Food and Drug Regulations</i></a>, Schedule M].</p> 
       <p>Usually the units used for the serving size are the same as the units used for the reference amount (see Schedule M, <abbr title="Food and Drug Regulations">FDR</abbr>).</p> 
       <p>Metric values are rounded to the nearest 0.1 for quantities of less than 10 and to the nearest whole number for quantities of 10 or more. These rounding rules are found in the table to B.01.401, <abbr title="Food and Drug Regulations">FDR</abbr>, and the <a href="/food/labelling/food-labelling-for-industry/nutrition-labelling/information-within-the-nutrition-facts-table/eng/1389198568400/1389198597278?chap=1#a1c2">Core Nutrition Information Table</a>.</p> 
       <h4 id="a1b1c">Abbreviations for Units of Measure</h4> 
       <p>Certain abbreviations, which are considered bilingual, must always be used in the Nutrition Facts table for the corresponding unit of measure:</p> 
       <ul> 
        <li>mg (milligram)</li> 
        <li>g (gram)</li> 
        <li>ml <i>or</i> mL (millilitres)</li> 
       </ul> 
       <p>Other abbreviations are optional and should only be used when space constraints exist.</p> 
       <ul> 
        <li>tsp (teaspoon)</li> 
        <li>tbsp (tablespoon)</li> 
        <li><span lang="fr"><abbr title="cuillère">c.</abbr> à thé</span> <i>or</i> <span lang="fr">cuil. à thé (cuillère à thé</span>)</li> 
        <li><span lang="fr"><abbr title="cuillère">c.</abbr> à soupe</span> <i>or</i> <span lang="fr">cuil. à soupe (cuillère à soupe</span>)</li> 
       </ul> 
       <h4 id="a1b1d">When Consumer Friendly Measure = Metric Measure</h4> 
       <p>Certain metric measures are visibly measurable and are considered consumer friendly measures. For liquid foods, these declarations fulfill the requirement to declare a serving size in both a consumer friendly measure and a metric unit.</p> 
       <div class="pull-right mrgn-lft-md"> 
        <p><img alt="measuring cup and measuring spoons" class="img-responsive" src="/DAM/DAM-food-aliments/STAGING/images-images/lble_industry_nutrition_label_table_information_img2_1388774021355_eng.jpg"></p> 
       </div> 
       <p>The following metric measures are considered consumer friendly measures. You will note that these measurements provide for multiples of the metric teaspoon (5&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr>), metric tablespoon (15&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr>) and fractions and multiples of the metric cup (250&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr>).</p> 
       <ul> 
        <li>5&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr></li> 
        <li>10&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr></li> 
        <li>15&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr></li> 
        <li>25&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr></li> 
        <li>30&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr></li> 
        <li>45&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr></li> 
        <li>50&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr>&nbsp;–&nbsp;500&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr>, in increments of 25&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr></li> 
       </ul> 
       <p>Other non-usual metric volumes, such as 185&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr>, 240&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr>, 287&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr>, <abbr title="et cetera">etc.</abbr>, are not considered consumer friendly measures and may not be used alone to fulfill the serving size requirements. (Exceptions to this rule apply to single serving containers. See the section Single Serving Containers below for further clarification).</p> 
       <h3 id="a1b2">Determining a Reasonable Serving Size</h3> 
       <p>Although there is considerable variation in the manner of declaration of the serving size among the different Nutrition Facts table formats, there is always a requirement for the serving size to be declared for the product as sold. This serving is based on the edible portion of the food and is the amount of food that one adult would reasonably eat at one eating occasion. The manufacturer has some flexibility in determining serving sizes.</p> 
       <p>A list of reasonable serving sizes is available in the <a href="/food/labelling/food-labelling-for-industry/nutrition-labelling/information-within-the-nutrition-facts-table/eng/1389198568400/1389198597278?chap=5#a5a">Tables: Reference Amounts and Serving Sizes</a>, and it may be used as a reference tool and guide when evaluating the appropriateness of a serving size.</p> 
       <p>Manufacturers have the option of using serving sizes that differ from the suggestions in the table provided they are reasonable and not misleading. Note that there are very specific requirements for single serving containers. The manner in which the serving size is presented must be as per the <abbr title="Food and Drug Regulations">FDR</abbr>.</p> 
       <p><strong>Note:</strong> When the food is pre-portioned into units commonly consumed by a person, then the serving size should be the unit or a multiple of the unit&nbsp;–&nbsp;<abbr title="for example">e.g.</abbr>, 1 burger, 1 steak, 1 granola bar, 2 cookies, 2 slices of bread, <abbr title="et cetera">etc.</abbr> (not ? of a burger, 1 ½ cookies, 1 ½ slices of bread).</p> 
       <p>Additional guidance for determining a reasonable serving size on the following products or types of products is also provided:</p> 
       <ul> 
        <li>Products with a non-uniform shape</li> 
        <li>Deli meats</li> 
        <li>Spaghetti</li> 
        <li>Foods requiring preparation and for which the nutrition information is the same for the food "as sold" and "as prepared"</li> 
        <li>Food sold by count</li> 
        <li>Single serving containers</li> 
       </ul> 
       <h4 id="a1b2a">Products with a Non-uniform Shape</h4> 
       <p>Certain products have a non-uniform shape, such as roasts, hams or whole fish. For these products, consumer friendly measures such as an "x&nbsp;<abbr title="millimetre">mm</abbr> slice" or a fraction of product are not useful measures. In these cases, a single metric declaration in weight, <abbr title="that is to say">i.e.</abbr>, 100 grams, will suffice to fulfill the requirement to declare a serving size in both a consumer friendly measure and a metric unit.</p> 
       <div class="row"> 
        <div class="col-md-4"> 
         <p><img alt="fish" src="/DAM/DAM-food-aliments/STAGING/images-images/lble_industry_nutrition_label_table_information_img3_1388774099621_eng.jpg" class="center-block img-responsive"></p> 
        </div> 
        <div class="col-md-4"> 
         <p><img alt="ham" src="/DAM/DAM-food-aliments/STAGING/images-images/lble_industry_nutrition_label_table_information_img4_1388774131808_eng.jpg" class="center-block img-responsive"></p> 
        </div> 
        <div class="col-md-4"> 
         <p><img alt="roast" src="/DAM/DAM-food-aliments/STAGING/images-images/lble_industry_nutrition_label_table_information_img5_1388774177293_eng.jpg" class="center-block img-responsive"></p> 
        </div> 
       </div> 
       <p>For non-uniform products, such as those shown above, a simple weight declaration of 100&nbsp;<abbr title="grams">g</abbr> is acceptable as a serving size declaration.</p> 
       <h4 id="a1b2b">Deli Meats</h4> 
       <p>In the case of deli meats sold in <strong>chubs or industrial formats</strong> the serving size may be declared using a consumer friendly measure and a metric unit in the following manner "Per approximately 3 slices (55&nbsp;<abbr title="grams">g</abbr>)" or by declaring a single metric measure of "100&nbsp;<abbr title="grams">g</abbr>". If choosing the second option, only "100&nbsp;<abbr title="grams">g</abbr>" may be used; other metric values may not be used.</p> 
       <div class="pull-right mrgn-lft-md"> 
        <p><img alt="spaghetti" class="img-responsive" src="/DAM/DAM-food-aliments/STAGING/images-images/lble_industry_nutrition_label_table_information_img6_1388774208840_eng.jpg"></p> 
       </div> 
       <h4 id="a1b2c">Spaghetti</h4> 
       <p>In the case of long, dry noodles which are difficult to measure, the following options are possible:</p> 
       <ul> 
        <li>"per ¼ box (85&nbsp;<abbr title="grams">g</abbr>)", or</li> 
        <li>"per 85&nbsp;<abbr title="grams">g</abbr> (about 1 cup prepared al dente)"</li> 
       </ul> 
       <p><strong><i>Foods requiring preparation and for which the nutrition information is the same for the food "As Sold" and "As Prepared":</i></strong> In cases where the nutrient content of a food is not altered by the preparation of the food, the food may use the Standard, Horizontal or Linear format and declare the serving size of both the food as sold and the food as prepared, <abbr title="for example">e.g.</abbr>, drink crystals: "Per 1 <abbr title="tablespoon">tbsp</abbr> (2&nbsp;<abbr title="grams">g</abbr>) (about 1 cup prepared)". This provision is always optional. Examples: frozen concentrated orange juice or drink crystals that are prepared through the addition of water, some popping corn, <abbr title="et cetera">etc.</abbr></p> 
       <p><img alt="On the Nutrition Facts table, under the title Nutrition Facts, the serving size of both the food as sold and as prepared is declared for example Per 1 Tablespoon (2grams) and (about 1 cup prepared)." class="center-block img-responsive" src="/DAM/DAM-food-aliments/STAGING/images-images/lble_industry_nutrition_label_table_information_img7_1388774245700_eng.jpg"></p> 
       <p>In the case of the <a href="/food/labelling/food-labelling-for-industry/nutrition-labelling/nutrition-facts-table-formats/eng/1389209684841/1389210023155?chap=2#s2st3d">Dual Format&nbsp;–&nbsp;Foods Requiring Preparation</a>, the serving size is declared for the product as sold and as prepared. As well, in both the <a href="/food/labelling/food-labelling-for-industry/nutrition-labelling/nutrition-facts-table-formats/eng/1389209684841/1389210023155?chap=2#s2st3f">Dual Formats&nbsp;–&nbsp;Different Amounts of Food</a> and <a href="/food/labelling/food-labelling-for-industry/nutrition-labelling/nutrition-facts-table-formats/eng/1389209684841/1389210023155?chap=2#s2st3g">Aggregate Formats&nbsp;–&nbsp;Different Amounts of Food</a>, two serving sizes are provided.</p> 
       <h4 id="a1b2d">Food Sold by Count</h4> 
       <p>Under the <abbr title="Food and Drug Regulations">FDR</abbr>, item 1 of the table to B.01.401 indicates how the serving size of a food is to be declared in the Nutrition Facts table. For items sold by count, the number of units or a common household measure or fraction of the food would be shown first, followed by the weight, expressed in grams, in parentheses; <abbr title="for example">e.g.</abbr> 1 apple (150&nbsp;<abbr title="grams">g</abbr>) or 2 cups lettuce (120&nbsp;<abbr title="grams">g</abbr>) or ½ grapefruit (118&nbsp;<abbr title="grams">g</abbr>).</p> 
       <h3 id="a1b3">Single Serving Containers</h3> 
       <p>The entire net quantity in the package is considered to be the serving size in the following cases [B.01.002A(2), D.01.001(3), <abbr title="Food and Drug Regulations">FDR</abbr>]:</p> 
       <ul> 
        <li>The food packaged in the container could reasonably be eaten by one person at a single sitting. <p>For example, a 600&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr> bottle of juice dispensed from a vending machine is normally consumed during a single occasion. Such a bottle is considered a single serving, despite the fact that juice has a 250&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr> reference amount and a serving size range of 175&nbsp;–&nbsp;250&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml.</abbr></p> </li> 
        <li>The reference amount of the food is less than 100&nbsp;<abbr title="grams">g</abbr> or 100&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr> and the package contains less than 200&nbsp;% of that reference amount. <p>For example, consider a 55&nbsp;<abbr title="grams">g</abbr> bag of mixed nuts. The reference amount for mixed nuts is 50&nbsp;<abbr title="grams">g</abbr> . The package contains less than 200&nbsp;% of 50&nbsp;<abbr title="grams">g</abbr> (less than 100&nbsp;<abbr title="grams">g</abbr>) and therefore, the 55&nbsp;<abbr title="grams">g</abbr> bag is considered to be a single serving container, with a serving size of 55&nbsp;<abbr title="grams">g</abbr>.</p> </li> 
        <li>The reference amount is 100&nbsp;<abbr title="grams">g</abbr> or 100&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr> or more and the package contains 150% or less of that reference amount. <p>For example, consider a soft drink in a 500&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr> bottle. The reference amount for soft drinks is 355&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr>. Since the bottle 1 contains less than 150&nbsp;% of the 355&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr> (150&nbsp;% of 355 = 532.5&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr>) reference amount, the 500&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr> bottle is considered to be a single serving container, with a serving size of 500&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr>.</p> <p>The provision for a single serving container is not an optional requirement. Products that meet the requirements must provide nutrition information based on the net quantity of the entire package. For example, a 355&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr> can of soft drink must base its nutrition information on 355&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr> and may not declare a 250&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr> serving size.</p> </li> 
       </ul> 
       <p>The <a href="/english/reg/jredirect2.shtml?drgr"><i>Food and Drug Regulations</i></a>, Schedule M or the <a href="#a5a">Tables: Reference Amounts and Serving Sizes</a> provide reference amounts.</p> 
       <h4 id="a1b3a">Serving Size Declarations for Single Serving Containers</h4> 
       <p>The consumer friendly measure for a single serving container is the entire container. Consequently, the serving size for a single serving container should be declared in the following manner, "Per 1 container (75&nbsp;<abbr title="grams">g</abbr>)", "Per 1 can (355&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr>)", "Per 1 <span lang="fr">entrée</span> (240&nbsp;<abbr title="grams">g</abbr>)", "Per 1 drinking box (200&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr>)", "Per 1 pouch (56&nbsp;<abbr title="grams">g</abbr>)", <abbr title="et cetera">etc.</abbr> Although the declaration of both the consumer friendly measure and the metric measure is strongly encouraged, no exception is taken to the declaration of only the metric unit. For example, a 355&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr> can of soft drink may declare "Per 1 can (355&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr>)" or simply "Per 355&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr>".</p> 
       <p><a href="/food/labelling/food-labelling-for-industry/nutrition-labelling/information-within-the-nutrition-facts-table/eng/1389198568400/1389198597278?chap=7">Additional information (<abbr title="Questions and Answers">Q&amp;A</abbr>)</a> about specific serving sizes cases is available.</p> 
       <h3 id="a1b4">Serving Size Examples and Infractions</h3> 
       <p>Certain metric volumetric measures (<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr>) are visibly measurable and fulfil the requirements for both a consumer friendly measure and a metric measure for <i>liquid</i> foods.</p> 
       <p><img alt="Nutrition Facts table – the serving size Per 250 millilitres is visibly measurable and meets requirements for measuring liquids." class="center-block img-responsive" src="/DAM/DAM-food-aliments/STAGING/images-images/lble_industry_nutrition_label_table_information_img8_1388774294138_eng.jpg"></p> 
       <p>A single metric weight declaration is generally not an acceptable serving size. A consumer friendly measure (1<sup>st</sup>) and a metric measure (2<sup>nd</sup>, in brackets) are required for most foods. Exceptions exist for oddly shaped foods for which no consumer friendly measure exists and single serving containers of food.</p> 
       <p><img alt="Nutrition Facts table – The declaration serving size 100 grams is usually not an acceptable serving size declaration since a consumer-friendly measure and metric measure in brackets are required for most foods." class="center-block img-responsive" src="/DAM/DAM-food-aliments/STAGING/images-images/lble_industry_nutrition_label_table_information_img9_1388774391138_eng.jpg"></p> 
       <p>Note that the serving declarations have been reversed. The consumer friendly measure should be declared first followed by the metric measure in brackets.</p> 
       <p><img alt="Nutrition Facts table - declares Per 50 grams (about 27 chips); serving declarations have been reversed in this case." class="center-block img-responsive" src="/DAM/DAM-food-aliments/STAGING/images-images/lble_industry_nutrition_label_table_information_img10_1388774458842_eng.jpg"></p> 
       <p>In this example the serving size is declared as "52&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr> (20&nbsp;<abbr title="grams">g</abbr>)". When metric units of volume (<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr>) are used as a consumer friendly measure, they should be rounded to the nearest 25&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr>. A more appropriate serving size declaration would be "Per 50&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr> (20&nbsp;<abbr title="grams">g</abbr>)".</p> 
       <p><img alt="Nutrition facts table - when metric units of volume are used as a consumer friendly measure, they should be rounded to the nearest 25 millitres." class="center-block img-responsive" src="/DAM/DAM-food-aliments/STAGING/images-images/lble_industry_nutrition_label_table_information_img11_1388774498701_eng.jpg"></p> 
       <p>On this 100&nbsp;<abbr title="grams">g</abbr> package, the serving size is declared as "130&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr> (50&nbsp;<abbr title="grams">g</abbr>)". 130&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr> is not a standardized measure. The consumer friendly measure should be rounded and the serving size declared as "Per 125&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr> (50&nbsp;<abbr title="grams">g</abbr>)" or "Per ½ package (50<abbr title="grams">g</abbr>)".</p> 
       <p><img alt="Nutrition facts table - the serving size is not a standardized measure." class="center-block img-responsive" src="/DAM/DAM-food-aliments/STAGING/images-images/lble_industry_nutrition_label_table_information_img12_1388774545905_eng.jpg"></p> 
       <p>This 355&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr> can of soft drink declares a 250&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">mL</abbr> serving. However, this product is obviously a single serving, <abbr title="that is to say">i.e.</abbr>, it is usually consumed at a single eating occasion and meets the requirements of a single serving container. Therefore, the information must be provided for the entire product, not a portion of the product, <abbr title="that is to say">i.e.</abbr>, Serving Size: "Per 1 can (355&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr>)" or "Per 355&nbsp;<abbr title="millilitres">ml</abbr>".</p>
