             <h3 xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" id="sites-page-title-header" style="" align="left"> <span id="sites-page-title" dir="ltr" tabindex="-1" style="outline: none">ApplicationId versus PackageName</span> </h3> 
             <div id="sites-canvas-main" class="sites-canvas-main"> 
              <div id="sites-canvas-main-content"> 
               <table xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" cellspacing="0" class="sites-layout-name-one-column sites-layout-hbox">

                   <div dir="ltr">
                    <div>
                     <b>This page is obsolete. Redirecting to&nbsp;</b>
                     <span style="background-color:transparent"><b><a href="https://developer.android.com/studio/build/application-id.html">https://developer.android.com/studio/build/application-id.html</a></b></span>
                    </div>
                    <div>
                     <span style="background-color:transparent">
                      <div class="sites-embed-align-left-wrapping-off">
                       <div class="sites-embed-border-off sites-embed sites-embed-full-width" style="width:100%;">

                    </div>
                    <div>

                    </div>
                    <div>
                     <br>
                    </div>
                    <div>
                     <br>
                    </div>
                    <div>
                     <br>
                    </div>
                    <div>
                     <br>
                    </div>
                    <div>
                     <br>
                    </div>
                    <div>
                     <br>
                    </div>
                    <div>
                     <br>
                    </div>All Android apps have a package name. The package name uniquely identifies the app on the device; it is also unique in the Google Play store. This means that once you have published an app with this package name, 
                    <b>you can never change it</b>; doing so would cause your app to be treated as a brand new app, and existing users of your app will not see the newly packaged app as an update.
                    <div>
                     <br>
                    </div>
                    <div>
                     Prior to the Android Gradle build system, the package name for your app was determined by the package attribute at the root element of your manifest file:
                    </div>
                    <div>
                     <div>
                      <br>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      AndroidManifest.xml:
                     </div>
                     <div></div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="sites-codeblock sites-codesnippet-block">
                     <div>
                      <div>
                       <code>&lt;?xml version="1.0" encoding="utf-8"?&gt;</code>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <code>&lt;manifest xmlns:android="http://schemas.android.com/apk/res/android"</code>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <code>&nbsp; &nbsp; </code>
                       <b><code>package="com.example.my.app"</code></b>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <code>&nbsp; &nbsp; android:versionCode="1"</code>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <code>&nbsp; &nbsp; android:versionName="1.0" &gt;</code>
                      </div>
                     </div>
                     <div></div>
                    </div>
                    <div>
                     <br>
                    </div>
                    <div>
                     However, the package defined here also serves a secondary purpose: it is used to name the package for your R resource class (as well as to resolve any relative class names to Activities). In the above example, the generated R class will be 
                     <code>com.example.my.app.R</code>, so if you have code in other packages that need to reference resources, it needs to import&nbsp;
                     <span style="line-height:21.3333320617676px;font-size:10pt;background-color:transparent">com.example.my.app.R</span>
                     <span style="line-height:1.6;font-size:10pt;background-color:transparent">.</span>
                    </div>
                    <div>
                     <br>
                    </div>With the new Android Gradle build system, you can easily build multiple different 
                    <b>versions</b>&nbsp;of your app; for example, you can build both a "free" version and a "pro" version of your app (using flavors), and these should have different packages in the Google Play store such that they can be installed and purchased separately, both installed at the same time, and so on. Similarly, you may also build both "debug" and "alpha" and "beta" versions of your app (using build types) and these can also similarly contribute to unique package names.
                    <div>
                     <br>
                    </div>
                    <div>
                     At the same time, the R class you are importing in your code must stay the same at all time; your .java source files should not have to change when you are building the different versions of your app.
                    </div>
                    <div>
                     <br>
                    </div>
                    <div>
                     Therefore, we have 
                     <b>decoupled</b>&nbsp;the two usages of package name:
                    </div>
                    <div>
                     <ul>
                      <li>The final package that is used in your built .apk's manifest, and is the package your app is known as on your device and in the Google Play store, is the "application id".</li>
                      <li>The package that is used in your source code to refer to your R class, and to resolve any relative activity/service registrations, continues to be called the "package".</li>
                     </ul>
                     <div>
                      You can specify the application id in your gradle file as follows:
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <br>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      app/build.gradle:
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <div></div>
                     </div>
                     <div class="sites-codeblock sites-codesnippet-block">
                      <div>
                       <div>
                        <code>apply plugin: 'com.android.application'</code>
                       </div>
                       <div>
                        <br>
                       </div>
                       <div>
                        <code>android {</code>
                       </div>
                       <div>
                        <code>&nbsp; &nbsp; compileSdkVersion 19</code>
                       </div>
                       <div>
                        <code>&nbsp; &nbsp; buildToolsVersion "19.1"</code>
                       </div>
                       <div>
                        <br>
                       </div>
                       <div>
                        <code>&nbsp; &nbsp; defaultConfig {</code>
                       </div>
                       <div>
                        <code>&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; </code>
                        <b><code>applicationId "com.example.my.app"</code></b>
                       </div>
                       <div>
                        <code>&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; minSdkVersion 15</code>
                       </div>
                       <div>
                        <code>&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; targetSdkVersion 19</code>
                       </div>
                       <div>
                        <code>&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; versionCode 1</code>
                       </div>
                       <div>
                        <code>&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; versionName "1.0"</code>
                       </div>
                       <div>
                        <code>&nbsp; &nbsp; }</code>
                       </div>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <code>&nbsp; &nbsp; ...</code>
                      </div>
                      <div></div>
                     </div>
                     <br>
                     <div>
                      As before, you need to specify the package used for your code in the Manifest file, just as shown in the above AndroidManifest.xml sample.
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <br>
                     </div>
                     <div>
                      <b>Here comes the critical part:&nbsp;</b>
                      <span style="line-height:1.6;font-size:10pt;background-color:transparent">When you've done the above, the two packages are independent. You are completely free to refactor your code - changing the internal package used for your activities and services, updating your Manifest package, and refactoring your import statements. This will have no bearing on the final id of your application, which is now always going to be the applicationId specified in the Gradle file.</span>
                     </div>
                     <div>
