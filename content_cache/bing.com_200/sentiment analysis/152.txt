           <p name="6a1f" id="6a1f" class="graf graf--p graf-after--figure"><strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Introduction?—?“Houston, we have a problem”</strong></p>
           <p name="a24b" id="a24b" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">We at Lab41 have recently become very interested in deep learning?—?specifically as applied to natural language processing (NLP).            <p name="61aa" id="61aa" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">For our first approach, we chose to start with a classic?—?Naive Bayes. This algorithm has been extensively used for spam filtering and other document classification tasks. It is fairly easy to implement and continues to demonstrate surprisingly good accuracy scores on a wide range of tasks. Without much data preprocessing, Naive Bayes can get around 60–70% accuracy on many tasks. In short, what does this algorithm do? Below is a formula for Bayes’ rule, which underpins Naive Bayes (mathphobes fear not, I will explain more below):</p>
           <figure name="1638" id="1638" class="graf graf--figure graf-after--p">
            <div class="aspectRatioPlaceholder is-locked" style="max-width: 298px; max-height: 83px;">

            </div>

           <p name="7d98" id="7d98" class="graf graf--p graf-after--figure">This formula can be read as saying that the probability of some class <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">c</em> given feature <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">f0</em> is equal to the probability of feature <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">f0</em> given <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">c</em> times the probability of class <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">c</em> divided by the prior probability of the feature <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">f0</em>. A good practical example of this would be something like the following:</p>
           <figure name="a5e9" id="a5e9" class="graf graf--figure graf-after--p">
            <div class="aspectRatioPlaceholder is-locked" style="max-width: 700px; max-height: 50px;">

            </div>
           </figure>
           <p name="af67" id="af67" class="graf graf--p graf-after--figure">This says that the probability that it is raining given that you see clouds in the sky is equal to the probability that it would be cloudy if it was raining (probably high), times the probability of it raining at all, clouds or no, divided by the probability that it is cloudy at any given moment. This gives a probability (of rain) conditioned on one feature (i being cloudy), but for real applications, we don’t care about just one feature! If we are thinking the context of the above example, some other indicators of rain might be whether it rained yesterday, the humidity, and many other things. For the purposes of our challenge though, every tweet has many indicators of sentiment. Each word potentially provides information, but there are also emoticons, hashtags, punctuation and more. The inputs to this model, <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">x1</em>, <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">x2</em>,&nbsp;…&nbsp;, <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">xv</em>, are just the words. The outputs, <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">y1</em>, <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">y2</em>,&nbsp;…&nbsp;, <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">yv</em>, also represent these words. Confused? Let me explain…<br>&nbsp;<br>&nbsp;The general logic behind word2vec is best explained by this maxim from the early days of corpus linguistics:<br>&nbsp;<br>&nbsp;<em class="markup--em markup--p-em">You shall know a word by the company it keeps (Firth, J. R. 1957:11)</em><br>&nbsp;<br>&nbsp;Essentially, word2vec is two algorithms?—?Continuous Bag Of Words (CBOW) and Skip-Gram. Given some window of words in a document (also know as the <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">context window</em>, or <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">word window</em>), the CBOW attempts to predict the center word in the window given the words surrounding it. The Skip-Gram model is the inverse of that operation?—?it tries to predict the surrounding words given the center word. These are visualized below for a single word with a window size of 5.</p>

These methods are how the neural net learns the weights matrices <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">W</em> and <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">W’</em> from the original picture. It is from these matrices that we get the mysterious, but effective, “word vectors”.</p>

           <p name="cf66" id="cf66" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">More specifically, given some training words <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">w1</em>, <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">w2</em>,&nbsp;…&nbsp;, <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">wv</em>, the skip-gram model tries to maximize the average log probability</p>

Skip-gram defines <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">p(wt+j | wj)</em> using the softmax function:</p>

           <p name="5a18" id="5a18" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">—?—?—?—?End Math Geek Break?—?—?—?—?—?-</p>
           <p name="a40c" id="a40c" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">There are some good implementations available on the web for this algorithm. Google has a very efficient C implementation <a href="http://word2vec.googlecode.com/svn/trunk/" data-href="http://word2vec.googlecode.com/svn/trunk/" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" rel="noopener" target="_blank">here</a>. Gensim has also implemented the algorithm in python. This is the package that we used in our implementation.</p>
           <p name="d8b4" id="d8b4" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p"><strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong"><em class="markup--em markup--p-em">So now we have word vectors&nbsp;… what do I do with them?</em></strong></p>
           <p name="8cfc" id="8cfc" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Well one thing that we would want to know is, how good are these vectors? What type of topics are they good at representing? One way researchers have used to quantify the effectiveness of trained vectors is to test them on a set of SAT style analogies. That is, given a relationship between words A and B, can the model correctly identify the relationship between C and D?</p>
           <p name="8b44" id="8b44" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">To do this, we can compare the similarity of vectors between words in the model. A good example of this in action is the picture above of the relationships between countries and their capitals. Google provides a set of over 20,000 of these types of analogies across a range of semantic and syntactic topics. Two cherry-picked examples are below:</p>
           <p name="7761" id="7761" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p"><em class="markup--em markup--p-em">Athens Greece Baghdad Iraq<br>&nbsp;decrease decreases find finds</em></p>
           <p name="3650" id="3650" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">We would want the vector between Athens and Greece to be similar to the vector between Baghdad and Iraq, to reflect the fact that both are capital-nation relationships. Luckily for us, gensim’s implementation of word2vec comes with a built in function to test the accuracy of our models against these analogies. When we did this for one of our models, these were our accuracy scores across different topics.</p>

Whether or not these particular vectors are valuable will be determined empirically when we use them for our sentiment analysis task. This output does, however, give us a general idea of what our data looks like.</p>
           <p name="e2ae" id="e2ae" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p"><strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong"><em class="markup--em markup--p-em">Great&nbsp;… but how do you represent sentences?</em></strong></p>
           <p name="9384" id="9384" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">The gensim package comes with two word vector models?—?word2vec and doc2vec. With doc2vec, we can additionally represent sentences as vectors. To do this, gensim implements the algorithm from Mikolov 2013 paper <a href="http://arxiv.org/pdf/1405.4053v2.pdf" data-href="http://arxiv.org/pdf/1405.4053v2.pdf" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">Distributed Representations of Sentences and Documents</strong></a>. It allows you to use different flags to specify vector averaging or vector concatenation when representing different sentences. Below is a picture taken from that paper representing this process:</p>

To do this, we need to convert the the word vectors and their sentiment labels into numpy arrays.            <p name="910b" id="910b" class="graf graf--p graf-after--figure">So, 70% accuracy…. not great! Why could this be? Other papers are showing word2vec algorithms yield very high accuracy scores when used for classification. What are we doing differently? Firstly, lets think about our data. We used exclusively tweets to train the model. One problem is that there is just not that much consecutive semantic/syntactic content in a single post. 140 characters isn’t a lot. Additionally, in the example above, all words in the dataset were included in the word2vec vocabulary. It seems logical that removing words with low frequencies would also help to remove noise from the model. In the future, we are looking to scale up to larger datasets such as the Amazon Product Review data set, which has longer text inputs, as well as move to our significantly larger in-house tweet dataset on the local Spark cluster and compare our results.</p>
           <p name="2554" id="2554" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p"><strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">A Deep Future?—?“Roads? Where we’re going, we don’t need roads.” (<em class="markup--em markup--p-em">Back to the Future</em>)</strong></p>
           <p name="807d" id="807d" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">So, what’s next? There is work yet to be done on baseline NLP methods for sentiment analysis! We plan on using different classifiers and parameters to stack on top of the word2vec model. Some things we have considered include random forests and SVMs. Hopefully we can gradually improve our accuracy score across datasets. Additionally, <a href="http://www-nlp.stanford.edu/pubs/glove.pdf" data-href="http://www-nlp.stanford.edu/pubs/glove.pdf" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" rel="noopener" target="_blank">GloVe (Global Vectors for Word Representation)</a>, an algorithm created by Richard Socher and colleagues from Stanford, is another promising tool. While somewhat similar to word2vec, Socher has claimed that his methods perform better on analogy tasks.</p>
           <p name="a5cf" id="a5cf" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Another avenue of exploration will be other preprocessing methods. Currently, when feeding the word2vec model training data, we are simply tokenizing the tweets. The logical next step would be to remove highly frequent, less informative words like <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">is</em>, <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">the</em>, <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">as</em>, etc. from documents. I think these words add unnecessary noise to our datasets. Including them increases the training time for our models as well as dilutes the information converyed by connections between words.</p>
           <p name="506d" id="506d" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">Further, a practice I think we should investigate is the replacement of “@ mentions”?—?where another Twitter user is called out by their Twitter handle in the body of a post. My intuition is that, for the purpose of sentiment analysis, these tokens add noise to the data. Hashtags, on the other hand are an interesting case. Some of them, like #sunshine, may connote positive sentiment, while something more like #tomorrow is likely to be more ambiguous. Hashtags also rise and fall in popularity, making the data somewhat temporal. It will be interesting to see whether including or excluding them from a word2vec model affects accuracy. As the popularity of word vectors continues to grow, they are also being used increasingly as inputs to a deep learning model. As <a href="http://www.lab41.org/" data-href="http://www.lab41.org/" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">the lab</strong></a> continues to expand into the deep learning space, we are hoping replicate some of these methods.</p>
           <p name="9261" id="9261" class="graf graf--p graf-after--p">To conclude, while it still appears that deep learning may have the edge when it comes to sentiment analysis task, it is important to consider feasibility. Deep Learning isn’t easy, and neither is hiring a pack of PhD’s students to design, build, and test different architectures. Is it more practical for organizations to just continue refining established traditional NLP methods? As part of the labs ongoing deep learning exploration, these are the considerations that we hope to shed light on. All of our work is open source, so if you are interested in following our project, please checkout our <a href="https://github.com/Lab41/sunny-side-up" data-href="https://github.com/Lab41/sunny-side-up" class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><strong class="markup--strong markup--p-strong">repo on GitHub</strong></a>.</p>
