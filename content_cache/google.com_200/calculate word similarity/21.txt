  <meta name="dc.description" content="Alignment-free methods are increasingly used to calculate evolutionary distances between DNA and protein sequences as a basis of phylogeny reconstruction. Most of these methods, however, use heuristic distance functions that are not based on any explicit model of molecular evolution. Herein, we propose a simple estimator d
                
                  N
                 of the evolutionary distance between two DNA sequences that is calculated from the number N of (spaced) word matches between them. We show that this distance function is more accurate than other distance measures that are used by alignment-free methods. In addition, we calculate the variance of the normalized number N of (spaced) word matches. We show that the variance of N is smaller for spaced words than for contiguous words, and that the variance is further reduced if our spaced-words approach is used with multiple patterns of ‘match positions’ and ‘don’t care positions’. Our software is available online and as downloadable source code at: 
                  http://spaced.gobics.de/
                  
                .">

  <meta name="citation_abstract" content="<h2>Abstract</h2>Alignment-free methods are increasingly used to calculate evolutionary distances between DNA and protein sequences as a basis of phylogeny reconstruction. Most of these methods, however, use heuristic distance functions that are not based on any explicit model of molecular evolution. Herein, we propose a simple estimator <i>d</i> <sub> <i>N</i> </sub> of the evolutionary distance between two DNA sequences that is calculated from the number <i>N</i> of (spaced) word matches between them. We show that this distance function is more accurate than other distance measures that are used by alignment-free methods. In addition, we calculate the variance of the normalized number <i>N</i> of (spaced) word matches. We show that the variance of <i>N</i> is smaller for spaced words than for contiguous words, and that the variance is further reduced if our spaced-words approach is used with multiple patterns of ‘match positions’ and ‘don’t care positions’. Our software is available online and as downloadable source code at: <a href=&quot;http://spaced.gobics.de/&quot;>http://spaced.gobics.de/</a>.">

          <h2 class="Heading js-ToggleCollapseSection">Abstract</h2>
          <div class="js-CollapseSection">
           <p xmlns:func="http://oscar.fig.bmc.com" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="Para">Alignment-free methods are increasingly used to calculate evolutionary distances between DNA and protein sequences as a basis of phylogeny reconstruction. Most of these methods, however, use heuristic distance functions that are not based on any explicit model of molecular evolution. Herein, we propose a simple estimator <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em> <sub> <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> </sub> of the evolutionary distance between two DNA sequences that is calculated from the number <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> of (spaced) word matches between them. We show that this distance function is more accurate than other distance measures that are used by alignment-free methods. In addition, we calculate the variance of the normalized number <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> of (spaced) word matches. We show that the variance of <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> is smaller for spaced words than for contiguous words, and that the variance is further reduced if our spaced-words approach is used with multiple patterns of ‘match positions’ and ‘don’t care positions’. Our software is available online and as downloadable source code at: <span class="ExternalRef"><a href="http://spaced.gobics.de/"><span class="RefSource">http://spaced.gobics.de/</span></a></span>.</p>
          </div>

         <section xmlns="" xmlns:fn="http://www.w3.org/2005/xpath-functions" xmlns:meta="http://www.springer.com/app/meta" id="Sec1" class="Section1 RenderAsSection1">
          <h2 class="Heading js-ToggleCollapseSection">Background</h2>
          <div class="js-CollapseSection">
           <p xmlns:func="http://oscar.fig.bmc.com" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="Para">Alignment-free methods are increasingly used for DNA and protein sequence comparison since they are much faster than traditional alignment-based approaches [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR1" data-toggle="citation">1</a></span>]. Applications of alignment-free approaches include <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">protein classification</em> [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR2" data-toggle="citation">2</a></span>-<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR5" data-toggle="citation">5</a></span>], <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">read alignment</em> [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR6" data-toggle="citation">6</a></span>-<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR8" data-toggle="citation">8</a></span>], <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">isoform quantification</em> from RNAseq reads [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR9" data-toggle="citation">9</a></span>], <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">sequence assembly</em> [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR10" data-toggle="citation">10</a></span>], <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">read-binning</em> in metagenomics [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR11" data-toggle="citation">11</a></span>-<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR16" data-toggle="citation">16</a></span>] or analysis of <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">regulatory elements</em> [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR17" data-toggle="citation">17</a></span>-<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR20" data-toggle="citation">20</a></span>]. Most alignment-free algorithms are based on the <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">word</em> or <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">k-mer composition</em> of the sequences under study [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR21" data-toggle="citation">21</a></span>]. To measure pairwise distances between genomic or protein sequences, it is common practice to apply standard metrics such as the <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">Euclidean</em> or the <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">Jensen-Shannon (JS)</em> distance [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR22" data-toggle="citation">22</a></span>] to the relative word frequency vectors of the sequences.</p>
           <p xmlns:func="http://oscar.fig.bmc.com" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="Para">Recently, we proposed an alternative approach to alignment-free sequence comparison. Instead of considering <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">contiguous</em> subwords of the input sequences, our approach considers <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">spaced words, i.e.</em> words containing <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">wildcard</em> or <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">don’t care</em> characters at positions defined by a pre-defined <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">pattern</em> <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">P</em>. This is similar as in the <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">spaced-seeds</em> approach that is used in database searching [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR23" data-toggle="citation">23</a></span>]. As in existing alignment-free methods, we compared the (relative) frequencies of these spaced words using standard distance measures [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR24" data-toggle="citation">24</a></span>]. In [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR25" data-toggle="citation">25</a></span>], we extended this approach by using whole sets <span class="InlineEquation" id="IEq1">\(\mathcal {P} = \{P_{1},\dots,P_{m}\}\)</span> of patterns and calculating the spaced-word frequencies with respect to <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">all</em> patterns in <a target="_blank" href="http://media.springernature.com/full/springer-static/image/art%3A10.1186%2Fs13015-015-0032-x/MediaObjects/13015_2015_32_Figa_HTML.gif"><img src="http://media.springernature.com/lw785/springer-static/image/art%3A10.1186%2Fs13015-015-0032-x/MediaObjects/13015_2015_32_Figa_HTML.gif"></a> . In this <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">multiple-pattern</em> approach, the distance between two sequences is defined as the <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">average</em> of the distances based on the <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">individual</em> patterns <span class="InlineEquation" id="IEq2">\(P_{i}\in {\mathcal {P}}\)</span>, see also [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR26" data-toggle="citation">26</a></span>]. ‘Spaced words’ have been proposed simultaneously by Onodera and Shibuya for protein classification [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR27" data-toggle="citation">27</a></span>] and by Ghandi <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">et al.</em> to study regulatory elements [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR28" data-toggle="citation">28</a></span>,<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR29" data-toggle="citation">29</a></span>].</p>
           <p xmlns:func="http://oscar.fig.bmc.com" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="Para"> <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">Phylogeny reconstruction</em> is an important application of alignment-free sequence comparison. Consequently, most alignment-free methods were benchmarked by applying them to phylogeny problems [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR30" data-toggle="citation">30</a></span>-<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR35" data-toggle="citation">35</a></span>]. The distance metrics used by these methods, however, are only rough measures of dissimilarity, not derived from any explicit model of molecular evolution. This may be one reason why distances calculated by alignment-free algorithms are usually not directly evaluated, but are used as input for distance-based phylogeny methods such as <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">Neighbour-Joining</em> [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR36" data-toggle="citation">36</a></span>]. The resulting tree topologies are then compared to trusted reference topologies. For applications to genomic sequences, we modified our distance <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em> <sub> <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> </sub> by taking into account that sequences can contain repeats and homologies on different strands. Obviously, this is only a very rough way of evaluating sequence-comparison methods, since the resulting tree topologies not only depend on the distance values calculated by the evaluated methods, but also on the tree-reconstruction method that is applied to them. Also, comparing topologies ignores branch lengths, so the results of these benchmark studies depend only indirectly on the distance values calculated by the alignment-free methods that are to be evaluated.</p>
           <p xmlns:func="http://oscar.fig.bmc.com" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="Para">Three remarkable exceptions are the papers describing <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">K</em> <sub> <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">r</em> </sub> [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR37" data-toggle="citation">37</a></span>], <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">Co-phylog</em> [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR38" data-toggle="citation">38</a></span>] and <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">andi</em> [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR39" data-toggle="citation">39</a></span>]. <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">K</em> <sub> <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">r</em> </sub> estimates evolutionary distances based on <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">shortest unique substrings</em>, <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">Co-phylog</em> uses so-called <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">microalignments</em> defined by <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">spaced-word</em> matches and considers the <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">don’t care positions</em> to estimate distances, while <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">andi</em> uses gap-free local alignments bounded by maximal unique matches. To our knowledge, these approaches are the only alignment-free methods so far that try to estimate phylogenetic distances in a rigorous way, based on a probabilistic model of evolution. Consequently, the authors of <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">K</em> <sub> <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">r</em> </sub>, <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">Co-phylog</em> and <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">andy</em> compared the distance values calculated by their methods directly to reference distances. Haubold <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">et al.</em> could show that <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">K</em> <sub> <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">r</em> </sub> can correctly estimate evolutionary distances between DNA sequences up to around 0.5 mutations per site [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR37" data-toggle="citation">37</a></span>].</p>
           <p xmlns:func="http://oscar.fig.bmc.com" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="Para">In previous papers, we have shown, that our <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">spaced-word</em> approach is useful for phylogeny reconstruction. Tree topologies calculated with <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">Neighbour-Joining</em> based on <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">spaced-word</em> frequency vectors are usually superior to topologies calculated from the <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">contiguous</em> word frequency vectors that are used by traditional alignment-free methods [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR25" data-toggle="citation">25</a></span>]. Moreover, we could show that the ‘multiple-pattern approach’ leads to much better results than the ‘single-pattern approach’; these results were confirmed by Noé and Martin [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR40" data-toggle="citation">40</a></span>]. We also showed experimentally that distance values and tree topologies produced from <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">spaced-word</em> frequencies are statistically more stable than those based on <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">contiguous</em> words. In fact, the main difference between our <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">spaced words</em> and the commonly used <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">contiguous words</em> is that spaced-word matches at neighbouring positions are statistically less dependent on each other.</p>
           <p xmlns:func="http://oscar.fig.bmc.com" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="Para">Since the aim of our previous papers was to compare (multiple) spaced-word frequencies to contiguous word frequencies, we applied the same distance metrics to our spaced-word frequencies that are applied by standard methods to <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">k</em>-mer frequencies, namely <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">Jensen-Shannon</em> and the <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">Euclidean</em> distance. In the present paper, we propose a new pairwise distance measure based on a probabilistic model of DNA evolution. We estimate the evolutionary distance between two nucleic-acid sequences based on the number <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> of space-word matches between them. We show that this distance measure is more accurate and works for more distantly related sequences than existing alignment-free distance measures. In addition, we calculate the <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">variance</em> of <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> for <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">contiguous</em> <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">k</em>-mers, as well as for <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">spaced words</em> using our single and multiple pattern approaches. We show that the variance of <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> is lower for spaced words than for contiguous words and that the variance is further reduced if <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">multiple</em> patterns are used.</p>
           <p xmlns:func="http://oscar.fig.bmc.com" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="Para">This paper is an extended version of a manuscript that was first published in the proceedings of <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">Workshop on Algorithms in Bioinformatics (WABI) 2013</em> in Wroclaw, Poland [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR41" data-toggle="citation">41</a></span>]. We added two extensions to our WABI paper that are crucial if our method is to be applied to real-world genomic sequences. (a) While the original version of our distance function assumed that homologies are located on the same <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">strand</em> of two genomes under comparison, we modified our original distance measure to account for homologies that are on different strands. (b) The number <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> of spaced-word matches is highly sensitive to <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">repeats</em> in the compared sequences, and our previously defined distance function could grossly under-estimate phylogenetic distances in the present of repeats. We therefore propose a simple modification of this distance function that is insensitive to repeats. Finally, we added more test data sets to evaluate our method.</p>

          <h2 class="Heading js-ToggleCollapseSection">Estimating evolutionary distances from the number <em class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> of spaced-word matches</h2>
          <div class="js-CollapseSection">
           <p xmlns:func="http://oscar.fig.bmc.com" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="Para">If the <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">weight</em> of the patterns – <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">i.e.</em> the number of match positions – in the <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">spaced-words</em> approach is sufficiently large, random space-word matches can be ignored. In this case, the <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">Jensen-Shannon</em> distance between two DNA sequences approximates the number of (spaced) words that occur in one of the compared sequences, but not in the other one. Thus, if two sequences of length <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">L</em> are compared and <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> is the number of (spaced) words that two sequences have in common, their <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">Jenson-Shannon</em> distance can be approximated by <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">L</em>?<em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em>. Accordingly, the <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">Euclidean</em> distances between two sequences can be approximated by the square root of this value if the distance is small and <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">k</em> is large enough. For <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">small</em> evolutionary distances, the <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">Jensen-Shannon</em> distance grows therefore roughly linearly with the distance between two sequences, and this explains why it is possible to produce reasonable phylogenies based on this metric. It is clear, however, that the <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">Jensen-Shannon</em> distance is far from linear to the real distance for larger distances. We therefore propose an alternative estimator of the evolutionary distance between two sequences in terms of the number <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> of <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">spaced-word</em> matches between them.</p>
           <div xmlns:func="http://oscar.fig.bmc.com" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="Para">

            <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em>) of spaced words shared by sequences 
            <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">S</em> 
            <sub>1</sub> and 
            <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">S</em> 
            <sub>2</sub> with respect to a set of patterns 
            <a target="_blank" href="http://media.springernature.com/full/springer-static/image/art%3A10.1186%2Fs13015-015-0032-x/MediaObjects/13015_2015_32_Figa_HTML.gif"><img src="http://media.springernature.com/lw785/springer-static/image/art%3A10.1186%2Fs13015-015-0032-x/MediaObjects/13015_2015_32_Figa_HTML.gif"></a> as given in equation (
            <span class="InternalRef"><a href="#Equ2">2</a></span>), we obtain 
            <div id="Equ3" class="Equation EquationMathjax">
             <div class="EquationContent">
              $$ \hat{p} = \sqrt[k]{\frac{N}{m\cdot (L-\ell+1)}- (L-\ell) \cdot q^{k}} $$
             </div> 
             <div class="EquationNumber">
              (3)
             </div>
            </div> 
           </div>
           <div xmlns:func="http://oscar.fig.bmc.com" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="Para">

             </div> 
             <div class="EquationNumber">

             </div>
            </div>

Note that for a model without insertions and deletions, it is, of course, not necessary to estimate the mismatch probability <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">p</em> from the number <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> of spaced-word matches.           <div class="js-CollapseSection">
           <p xmlns:func="http://oscar.fig.bmc.com" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="Para">Our new distance measure and other word-based distance measures depend on the number <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> of (spaced) word matches between sequences. To simplify this, we make a further assumption on our sequence model: we assume that the four nucleotides occur with the same probability 0.25.             <sup>?</sup>,
            <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">s</em>) to be the number of integers that are match positions of 
            <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">P</em> or match positions of 
            <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">P</em>

            <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">s</em> positions to the right (or both). Formally, if

            <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">s</em> positions to the right, we define

            </div>

             </div>
            </div> so one has 
            <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">n</em>(
            <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">P</em>,
            <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">P</em> 
            <sup>?</sup>,
            <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">s</em>)=6. In particular, one has 
            <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">n</em>(
            <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">P</em>,
            <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">P</em>,0)=
            <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">k</em> for all patterns 
            <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">P</em> of weight 
            <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">k</em>, and 
            <div id="Equq" class="Equation EquationMathjax">
             <div class="EquationContent">
              $$ n(P,P,s) = k + \max\{s,k\} $$
             </div>
            </div> for all 
            <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">contiguous</em> patterns 
            <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">P</em> of weight (or length) 
            <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">k</em>. With this notation, we can write 
            <div id="Equ10" class="Equation EquationMathjax">
             <div class="EquationContent">
              $$\begin{array}{@{}rcl@{}} E\left(X_{i,j}^{P} \cdot X_{i+s,j+s}^{P'} \right) &amp; = &amp;\left\{ \begin{array}{ll} p^{n(P,P',s)} &amp; \text{if}\ \ i = j\\ q^{n(P,P',s)} &amp; \text{else} \end{array} \right. \end{array} $$
             </div> 
             <div class="EquationNumber">
              (9)
             </div>
            </div> 
           </div>
           <p xmlns:func="http://oscar.fig.bmc.com" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="Para">for all <span class="InlineEquation" id="IEq31">\(X_{i,j}^{P}, X_{i+s,j+s}^{P'}\)</span> </p>
           <div xmlns:func="http://oscar.fig.bmc.com" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="Para">
            To calculate the covariance of two random variables from 
            <a target="_blank" href="http://media.springernature.com/full/springer-static/image/art%3A10.1186%2Fs13015-015-0032-x/MediaObjects/13015_2015_32_Figb_HTML.gif"><img src="http://media.springernature.com/lw785/springer-static/image/art%3A10.1186%2Fs13015-015-0032-x/MediaObjects/13015_2015_32_Figb_HTML.gif"></a> , we distinguish again between homologous and random matches. We first consider ’homologous’ pairs 
            <span class="InlineEquation" id="IEq32">\(X_{i,i}^{P}, X_{i+s,i+s}^{P'} \in \mathcal {X}_{\textit {Hom}}\)</span>. Here, we obtain with (
            <span class="InternalRef"><a href="#Equ10">9</a></span>) 
            <div id="Equ11" class="Equation EquationMathjax">
             <div class="EquationContent">
              $$ \begin{aligned} Cov \left(X_{i,i}^{P}, X_{i+s,i+s}^{P'}\right) &amp;= p^{n(P,P',s)} - p^{2k} \end{aligned} $$
             </div> 
             <div class="EquationNumber">
              (10)
             </div>
            </div> 
           </div>
           <div xmlns:func="http://oscar.fig.bmc.com" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="Para">
            Similarly, for a pair of ‘background’ variables 
            <span class="InlineEquation" id="IEq33">\(X_{i,j}^{P},\)</span> 
            <span class="InlineEquation" id="IEq34">\( X_{i+s,j+s}^{P'} \in \mathcal {X}_{\textit {BG}}\)</span>, one obtains 
            <div id="Equ12" class="Equation EquationMathjax">
             <div class="EquationContent">
              $$ \begin{aligned} Cov \left(X_{i,j}^{P}, X_{i+s,j+s}^{P'}\right) &amp;= q^{n(P,P',s)} - q^{2k}. \end{aligned} $$
             </div> 
             <div class="EquationNumber">
              (11)
             </div>
            </div> 
           </div>
           <div xmlns:func="http://oscar.fig.bmc.com" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="Para">
            Since ‘homologous’ and ‘background’ variables are uncorrelated, the variance of 
            <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> can be written as 
            <div id="Equr" class="Equation EquationMathjax">
             <div class="EquationContent">
              $$\begin{aligned} Var(N) =&amp;\; Var \left(\sum_{X\in\mathcal{X}} X\right) = Var \left(\sum_{X\in \mathcal{X}_{Hom}}\right)\\ &amp;+ Var \left(\sum_{X\in \mathcal{X}_{BG}}\right) \end{aligned} $$
             </div>
            </div> 
           </div>
           <div xmlns:func="http://oscar.fig.bmc.com" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="Para">
            We express the variance of these sums of random variable as the sum of all of their covariances, so for the ’homologous’ random variables we can write 
            <div id="Equs" class="Equation EquationMathjax">
             <div class="EquationContent">
              $$ Var \left(\sum_{X\in \mathcal{X}_{Hom}}X\right) = \sum_{P,P'\in\mathcal{P}} \sum_{i,i'=1}^{L-l+1} Cov \left(X_{i,i}^{P}, X_{i',i'}^{P'} \right) $$
             </div>
            </div> 
           </div>
           <div xmlns:func="http://oscar.fig.bmc.com" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="Para">
            Since the covariance for non-correlated random variables vanishes, we can ignore the covariances of all pairs 
            <span class="InlineEquation" id="IEq35">\(\left (X_{i,i}^{P}, X_{i',i'}^{P'}\right)\)</span> with |
            <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">i</em>?
            <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">i</em> 
            <sup>?</sup>|?
            <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">l</em> so, ignoring side effects, we can write the above sum as 
            <div id="Equt" class="Equation EquationMathjax">
             <div class="EquationContent">
              $${\fontsize{9.3}{6}\begin{aligned} Var \left(\sum_{X\in \mathcal{X}_{Hom}} X\right) &amp; \approx &amp;\sum_{i=1}^{L-\ell+1} \sum_{P,P'\in\mathcal{P}} \sum_{s= -\ell + 1}^{\ell-1} Cov \left(X_{i,i}^{P}, X_{i+s,i+s}^{P'} \right) \end{aligned}} $$
             </div>
            </div> and since the above covariances depend only on 
            <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">s</em> but not on 
            <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">i</em>, we can use (
            <span class="InternalRef"><a href="#Equ10">9</a></span>) and (
            <span class="InternalRef"><a href="#Equ12">11</a></span>) and obtain 
            <div id="Equu" class="Equation EquationMathjax">
             <div class="EquationContent">
              $$\begin{aligned} Var &amp;\left(\sum_{X\in \mathcal{X}_{Hom}} X\right) \approx (L-\ell+1)\\ &amp;\times\sum_{P,P'\in\mathcal{P}} \sum_{s=-\ell+1}^{\ell-1} \left(p^{n(P,P',s)} - p^{2k}\right) \end{aligned} $$
             </div>
            </div> and similarly 
            <div id="Equv" class="Equation EquationMathjax">
             <div class="EquationContent">
              $$\begin{aligned} Var &amp;\left(\sum_{X\in \mathcal{X}_{BG}}X\right) \approx (L-\ell+1) \cdot (L-\ell)\\ &amp;\times\sum_{P,P'\in\mathcal{P}}\sum_{s=-\ell+1}^{\ell-1} \left(q^{n(P,P',s)} - q^{2k}\right) \end{aligned} $$
             </div>
            </div> 
           </div>
           <div xmlns:func="http://oscar.fig.bmc.com" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="Para">
            Together, we get 
            <div id="Equ13" class="Equation EquationMathjax">
             <div class="EquationContent">
              $$ {\begin{aligned} Var(N) \approx &amp; \;(L-\ell+1) \cdot \sum_{P,P'\in\mathcal{P}} \sum_{s=-\ell+1}^{\ell-1} \left(p^{n(P,P',s)} - p^{2k} \right)\\ &amp; +\ \ (L-\ell+1) \cdot (L-\ell) \\ &amp;\times\sum_{P,P'\in\mathcal{P}} \sum_{s=-\ell+1}^{\ell-1} \left(q^{n(P,P',s)} - q^{2k}\right) \end{aligned}} $$
             </div> 
             <div class="EquationNumber">
              (12)
             </div>
            </div> 
           </div>
          </div>

            <h3 class="Heading">Simulated DNA sequences</h3> 
            <p xmlns:func="http://oscar.fig.bmc.com" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="Para">To evaluate the distance function <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em> <sub> <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> </sub> defined by equation (<span class="InternalRef"><a href="#Equ4">4</a></span>), we simulated pairs of DNA sequences with an (average) length of 100,000 and with an average of <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em> substitutions per sequence position. More precisely, we generated sequence pairs by generating a first sequence using a Bernoulli model with probability 0.25 for each nucleotide. A second sequence was then generated from the first sequence by substituting nucleotides with a probability corresponding to the substitution frequency <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em>, as calculated with Jukes-Cantor. We varied <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em> between 0 and 1 and compared the distances estimated by our distance measure and by various other alignment-free programs to the ‘real’ distance <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em>. We performed these experiments for sequence pairs without insertions and deletions and for sequence pairs where we included insertions and deletions with a probability of 1<em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">%</em> at every position. The length of indels was randomly chosen between 1 and 50 with uniform probability.</p> 
            <div xmlns:func="http://oscar.fig.bmc.com" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="Para">
             Figure 
             <span class="InternalRef"><a href="#Fig1">1</a></span> shows the results of these experiments. Our new distance measure 
             <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em> 
             <sub> <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> </sub> applied to spaced-word frequencies is well in accordance with the real distances 
             <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em> for values of 
             <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em>?0.8 on sequence pairs without insertions and deletions if the 
             <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">single-pattern</em> version of our program is used. For the 
             <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">multiple-pattern</em> version, our distance function estimates the real distances correctly for all values of 
             <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em>?1. If indels are added as specified above, our distance functions slightly overestimates the real distance 
             <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em>. By contrast, the 
             <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">Jensen-Shannon</em> distance applied to the same spaced-word frequencies increased non-linearly with 
             <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em> and flattened for values of around 
             <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em>?0.4.

In our study, <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">K</em> <sub> <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">r</em> </sub> correctly estimated the true distance <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em> for values of around <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em>?0.6, this precisely corresponds to the results reported by the authors of the program. For larger distances, <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">K</em> <sub> <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">r</em> </sub> grossly overestimates the distance <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em>, though, and the variance strongly increases. The distances estimated by <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">Co-phylog</em> [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR38" data-toggle="citation">38</a></span>] nearly coincide with the substitution rate <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em> for values <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em>?0.7, then the curve flattens. The distance values calculated by the program <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">k mismatch average common substring (kmacs)</em> that we previously developed [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR47" data-toggle="citation">47</a></span>] are roughly linear to the real distances <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em> for values of up to around <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em>=0.3. From around <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em>=0.5 on, the curve becomes flat. With <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">k</em>=30 mismatches, the performance of <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">kmacs</em> was better than with <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">k</em>=0, in which case <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">kmacs</em> corresponds to the <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">Average Common Substring (ACS)</em> approach [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR30" data-toggle="citation">30</a></span>].</p>

            <h3 class="Heading">Real-world genomes</h3> 
            <p xmlns:func="http://oscar.fig.bmc.com" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="Para">Next, we applied various distance measures to a set of 27 mitochondrial genomes from different primates that were previously used by [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR46" data-toggle="citation">46</a></span>] as a benchmark data set for alignment-free approaches. We used our <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">multiple spaced-words</em> approach with the parameters that we used in [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR25" data-toggle="citation">25</a></span>], that is with a pattern weight (number of match positions) of <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">k</em>=9 and with pattern lengths <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">?</em> between 9 and 39, <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">i.e.</em> with up to 30 <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">don’t-care</em> positions in the patterns. For each value of <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">?</em>, we randomly generated sets <a target="_blank" href="http://media.springernature.com/full/springer-static/image/art%3A10.1186%2Fs13015-015-0032-x/MediaObjects/13015_2015_32_Figa_HTML.gif"><img src="http://media.springernature.com/lw785/springer-static/image/art%3A10.1186%2Fs13015-015-0032-x/MediaObjects/13015_2015_32_Figa_HTML.gif"></a> of <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">m</em>=100 patterns. For this data set, we used the distance <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em> <sub> <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">RC</em> </sub> defined in equation (<span class="InternalRef"><a href="#Equ9">8</a></span>) that takes the reverse complement of the input sequences into account. (We did not use the ‘binary’ version <span class="InlineEquation" id="IEq36">\(d_{\textit {RC}}^{bin}\)</span> of our distance function, since these sequences do not contain major repeats). In addition, we used the competing approaches <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">FFP</em> [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR32" data-toggle="citation">32</a></span>], CVTree [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR48" data-toggle="citation">48</a></span>], <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">K</em> <sub> <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">r</em> </sub> [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR37" data-toggle="citation">37</a></span>], <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">kmacs</em> [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR47" data-toggle="citation">47</a></span>], <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">ACS</em> [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR30" data-toggle="citation">30</a></span>] and Co-phylog [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR38" data-toggle="citation">38</a></span>]. The first data set consists of 26 <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">E. coli</em> and <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">Shigella</em> genomes, which are very closely related and the second data set consist of 32 <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">Roseobacter</em> genomes, which are far more divergent. As for the primate mitochondrial genomes, we calculated distance matrices using the same alignment-free methods, constructed trees with <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">Neighbor-Joining</em> and compared the resulting tree topologies to a benchmark tree using the <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">RF</em> metric. For the <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">E.coli/Shigella</em> genomes we used the tree proposed by [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR53" data-toggle="citation">53</a></span>] as reference which is based on concatenated alignments of the 2034 core genes. For the <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">Roseobacter</em> genomes we used the tree by [<span class="CitationRef"><a href="#CR54" data-toggle="citation">54</a></span>] as reference.            <section id="Sec10" class="Section2 RenderAsSection2"> 
            <h3 class="Heading">The variance of <em class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em>: experimental results</h3> 
            <div xmlns:func="http://oscar.fig.bmc.com" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="Para">
             Figure 
             <span class="InternalRef"><a href="#Fig1">1</a></span> shows not only striking differences in the shape of the distance functions used by various alignment-free programs. There are also remarkable differences in the 
             <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">variance</em> of the distances calculated with our new distance measure 
             <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em> 
             <sub> <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> </sub> that we defined in equation (
             <span class="InternalRef"><a href="#Equ4">4</a></span>). The distance 
             <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">D</em> 
             <sub> <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> </sub> is defined in terms of the number 
             <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> of 
             <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">(spaced) word</em> matches between two sequences. As mentioned above, the established 
             <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">Jensen-Shannon</em> and 
             <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">Euclidean</em> distances on (spaced) word frequency vectors also depend on 
             <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em>, for small distances, they can be approximated by 
             <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">L</em>?
             <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> and 
             <span class="InlineEquation" id="IEq40">\(\sqrt {L-N}\)</span>, respectively. Thus, the variances of these three distance measures directly depend on the variance of 
             <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em>. As can be seen in Figure 
             <span class="InternalRef"><a href="#Fig1">1</a></span>, the variance of 
             <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em> 
             <sub> <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> </sub> increases with the frequency of substitutions. Also, the variance is higher for the single-pattern approach than for the multiple-pattern approach. To explain this observation, we calculated the 
             <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">variance</em> of the normalized number 
             <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em>/
             <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">m</em> of spaced-word matches using equation 
             <span class="InternalRef"><a href="#Equ13">12</a></span>. Figure 
             <span class="InternalRef"><a href="#Fig6">6</a></span> summarizes the results for a sequence length of 
             <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">L</em>=16.000 and mismatch frequencies of 0.7 and 0.25, respectively. As can be seen, for single spaced words the variance of 
             <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em>/
             <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">m</em> is far smaller than for contiguous words, and for multiple spaced words, the variance is further reduced. 
             <figure xmlns="" class="Figure" id="Fig6">
              <div xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="MediaObject" id="MO6"> 
               <a target="_blank" href="http://media.springernature.com/full/springer-static/image/art%3A10.1186%2Fs13015-015-0032-x/MediaObjects/13015_2015_32_Fig6_HTML.gif"><img src="http://media.springernature.com/lw785/springer-static/image/art%3A10.1186%2Fs13015-015-0032-x/MediaObjects/13015_2015_32_Fig6_HTML.gif"></a> 
              </div>
              <figcaption class="Caption" lang="en">
               <div xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="CaptionContent">
                <span class="CaptionNumber">Figure 6</span> 
                <p xmlns="" class="SimplePara"> <strong class="EmphasisTypeBold">Variance of the number of spaced-word matches.</strong> Variance of the normalized number <span xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="InlineEquation" id="IEq41">\(\frac {N}{m}\)</span> of spaced-word matches where <span xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="InlineEquation" id="IEq42">\(m={|\mathcal {P}|}\)</span> is the number of patterns in the multiple-pattern approach. Formula (<span xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="InternalRef"><a href="#Equ13">12</a></span>) was applied to contiguous words and to single and multiple spaced words for un-gapped sequence pairs of length 16,000 <em class="EmphasisTypeItalic">nt</em> with a mismatch frequency of 0.7 (left) and 0.25 (right).</p>

          <h2 class="Heading js-ToggleCollapseSection">Discussion</h2>
          <div class="js-CollapseSection">
           <p xmlns:func="http://oscar.fig.bmc.com" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="Para">In this paper, we proposed a new estimator <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em> <sub> <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> </sub> for the evolutionary distance between two DNA sequences that is based on the number <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> of <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">spaced-word</em> matches between them. While most alignment-free methods use ad-hoc distance measures, the distance function that we defined is based on a probabilistic model of evolution and seems to be a good estimator for the number of substitutions per site that have occurred since two sequences have evolved separately. For simplicity, we used a model of evolution without insertions and deletions. Nevertheless, our test results show that our distance function is still a reasonable estimator if the input sequences contain a moderate number of insertions and deletions although, in this case, distances between the input sequences are overestimated since the number <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> of spaced-word matches is smaller than it would be for sequences without indels.</p>
           <p xmlns:func="http://oscar.fig.bmc.com" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="Para">The model that we used to derive our distance <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em> <sub> <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> </sub> assumes that two sequences are globally related. If sequences share only local homology, the number <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> of spaced-word matches would be smaller than for globally related sequences with the same length and rate of mismatches, so their distance would be over-estimated by our distance measure <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em> <sub> <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> </sub>. This is clearly a limitation of our approach. However, as indicated in section <span class="InternalRef"><a href="#Sec4">Estimating evolutionary distances from the number <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> of spaced-word matches</a></span>, our distance function can be adapted to the case of <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">local</em> homologies if the length of these homologies and the number of gaps in the homologous regions can be estimated. In principle, it should therefore be possible to apply our method to locally related sequences by first estimating the extent of their shared (local) homology and then using the distance <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em> <sub> <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">loc</em> </sub> defined in equation (<span class="InternalRef"><a href="#Equ6">5</a></span>) instead of <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">d</em> <sub> <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> </sub>.</p>
           <p xmlns:func="http://oscar.fig.bmc.com" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="Para">The distance measures introduced in this paper and other distances that we previously used for our <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">spaced words</em> approach depend on the number <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> of space-word matches between two sequences with respect to a set <a target="_blank" href="http://media.springernature.com/full/springer-static/image/art%3A10.1186%2Fs13015-015-0032-x/MediaObjects/13015_2015_32_Figa_HTML.gif"><img src="http://media.springernature.com/lw785/springer-static/image/art%3A10.1186%2Fs13015-015-0032-x/MediaObjects/13015_2015_32_Figa_HTML.gif"></a> of patterns of ‘match’ and ‘don’t care’ positions. This is similar for more traditional alignment-free methods that calculated distances based on <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">k</em>-mer frequencies. While the <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">expected</em> number of (spaced) word matches is essentially the same for contiguous words and for spaced words of the corresponding <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">weight</em>, we have showed that the <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">variance</em> of <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> is considerably lower for spaced-words than for the traditionally used contiguous words. Moreover, with our <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">multiple-pattern</em> approach the variance of the normalized number of spaced-word matches is further reduced. This seems to be the main reason why our <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">multiple spaced words</em> approach outperforms the <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">single-pattern</em> approach that we previously introduced as well as the classical <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">k</em>-mer approach when used for phylogeny reconstruction.</p>
           <p xmlns:func="http://oscar.fig.bmc.com" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1999/xhtml" class="Para">As we have shown, the variance of <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> depends on the number of overlapping ‘match’ positions if patterns from <a target="_blank" href="http://media.springernature.com/full/springer-static/image/art%3A10.1186%2Fs13015-015-0032-x/MediaObjects/13015_2015_32_Figa_HTML.gif"><img src="http://media.springernature.com/lw785/springer-static/image/art%3A10.1186%2Fs13015-015-0032-x/MediaObjects/13015_2015_32_Figa_HTML.gif"></a> are shifted against each other. Consequently, in our single-pattern approach, the variance of <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> is higher for periodic patterns than for non-periodic patterns. For example, for the periodic pattern 101010…, the variance is equal to the variance of the contiguous pattern of the corresponding weight. In our previous benchmark studies, we could experimentally confirm that <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">spaced words</em> performs better with non-periodic patterns than with periodic patterns. The theoretical results of this study may be useful to find patterns or sets of patterns that minimize the variance of <em xmlns="" class="EmphasisTypeItalic">N</em> to further improve our spaced-words approach.</p>
          </div>
