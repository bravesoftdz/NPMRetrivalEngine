           <p pid="1931"><span style="color: rgb(0, 0, 0); float: none; font-size: medium; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px;">Whether it is for Twitter sentiment analysis or for solving search problems natural language processing (NLP) has become the fulcrum of much of my hobby work in recent years. Initially I usually found myself relying on the</span><a href="http://www.nltk.org/" style="Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-family: &amp;apos; font-size: medium; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px;" rel="nofollow"><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>Natural Language Toolkit (NLTK)</a><span style="color: rgb(0, 0, 0); float: none; font-size: medium; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px;"><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>which is a rich library of NLP algorithms for</span><a href="http://www.python.org/" style="Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-family: &amp;apos; font-size: medium; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px;" rel="nofollow"><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>Python</a><span style="color: rgb(0, 0, 0); float: none; font-size: medium; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px;">. The NLTK is simply fantastic. It's a true one-stop-NLP-shop that's widely adopted, well documented, and open source. Certainly I had to learn what the algorithms did and how they fit together but for the most part the hard work was done for me. It was a very productive situation, to be sure!</span></p> 
           <p pid="1931">Last year, however, brought a new platform to my hobby work:<a href="http://nodejs.org/" style="background-color: rgb(255, 255, 255); font-size: medium; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; widows: 2;" rel="nofollow">node.js</a>. My, node and its community were young but maturing rapidly.</p> 
           <p pid="1931">When the need for natural language facilities arose and I found the pickings pretty slim. I have to be honest. That's *exactly* what I was hoping for; an opportunity to sink my teeth into the algorithms themselves and contribute them back to a young, but growing, community.</p> 
           <p pid="1931">Thus I began work on<a href="https://github.com/NaturalNode/natural" style="background-color: rgb(255, 255, 255); font-size: medium; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; widows: 2;" rel="nofollow">"natural"</a>, a module of base natural languages processing algorithms for node.js. The idea was loosely based on the Python NLTK in that all algorithms are in the same package. Initially I didn't think "natural" could be as complete as the NLTK, but as my own understanding as well as community contributions picked up I've become much more hopeful. Also, merging with Rob Ellis's node-nltools back in August of 2011 strengthened "natural" further by rapidly bringing new algorithms and features into the fold.</p>

           <h2>Installing</h2> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1938">Like most node modules "natural" is packaged as an NPM and can be installed from the command line as such:</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">npm install natural</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1939">If you want to install from source (or contribute for that matter) it can be found<a href="https://github.com/NaturalNode/natural" rel="nofollow"><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>here on GitHub</a>.</p> 
           <h2>Stemming</h2> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1941">The first class of algorithms I'd like to outline is stemming. Stemming is the processes of reducing a word to a root (not necessarily the morphological root). In other words the idea is to boil all conjugations, tenses and forms down to a single root word. That root may not end up looking exactly like the English root, but should be close enough for comparison.</p> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1942">Stemming is a typical step in preparing text for use by other algorithms or storage such as classification or even full-text indexing. Both the Lancaster and Porter algorithms are supported as of 0.1.5. Here's a basic example of stemming a word with a Porter Stemmer.</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">var natural = require('natural'),
stemmer = natural.PorterStemmer;

var stem = stemmer.stem('stems');
console.log(stem);
stem = stemmer.stem('stemming');
console.log(stem);
stem = stemmer.stem('stemmed');
console.log(stem);
stem = stemmer.stem('stem');
console.log(stem);</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1943">Above I simply required-up the main "natural" module and grabbed the PorterStemmer sub-module from within. Calling the "stem" function takes an arbitrary string and returns the stem. The above code returns the following output:</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">stem
stem
stem
stem</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1944">For convenience stemmers can patch String with methods to simplify the process by calling the<em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>attach</em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>method. String objects will then have a<em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>stem</em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>method.</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">stemmer.attach();
stem = 'stemming'.stem();
console.log(stem);</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1945">It's very possible you'd be interested in stemming a string composed of many words, perhaps an entire document. The<em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>attach</em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>method provides a<em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>tokenizeAndStem</em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>method to accomplish this. It breaks the owning string up into an array of strings, one for each word, and stems them all. For example:</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">var stems = 'stems returned'.tokenizeAndStem();
console.log(stems);</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1946">produces the output:</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">[ 'stem', 'return' ]</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1947">Note that the<em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>tokenizeAndStem</em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>method will omit certain words by default that are considered irrelevant (stop words) from the return array. To instruct the stemmer to not omit stop words pass a<em>true</em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>in to<em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>tokenizeAndStem</em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>for the<em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>keepStops</em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>parameter. Consider:</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">console.log('i stemmed words.'.tokenizeAndStem());
console.log('i stemmed words.'.tokenizeAndStem(true));</code></pre> 
           <p pid="1948"><span style="color: rgb(0, 0, 0); float: none; font-size: medium; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px;">outputting:</span></p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">[ 'stem', 'word' ]
[ 'i', 'stem', 'word' ]</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1949">All of the code above would also work with a Lancaster stemmer by requiring the LancasterStemmer module instead, like:</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">var natural = require('natural'),
    stemmer = natural.LancasterStemmer;</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1950">Of course the actual stems produced could be different depending on the algorithm chosen. The Lancaster stemmer tends to be a bit more agressive resulting in roots that look less like their English equivalents, but will likely perform better.</p> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1951"><strong>Phonetics</strong></p> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1952">Phonetic algorithms are also provided to determine what words sound like and compare them accordingly. The old (and I mean pre-electronic computers old... like 1918 old) SoundEx and the more modern Metaphone/Double Metaphone algorithms are supported as of 0.1.5.</p> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1953">The following example compares the string "phonetics" and the intentional misspelling "fonetix" and determines they sound alike according to the Metaphone module but the same pattern could be applied to the DoubleMetaphone or SoundEx modules.</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">var natural = require('natural'),
phonetic = natural.Metaphone;

var wordA = 'phonetics';
var wordB = 'fonetix';

if(phonetic.compare(wordA, wordB))
    console.log('they sound alike!');</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1954">The raw code the phonetic algorithm produces can be retrieved with the<em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>process</em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>method:</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">var phoneticCode = phonetic.process('phonetics');
console.log(phoneticCode);</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1955">resulting in:</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">FNTKS</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1956">Like the stemming implementations the phonetic modules have an<em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>attach</em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>method that patches String with shortcut methods, most notably<em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>soundsLike</em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>for comparison:</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">phonetic.attach();

if(wordA.soundsLike(wordB))
    console.log('they sound alike!');</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1957"><em>attach</em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>also patches in a<em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>phonetics</em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>and<em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>tokenizeAndPhoneticize</em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>methods to retrieve the phonetic code for a single word and an entire corpus respectively.</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">console.log('phonetics'.phonetics());
console.log('phonetics rock'.tokenizeAndPhoneticize());</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1958">which outputs:</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">FNTKS
[ 'FNTKS', 'RK' ]</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1959">The above could could also use SoundEx by substituting the following in for the require.</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">var natural = require('natural'),
    phonetic = natural.SoundEx;</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1960">Note that SoundEx and Metaphone may have trouble with non-English words, but Double Metaphone should have some degree of success with many other languages.</p> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1961"><strong>tf*idf</strong></p> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1962">tf*idf weights can be used to judge how important a given word is to a given document in a broader corpus (collection of documents). There are two components to a tf*idf weight: the term frequency and the inverse document frequency. To guarantee that a frequently-used, albeit semantically less important, word doesn't gain too much favor you'll want to ensure you have many documents in your TfIdf clone.</p> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1963">Consider the following code which adds a few documents to a corpus and then determines how important the words "ruby" and "node" are to them.</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">var natural = require('natural'),
    TfIdf = natural.TfIdf,
    tfidf = new TfIdf();

tfidf.addDocument('i code in c.');
tfidf.addDocument('i code in ruby.');
tfidf.addDocument('i code in ruby and node, but node more often.');
tfidf.addDocument('this document is about natural, written in node');
tfidf.addDocument('i code in fortran.');

console.log('node --------------------------------');
tfidf.tfidfs('node', function(i, measure) {
    console.log('document #' + i + ' is ' + measure);
});

console.log('ruby --------------------------------');
tfidf.tfidfs('ruby', function(i, measure) {
    console.log('document #' + i + ' is ' + measure);
});
</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1964">The previous code will output the tf*idf weights for "node" and "ruby". The higher the weight the more important the word is to the document.</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">node --------------------------------
document #0 is 0
document #1 is 0
document #2 is 3.347952867143343
document #3 is 1.6739764335716716
document #4 is 0
ruby --------------------------------
document #0 is 0
document #1 is 1.6739764335716716
document #2 is 1.6739764335716716
document #3 is 0
document #4 is 0
</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1965">Additionally, you can measure a word against a single document.</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">console.log(tfidf.tfidf('node', 0 /* document index */));
console.log(tfidf.tfidf('node', 1));</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1966">You can also get a list of all terms in a document ordered by their importance.</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">tfidf.listTerms(4 /* document index */).forEach(function(item) {
    console.log(item.term + ': ' + item.tfidf);
});</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1967">yeilding:</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">fortran: 1.7047480922384253
code: 1.6486586255873816</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1968"><strong>Inflection</strong></p> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1969">Basic inflectors are in place to convert nouns between plural and singular forms and to turn integers into string counters (i.e. '1st', '2nd', '3rd', '4th 'etc.).</p> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1970">The following example converts the word "radius" into its plural form "radii".</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">var natural = require('natural'),
    nounInflector = new natural.NounInflector();

var plural = nounInflector.pluralize('radius');
console.log(plural);</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1971">Singularization follows the same pattern as is illustrated in the following example wich converts the word "beers" to its singular form, "beer".</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">var singular = nounInflector.singularize('beers');
console.log(singular);</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1972">Just like the stemming and phonetic modules an<em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>attach</em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>method is provided to patch String with shortcut methods.</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">nounInflector.attach();
console.log('radius'.pluralizeNoun());
console.log('beers'.singularizeNoun()); </code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1973">A NounInflector instance can do custom conversion if you provide expressions via the<em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>addPlural</em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>and<em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>addSingular</em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>methods. Because these conversion aren't always symmetric (sometimes more patterns may be required to singularize forms than pluralize) there needn't be a one-to-one relationship between<em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>addPlural</em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>and<em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>addSingular</em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>calls.</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">nounInflector.addPlural(/(code|ware)/i, '$1z');
nounInflector.addSingular(/(code|ware)z/i, '$1');

console.log('code'.pluralizeNoun());
console.log('ware'.pluralizeNoun());

console.log('codez'.singularizeNoun());
console.log('warez'.singularizeNoun());</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1974">which would result in:</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">codez
warez
code
ware</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1975">Here's an example of using the CountInflector module to produce string counter for integers.</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">var natural = require('natural'),
    countInflector = natural.CountInflector;

console.log(countInflector.nth(1));
console.log(countInflector.nth(2));
console.log(countInflector.nth(3));
console.log(countInflector.nth(4));
console.log(countInflector.nth(10));
console.log(countInflector.nth(11));
console.log(countInflector.nth(12));
console.log(countInflector.nth(13));
console.log(countInflector.nth(100));
console.log(countInflector.nth(101));
console.log(countInflector.nth(102));
console.log(countInflector.nth(103));
console.log(countInflector.nth(110));
console.log(countInflector.nth(111));
console.log(countInflector.nth(112));
console.log(countInflector.nth(113));</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1976">producing:</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">1st
2nd
3rd
4th
10th
11th
12th
13th
100th
101st
102nd
103rd
110th
111th
112th
113th</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1977"><strong>Classification</strong></p> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1978">Classification is currently supported by the Naive Bayes and Logistic regression algorithms, although natural's Naive Bayes implementation is the most mature of the two. You can use them for tasks like spam detection and sentiment analysis.</p> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1979">There are two fundamental steps involved in using a classifier: training and classification.</p> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1980">The following example takes care of the first step by requiring-up the classifier and training it with data. Naturally, this is only a sample. To do any production tasks you'd want many more training documents (hundreds per class depending on their size).</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">var natural = require('natural'),
classifier = new natural.BayesClassifier();
classifier.addDocument("my unit-tests failed.", 'software');
classifier.addDocument("tried the program, but it was buggy.", 'software');
classifier.addDocument("the drive has a 2TB capacity.", 'hardware');
classifier.addDocument("i need a new power supply.", 'hardware');
classifier.train();
</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1981">By default the classifier will tokenize the corpus and stem it with a PorterStemmer. You can use a LancasterStemmer by passing it in to the BayesClassifier constructor as such:</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">var natural = require('natural'),
    stemmer = natural.LancasterStemmer,
    classifier = new natural.BayesClassifier(stemmer);</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1982">With the classifier trained it can now classify documents via the<em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>classify</em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>method:</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">console.log(classifier.classify('did the tests pass?'));
console.log(classifier.classify('did you buy a new drive?'));</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1983">resulting in the output:</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">software
hardware</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1984">Similarly the classifier can be trained on arrays rather than strings, bypassing tokenization and stemming. This allows the consumer to perform custom tokenization and stemming if any at all. This is especially useful if the corpus is not English.</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">classifier.addDocument(['unit', 'test'], 'software');
classifier.addDocument(['bug', 'program'], 'software');
classifier.addDocument(['drive', 'capacity'], 'hardware');
classifier.addDocument(['power', 'supply'], 'hardware');

classifier.train();
</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1985">It's possible to persist and recall the results of a training via the<em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>save</em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>method:</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">var natural = require('natural'),
classifier = new natural.BayesClassifier();

classifier.addDocument(['unit', 'test'], 'software');
classifier.addDocument(['bug', 'program'], 'software');
classifier.addDocument(['drive', 'capacity'], 'hardware');
classifier.addDocument(['power', 'supply'], 'hardware');

classifier.train();

classifier.save('classifier.json', function(err, classifier) {
    // the classifier is saved to the classifier.json file!
 });
</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1986">The training could then be recalled later with the<em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>load</em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>method:</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">var natural = require('natural'),
    classifier = new natural.BayesClassifier();

natural.BayesClassifier.load('classifier.json', null, function(err, classifier) {
    console.log(classifier.classify('did the tests pass?'));
});</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1987">Note that substituting<em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>LogisticRegressionClassifier</em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>for<em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>BayesClassifier</em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>should generally work as a drop-in replacement.</p> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1988"><strong>n-grams</strong></p> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1989">n-grams are essentially the destructuring of a sentence into overlapping, contiguous lists of<em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>n</em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>size and are useful for building probabilistic language models. In this case the n-grams are composed of words but outside of "natural" or even natural language processing they could be of other countable objects.</p> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1990">Consider the following examples which illustrate the production of trigrams (n-grams of length 3), bigrams (n-grams of length 2), and arbitrary n-grams using the<em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>trigrams</em>,<em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>bigrams</em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>and<em><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>ngrams</em>functions respectively.</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">var NGrams = natural.NGrams;
console.log(NGrams.trigrams('some other words here'));
console.log(NGrams.trigrams(['some',  'other', 'words',  'here']));
</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1991">both of which produce:</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">[ [ 'some', 'other', 'words' ], [ 'other', 'words', 'here' ] ]    
</code></pre> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">console.log(NGrams.bigrams('some words here'));
console.log(NGrams.bigrams(['some',  'words',  'here']));
</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1992">both of which produce:</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">[ [ 'some', 'words' ], [ 'words', 'here' ] ]
</code></pre> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">console.log(NGrams.ngrams('some other words here for you', 4));
</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1993">which output:</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">[ [ 'some', 'other', 'words', 'here' ], [ 'other', 'words', 'here', 'for' ], [ 'words', 'here', 'for', 'you' ] ]
</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1994"><strong>String Distance</strong></p> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1995">"natural" supplies the Dice's coefficient, Levenshtein distance, and Jaro-Winkler distance algorithms for determining string similarity. These algorithms are concerned with orthographic (spelling) similarity, not necessarily phonetics.</p> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1996">Each algorithm produces a number indicating its perception of similarity, but each is determined differently and can even move in opposite directions. For instance, the more dissimilar two strings are the greater the Levenshtein distance, but Jaro-Winkler considers two totally dissimilar strings to have a value of 0 with identical strings having a value of 1.</p> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1997">The following example shows each algorithm's perception of the difference between the words "execution" and "intention".</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">var natural = require('natural');

console.log(natural.JaroWinklerDistance('execution', 'intention'));
console.log(natural.LevenshteinDistance('execution', 'intention'));
console.log(natural.DiceCoefficient('execution', 'intention'));
</code></pre> 
           <p pid="1998"><span style="color: rgb(0, 0, 0); float: none; font-size: medium; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px;">resulting in the output:</span></p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">0.48148148148148145
8
0.375
</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="1999">Now to consider totally identical strings.</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">var natural = require('natural');

console.log(natural.JaroWinklerDistance('same', 'same'));
console.log(natural.LevenshteinDistance('same', 'same'));
console.log(natural.DiceCoefficient('same', 'same'));
</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="2000">which yeilds:</p> 
           <pre><code lang="text/x-java">1
0
1
</code></pre> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="2001"><strong>Conclusion and Roadmap</strong></p> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="2002">Well, that was a summary of a sizable portion of "natural". Many of the algorithms have additional parameters that can be used to tweak their operation and a few modules weren't represented at all, but<a href="https://github.com/NaturalNode/natural/blob/master/README.md" rel="nofollow"><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>the official README</a><span class="Apple-converted-space"></span>can help fill that gap.</p> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="2003">There's still plenty in store for "natural". While the current plan is certainly not limited to the following points, these are indeed slated for at least some kind of attention by fall 2012.</p> 
           <ul style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium"> 
            <li>Non-English-specific stemming algorithms</li> 
            <li>Pure javascript version</li> 
            <li>Maximum entropy classifier</li> 
            <li>Clustering algorithms (k-means in development)</li> 
            <li>Part of speech tagging</li> 
            <li>Punkt sentence segmentation</li> 
           </ul> 
           <p style="color: #000000; font-family: &amp;apos;Times New Roman&amp;apos;; font-style: normal; font-variant: normal; font-weight: normal; letter-spacing: normal; line-height: normal; orphans: 2; text-indent: 0px; text-transform: none; white-space: normal; widows: 2; word-spacing: 0px; font-size: medium" pid="2004">With the exception of k-means, which is near completion, I'd love community help on nearly every one!                     article.isLimited = true;
                    TH$Dialog.success('You have limited comments for this Article. Now all comments will go through moderation.');
                } else {
                    TH$Dialog.error('error', 'Your requested was denied')
                }
            });
