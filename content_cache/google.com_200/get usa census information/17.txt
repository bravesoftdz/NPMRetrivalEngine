        <h2 id="item-213707">Equifax Data Breach</h2>
       </header> 
       <div> 
        <span class="fea-img-cont col-md-6 col-sm-12"> 
         <figure class="fea-cntntmddl-img"> 
          <a href=""> <img class="fea-img" src="//gsa-cmp-fileupload.s3.amazonaws.com/Topic_Equifax.jpg" alt=""> </a> 
         </figure> </span> 
        <p>Equifax, one of the three major credit reporting agencies in the U.S., announced a data breach that affects 143 million consumers. The hackers accessed Social Security numbers, birthdates, addresses, and driver’s license numbers.</p>
        <p>Equifax has launched a tool that will let you know if you were affected by the breach. Visit<a href="https://www.equifaxsecurity2017.com/potential-impact/"> Equifax’s website dedicated to this breach</a> to learn if you were impacted. You will need to provide your last name and the last six numbers of your Social Security number.</p>
        <p>If you are impacted, Equifax offers you a free credit monitoring service, TrustedIDPremier. However, you won’t be able to enroll in it immediately. You will be given a date when you can return to the site to enroll. Equifax will not send you a reminder to enroll. Mark that date on your calendar, so you can start monitoring your credit as soon as possible.</p>
        <p>If you detect suspicious activity on your credit report due to the breach, <a href="https://www.consumerfinance.gov/ask-cfpb/how-do-i-dispute-an-error-on-my-credit-report-en-314/">learn how to report it</a> immediately.</p>
        <p>The FTC also offers more information to <a href="https://www.consumer.ftc.gov/blog/2016/09/data-breaches-and-you-new-video">protect yourself after a data breach</a>. Learn how to report and recover from identity theft at <a href="http://www.identitytheft.gov/">IdentityTheft.gov</a>.</p>

       <header>
        <h2 id="item-37207">Telephone Scams</h2>
       </header> 
       <p><a href="https://www.consumer.ftc.gov/articles/0076-phone-scams">Telephone scammers</a> try to trick you out of money or get access to your personal information. Scams may come through phone calls from real people, robocalls, or text messages. The callers often make false promises, such as opportunities to buy products, invest your money, or receive free product trials. They may also offer you money through free grants and lotteries. Some scammers may call with threats of jail or lawsuits if you don’t pay them.</p>
       <h3>Report Telephone Scams</h3>
       <p>Reporting scams to federal agencies helps them collect evidence for lawsuits against people committing these scams. However, federal agencies don’t investigate individual cases of telephone scams.</p>
       <ul>
        <li><p><a href="https://www.ftccomplaintassistant.gov/#&amp;panel1-1">Report telephone scams to the Federal Trade Commission</a>, either online or by phone at 1-877-382-4357. This is the primary government agency that collect scam complaints.</p></li>
        <li><p><a href="https://consumercomplaints.fcc.gov/hc/en-us">Report all robocalls and unwanted telemarketing calls</a> to the Do Not Call Registry.</p></li>
        <li><p><a href="https://consumercomplaints.fcc.gov/hc/en-us">Report caller ID spoofing</a> to the Federal Communications Commission either online or by phone at 1-888-225-5322.</p></li>
       </ul>
       <p>Also report the scam to <a href="https://www.usa.gov/state-consumer">your state consumer protection office</a>. Some consumer protection offices help residents resolve consumer problems.&nbsp;</p>
       <h3>How to Protect Yourself</h3>
       <p>Remember these tips to avoid being a victim of a telephone scam:</p>
       <h4>Do</h4>
       <ul>
        <li><p>Register your phone number with the <a href="https://www.donotcall.gov/">National Do Not Call Registry</a>. You may <a href="https://www.donotcall.gov/register/reg.aspx">register online</a> or by calling 1-888-382-1222. If you still receive telemarketing calls after registering, there’s a good chance that the calls are scams.</p></li>
        <li><p>Be wary of callers claiming that you’ve won a prize or vacation package.</p></li>
        <li><p>Hang up on suspicious phone calls.</p></li>
        <li><p>Be cautious of caller ID. Scammers can change the phone number that shows up on your caller ID screen. <a href="https://www.consumer.ftc.gov/blog/2016/05/scammers-can-fake-caller-id-info">This is called “spoofing.”</a></p></li>
        <li><p>Research business opportunities, charities, or travel packages separately from the information the caller has provided.</p></li>
       </ul>
       <h4>Don’t</h4>
       <ul>
        <li><p>Don’t give in to pressure to take immediate action.</p></li>
        <li><p>Don’t say anything if a caller starts the call asking, “Can you hear me?” This is a common tactic for scammers to record you saying “yes.” Scammers record your “yes” response to use as proof that you agreed to a purchase or credit card charge.</p></li>
        <li><p>Don’t provide your credit card number, bank account information, or other personal information to a caller.</p></li>
        <li><p>Don’t send money if the caller tells you to wire money or pay with a prepaid debit card.</p></li>
       </ul>

       <header>
        <h2 id="item-36617">Banking Scams</h2>
       </header> 
       <p>Banking scams involve attempts to access your bank account. Some popular banking scams include:</p>
       <ul>
        <li><p><a href="http://www.consumer.ftc.gov/articles/0159-fake-checks">Overpayment scams</a> - &nbsp;A scam artist sends you a counterfeit check. They tell you to deposit it in your bank account, and wire a portion of the money back to them. Since the check was fake, you’ll have to pay your bank the amount of the check, plus you’ll lose any money you wired.</p></li>
        <li><p><a href="http://consumer.georgia.gov/consumer-topics/unsolicited-checks">Unsolicited check fraud</a> - A scammer sends you a check for no reason. If you cash it, you may be authorizing the purchase of items or signing up for a loan you didn’t ask for.</p></li>
        <li><p><a href="http://www.helpwithmybank.gov/get-answers/bank-accounts/automatic-withdrawals/bank-accounts-auto-preauth-quesindx.html">Automatic withdrawals</a> - A company sets up an automatic debit from your bank account, as part of a free trial or to collect lottery winnings.</p></li>
        <li><p><a href="https://www.consumer.ftc.gov/articles/0003-phishing">Phishing</a> - You receive an email message that asks you to verify your bank account or debit card number.</p></li>
       </ul>
       <h3>Report Banking Scams</h3>
       <p>The proper organization to report a banking scam to depends on which type you were a victim of.</p>
       <ul>
        <li><p><a href="http://ehome.uspis.gov/fcsexternal/default.aspx">Report fake checks you receive by mail</a> to the US Postal Inspection Service.</p></li>
        <li><p><a href="https://www.ftccomplaintassistant.gov">Report counterfeit checks to the Federal Trade Commission</a>, either online or by phone at 1-877-382-4357.</p></li>
        <li><p>Contact your bank to report and stop unauthorized automatic withdrawals from your account. &nbsp;</p></li>
        <li><p>Forward phishing email to the Federal Trade Commission at <a href="mailto:spam@uce.gov">spam@uce.gov</a>.</p></li>
       </ul>
       <h3>How to Protect Yourself</h3>
       <p>Remember these tips to avoid being a victim of a banking scam:</p>
       <h4>Do</h4>
       <ul>
        <li><p>Be suspicious if you are told to wire a portion of funds from a check back to a company.</p></li>
        <li><p>Be wary of lotteries or free trials that ask for your bank account number.</p></li>
        <li><p>Verify the authenticity of a cashier’s check with the bank that it is drawn on before depositing a check.</p></li>
        <li><p>When verifying a check or the issuer, use contact information on a bank’s website.</p></li>
       </ul>
       <h4>Don’t</h4>
       <ul>
        <li><p>Don’t be fooled by the appearance of checks or money orders. Scammers can make them look legitimate and official.</p></li>
        <li><p>Don’t deposit checks or money orders from strangers or companies you don’t have a relationship with.</p></li>
        <li><p>Don’t wire money to people or companies you don’t know.</p></li>
        <li><p>Don’t give your bank account number to someone who calls you, even for verification purposes.</p></li>
        <li><p>Don’t click on links in email to verify your bank account.</p></li>
        <li><p>Don’t accept a check that includes an overpayment.</p></li>
       </ul>

        <h2 id="item-213469">IRS Imposter Scams</h2>
       </header> 
       <p><a href="https://www.consumer.ftc.gov/articles/0519-irs-imposter-scams-infographic">IRS imposter scams</a> occur when someone contacts you, pretending to work for the IRS. The imposter may contact you by phone, email, postal mail, or even a text message. There are two common types of scams:</p>
       <ul>
        <li><p><strong>Tax collection</strong> - You receive a phone call or letter, claiming that you owe taxes. They will demand that you pay the amount immediately, often with a prepaid debit card or wire transfer. They may even threaten to arrest you if you don’t pay.</p></li>
        <li><p><strong>Verification</strong> - You receive an email or text message that requires you to verify your personal information. The message often includes a hyperlink phrase “click here” or a button to a fraudulent form or website.</p></li>
       </ul>
       <h3>Report IRS Imposter Scams</h3>
       <p>Contact the Treasury Inspector General for Tax Administration (TIGTA) if you believe that an IRS imposter has contacted you. <a href="https://www.treasury.gov/tigta/contact_report_scam.shtml">Report IRS imposter scams online</a> or by calling 1-800-366-4484. Forward email messages that claim to be from the IRS to <a href="mailto:phishing@irs.gov">phishing@irs.gov</a>.</p>
       <p>You can also report IRS imposter scams to the <a href="https://www.ftccomplaintassistant.gov/#crnt&amp;panel1-1">Federal Trade Commission (FTC) online</a> or by calling 1-877-FTC-HELP (1-877-382-4357).</p>
       <h3>How to Protect Yourself</h3>
       <p>There are things to look out for to prevent being a victim of an IRS imposter scam.</p>
       <h4>Do:</h4>
       <ul>
        <li><p>Beware if someone calls, claiming to be from the IRS. The IRS will always contact you by mail before calling you about unpaid taxes.</p></li>
        <li><p>Ask a caller to provide their name and badge number, and callback number. Then call TIGTA at 1-800-366-4484 to determine if the caller is an IRS employee with a legitimate need to contact you. If the person legitimately is from the IRS, call them back. Otherwise <a href="https://www.treasury.gov/tigta/contact_report_scam.shtml">report it to the IRS</a>.</p></li>
        <li><p>Become familiar with what fraudulent IRS email messages look like. <a href="https://www.irs.gov/newsroom/dont-take-the-bait-step-1-avoid-spear-phishing-emails">Review a sample IRS phishing email</a>.</p></li>
        <li><p><a href="https://www.irs.gov/individuals/understanding-your-irs-notice-or-letter">Verify the number</a> of the letter, form, or notice on the IRS website.</p></li>
        <li><p>Be suspicious of threats. The IRS won’t threaten to have police arrest you for not paying a bill.</p></li>
       </ul>
       <h4>Don’t:</h4>
       <ul>
        <li><p>Don’t give in to demands to pay money immediately. Be especially suspicious of demands to wire money or pay with a prepaid debit card.</p></li>
        <li><p>Don’t trust the name or phone number on a caller ID display that shows “IRS.” <a href="https://www.consumer.ftc.gov/blog/scammers-can-fake-caller-id-info">Scammers often change the name</a> that shows on caller ID.</p></li>
        <li><p>Don’t click on any links in email or text messages to verify your information.</p></li>
       </ul>

        <h2 id="item-211606">Charity Scams</h2>
       </header> 
       <p>Some scammers set up fake organizations, taking advantage of the public’s generosity, especially after a tragedy or disaster.</p>
       <h3>Report Charity Scams</h3>
       <ul>
        <li><p>Your <a href="https://www.usa.gov/state-consumer">state consumer protection office</a> can accept and investigate consumer complaints.</p></li>
        <li><p>File a complaint with the <a href="https://www.ftccomplaintassistant.gov/#nb">Federal Trade Commission</a> (FTC). While the FTC does not resolve individual matters, it tracks charity fraud claims and sues companies on the behalf of consumers.</p></li>
        <li><p>If the suspected fraud is related to a natural disaster, contact the <a href="https://www.justice.gov/criminal-disasters/how-report-disaster-related-fraud">National Center for Disaster Fraud</a>.</p></li>
       </ul>

       <h3>How to Protect Yourself</h3>
       <p><a href="http://give.org/for-donors/about-specific-giving-guidance">Follow these tips</a> to help you detect common <a href="https://www.consumer.ftc.gov/articles/0074-giving-charity">charity scam tactics</a>:</p>
       <h4>Do</h4>
       <ul>

        <li><p>Verify the name. Fake charities often choose names that are similar to well established charities or use keywords that elicit sympathy, such as “children,” “cancer,” or “disaster relief.”</p></li>
       </ul>
       <h4>Don’t</h4>
       <ul>
        <li><p>Don’t give in to high pressure tactics such as urging you to donate immediately.</p></li>
        <li><p>Don’t assume that you can get a tax deduction for donating to an organization. Use the <a href="http://apps.irs.gov/app/eos/">IRS’s database of 501(c)3 organizations</a> to find out if it has this status.</p></li>
        <li><p>Don’t send cash. Pay with a check or credit card.</p></li>
       </ul>

        <h2 id="item-212864">Ticket Scams</h2>
       </header> 
       <p>Ticket selling scams often happen when tickets for popular concerts, plays, special venues, and sporting events sell out quickly but there is a still a very high demand for tickets. Scammers including individuals and fake resale ticket sellers will take advantage of the situation by:</p>
       <ul>
        <li>Charging prices much higher than the face value of a ticket</li>
        <li>Creating counterfeit tickets with forged barcodes and logos of real ticket companies</li>
        <li>Selling duplicates of a legitimate ticket in the form of electronic tickets that they email to multiple buyers</li>
        <li>Posing as sellers of tickets that are stolen and telling you a hard luck story about why they can’t go to the event at the last minute</li>
        <li>Pretending to sell tickets online for the purpose of stealing your identity or credit card information</li>
       </ul>
       <p>&nbsp;</p>
       <h3>Report ticket scams</h3>
       <p>There are several options to report a ticket scam.</p>
       <ul>
        <li>Contact your <a href="https://www.usa.gov/state-consumer">state consumer protection office</a>.</li>
        <li>Contact the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) using the <a href="https://www.ftccomplaintassistant.gov/#crnt&amp;panel1-1">Online Complaint Assistant</a>.</li>
        <li>File a local police report especially if you met the scammer in person and have a picture to give to law enforcement.</li>
        <li>File a complaint about a ticket company using the <a href="https://www.bbb.org/scamtracker">Better Business Bureau’s Scam Tracker</a>.</li>
        <li>If you paid by credit card, report the problem to the card company. You may be able to dispute the ticket purchase.</li>
       </ul>
       <h3><span style="font-size:18px">How to protect yourself</span></h3>
       <p>There are steps you can take so you are not a victim:</p>
       <h4>Do</h4>
       <ul>
        <li>Buy tickets at the venue box office.</li>
        <li>Buy tickets from authorized brokers and third party sellers who have legitimate physical addresses and phone numbers that you can contact to guarantee the validity of the tickets.</li>
        <li>Check the actual web address of the resale ticket seller; some scammers will create phony websites that closely resemble authentic ticket company websites.</li>
        <li>Search for the seller’s name, email address, and phone number online, along with the words “fraud,” “scams,” and “fake tickets” to see if there are negative reviews.</li>
        <li>Look at the tickets before you buy and verify the date and the time printed on them.</li>
        <li>Make sure the section and seat numbers on the tickets actually exist at the venue.</li>
        <li>Get contact information for the seller, and have the seller meet you in person in a public place for the ticket exchange.</li>
        <li>Ask the seller for their proof of purchase of the tickets if you are buying from an individual.</li>
        <li>Use a credit card to pay third party sellers and make sure the URL for the website has an “s” in the https portion of the address link so your credit card information will be secure.</li>
        <li>Look for any complaints filed against a ticket company by checking with your state’s <a href="https://www.usa.gov/state-consumer">consumer protection agency</a>.</li>
       </ul>
       <h4>Don’t</h4>
       <ul>
        <li>Don’t wire transfer money to pay for tickets. &nbsp;</li>
        <li>Don’t trust sellers who want you to pay with a prepaid money card.</li>
        <li>Don’t pay before seeing the tickets</li>
        <li>Don’t meet an individual ticket seller alone or in a low-traffic area.</li>
        <li>Don’t be fooled by online search results.The first ones at the top of the search may be paid ads for ticket services that charge excessive fees, rather than reputable sellers.</li>
       </ul>

        <h2 id="item-35527">Lottery and Sweepstakes Scams</h2>
       </header> 
       <p><a href="https://www.consumer.ftc.gov/articles/0199-prize-scams">Prize scammers</a> try to get your money or personal information through fake lotteries, sweepstakes, or other contests. Many claim that you’ve won a prize but must pay a fee to collect it. Others require you to provide personal information to enter a “contest.” These scams may reach you by postal mail, email, phone call, robocall, or text message.</p>
       <h3>Report Lottery and Sweepstakes Scams</h3>
       <p>To report a prize scam:</p>
       <ul>
        <li><p><a href="https://www.ftccomplaintassistant.gov/#&amp;panel1-1">Contact the Federal Trade Commission</a> online or by phone at 1-877-382-4357.</p></li>
        <li><p><a href="https://postalinspectors.uspis.gov/aboutus/contact.aspx">Contact a postal inspector</a> if the scam uses U.S. mail to further its scheme. It doesn’t matter if the scam notice arrived by phone or email.</p></li>
        <li><p><a href="https://consumercomplaints.fcc.gov/hc/en-us">Report robocalls and unwanted telemarketing calls to the Do Not Call Registry</a>.</p></li>
       </ul>
       <p>Federal agencies investigate scams and pursue criminal charges against the scammers. They don’t, however, investigate individual cases. <a href="https://www.usa.gov/state-consumer">State consumer protection offices</a> do sometimes pursue individual cases as well as investigate scams.</p>
       <h3>How to Protect Yourself</h3>
       <p>Remember these tips to avoid being a victim of a lottery or sweepstakes scam:</p>
       <h4>Do</h4>
       <ul>
        <li><p>Check the postage on a mailed prize notice. If it was sent bulk rate, it’s probably a scam.</p></li>
        <li><p>Try to remember if you entered a particular contest. If you don’t remember entering it, you probably didn’t, and the prize notice is a fake.</p></li>
        <li><p>Contact the actual company to verify a prize notice from an organization known to run a real sweepstakes.</p></li>
        <li><p>Register your phone number with the <a href="https://www.donotcall.gov/">National Do Not Call Registry</a>. You may <a href="https://www.donotcall.gov/register/reg.aspx">register online</a> or by calling 1-888-382-1222. If you receive telemarketing calls after registering, there’s a good chance that the calls are scams.</p></li>
        <li><p>Report <a href="https://www.consumer.ftc.gov/articles/0350-text-message-spam">spam text</a> messages to your mobile carrier, then delete them.</p></li>
        <li><p>Hang up on suspicious calls.</p></li>
       </ul>
       <h4>Don’t</h4>
       <ul>
        <li><p>Don’t pay a fee, taxes, or shipping charges to receive a prize.</p></li>
        <li><p>Don’t wire money to, or deposit a check from, any organization claiming to run a sweepstakes or lottery.</p></li>
        <li><p>Don’t provide your credit card number, bank account information, or other personal information.</p></li>
        <li><p>Don’t believe anyone who says they’re from the government or an official-sounding organization.</p></li>
        <li><p>Don’t reply to, or click on any links in, a <a href="https://www.consumer.ftc.gov/articles/0350-text-message-spam">spam text message</a>.</p></li>
        <li><p>Don’t attend a sales meeting to be eligible to win a prize.</p></li>
        <li><p>Don’t give in to pressure to take immediate action.</p></li>
        <li><p>Don’t believe anyone claiming to be from a foreign lottery or sweepstakes. It’s <a href="https://archives.fbi.gov/archives/news/stories/2006/august/lotto_scams080906">illegal to enter foreign contests</a> like these.</p></li>
       </ul>

       <header>
        <h2 id="item-37297">Pyramid Schemes</h2>
       </header> 
       <p><a href="https://www.investor.gov/investing-basics/avoiding-fraud/types-fraud/pyramid-scheme">Pyramid schemes</a> are scams that require a constant flow of new participants to keep them going. They masquerade as <a href="https://www.consumer.ftc.gov/articles/0065-multilevel-marketing">multi-level marketing programs</a> or other types of legitimate businesses. They use new recruits’ required payments to provide “profits” to those participating longer.</p>
       <p>Pyramid schemes collapse when they run short of new recruits needed to pay earlier investors. These scams always fail—<a href="https://www.ftc.gov/public-statements/1998/05/pyramid-schemes#N_3_">it’s mathematically guaranteed.</a></p>
       <h3>Report Pyramid Schemes</h3>
       <p>Report pyramid schemes to:</p>
       <ul>
        <li><p>Your <a href="https://www.usa.gov/state-consumer">state consumer protection office</a></p></li>
        <li><p>Your <a href="http://www.naag.org/naag/attorneys-general/whos-my-ag.php">state attorney general</a></p></li>
        <li><p>The <a href="http://www.bbb.org/">Better Business Bureau (BBB)</a></p></li>
       </ul>
       <h3>How to Protect Yourself</h3>
       <p>Keep these tips in mind to avoid falling for a pyramid scheme:</p>
       <h3><span style="font-size:16px">Do</span></h3>
       <ul>
        <li><p>Be wary of "business opportunities" that require you to recruit more participants to increase your profit, or recoup your initial investment.</p></li>
        <li><p>Be wary if the company sells non-tangible products or technical services, rather than physical items.</p></li>
        <li><p>Independently verify the legitimacy of any business with the <a href="http://www.bbb.org/">Better Business Bureau</a>, your <a href="http://www.naag.org/naag/attorneys-general/whos-my-ag.php">state attorney general</a>, or any licensing agencies.</p></li>
        <li><p>Ask to see documents, such as financial statements audited by a certified public accountant (CPA), showing that the company generates revenue from selling its products or services to people outside the program.</p></li>
        <li><p>Be skeptical of success stories and testimonials of fantastic earnings.</p></li>
       </ul>
       <h4>Don’t</h4>
       <ul>
        <li><p>Don’t invest until you’ve verified that the business is legitimate.</p></li>
        <li><p>Don’t get involved in businesses that require you to recruit new participants.</p></li>
        <li><p>Don’t buy into franchises that guarantee big profits quickly.</p></li>
        <li><p>Don’t invest in any “opportunity” bearing <a href="https://www.investor.gov/additional-resources/news-alerts/alerts-bulletins/investor-alert-beware-pyramid-schemes-posing-multi">warning signs of a pyramid scheme</a>.</p></li>
       </ul>

       <header>
        <h2 id="item-37147">Tax-Related Identity Theft</h2>
       </header> 
       <p>Tax-related identity theft occurs when someone uses your stolen Social Security Number (SSN) to get a tax refund or a job. These tips can help you prevent and report tax identity theft:</p>
       <h3>Warning Signs&nbsp;</h3>
       <p>To prevent tax identity theft, be wary of any Internal Revenue Service (IRS) letter or notice that states:</p>
       <ul>
        <li>More than one tax return was filed using your SSN</li>
        <li>You owe additional tax, you have had a tax refund offset, or you have had collection actions taken against you for a year you did not file a tax return.</li>
        <li>IRS records indicate you received wages from an employer unknown to you</li>
       </ul>
       <p>The IRS does not initiate contact with a taxpayer by sending an email, text, or social message requesting personal or financial information.</p>
       <ul>
        <li>Should you get an email that claims to be from the IRS, do not reply or click on any links. Instead, you should <a href="http://www.irs.gov/uac/Report-Phishing">report it to the IRS</a>.</li>
        <li>The United States Computer Emergency Readiness Team (US-CERT) provides alerts and tips on how you can protect yourself against <a href="https://www.us-cert.gov/ncas/current-activity/2014/02/26/US-Tax-Season-Phishing-Scams-and-Malware-Campaigns">U.S. tax season phishing scams and malware campaigns</a>.</li>
       </ul>
       <h3>Dealing with Tax-Related Identity Theft</h3>
       <p>If you suspect someone used your Social Security Number (SSN) for a tax refund or a job—or the IRS sends you a letter or notice indicating a problem—take these steps:&nbsp;</p>
       <ul>
        <li><a href="https://www.identitytheft.gov/Assistant">File a report</a>&nbsp;with the Federal Trade Commission (FTC). You can also&nbsp;call&nbsp;the FTC Identity Theft Hotline at 1-877-438-4338 or TTY 1-866-653-4261.</li>
        <li>Contact one of the three major credit bureaus to place a fraud alert on your credit records:
         <ul>
          <li><a href="http://www.Equifax.com">Equifax:</a>&nbsp;1-888-766-0008</li>
          <li><a href="http://www.Experian.com">Experian:</a>&nbsp;1-888-397-3742</li>
          <li><a href="http://www.TransUnion.com">TransUnion:</a>&nbsp;1-800-680-7289</li>
         </ul></li>
        <li>Contact your financial institutions, and close any accounts opened without your permission or tampered with.</li>
        <li>Respond immediately to any IRS notice; call the number provided. If instructed, go to the <a href="https://idverify.irs.gov/IE/e-authenticate/welcome.do">Identity Verification Service</a>.</li>
        <li>Complete <a href="http://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-pdf/f14039.pdf">IRS Form 14039, Identity Theft Affidavit</a>; print, then mail or fax according to instructions.</li>
        <li>Continue to pay your taxes and file your tax return, even if you must do so by paper.</li>
       </ul> 
       <p class="volver clearfix"><a href="#skiptarget"><span class="icon-backtotop-dwnlvl">Back to Top</span></a></p> 
      </article> 
      <article> 
       <header>
        <h2 id="item-213708">Investment Scams</h2>
       </header> 
       <p><a href="https://investor.gov/protect-your-investments/fraud/types-fraud#.VA32ZsVSHPR">Investment scams</a> prey on your hope to earn high returns on a regular basis, without financial risk.&nbsp;</p>
       <h3>Report Investment Scams</h3>
       <p>Report investment scams, if you have been a victim.</p>
       <ul>
        <li><p>File a complaint about an investment or an investment account with the <a href="https://www.sec.gov/oiea/Complaint.html">Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC)</a>.</p></li>
        <li><p>Report pyramid or Ponzi schemes to the <a href="https://www.ftccomplaintassistant.gov/">Federal Trade Commission (FTC)</a>.</p></li>
        <li><p>Report investment scams by companies that are licensed in your state to <a href="http://www.nasaa.org/about-us/contact-us/contact-your-regulator/">your state's securities administrator</a>.</p></li>
       </ul>
       <p>The SEC may forward your complaint to the investment company and request that the company reply. The FTC will not research your individual case of investment fraud.</p>
       <h3>How to Protect Yourself</h3>
       <p>Remember these tips to avoid being a victim of an investment scam:</p>
       <h4>Do</h4>
       <ul>
        <li><p>Research investment opportunities and investment professionals with your <a href="http://www.nasaa.org/about-us/contact-us/contact-your-regulator">state securities regulator</a> and the <a href="https://brokercheck.finra.org">Financial Industry Regulatory Authority</a>.</p></li>
        <li><p>Learn where the investment and the investment professional are registered, whether in your state or with other regulators.</p></li>
        <li><p>Get all the details of an investment in writing, but still do your own research.</p></li>
        <li><p>Ask questions about costs, timing, risks, and other issues.</p></li>
       </ul>
       <h4>Don’t</h4>
       <ul>
        <li><p>Don’t be pressured to invest immediately.</p></li>
        <li><p>Don’t be influenced by promises that seem too good to be true, such as “guaranteed earnings” or “risk-free” investments</p></li>
        <li><p>Don’t be swayed to invest in something because the investment professional is likable, seems trustworthy, or has credentials and professional titles.</p></li>
        <li><p><a href="http://www.sec.gov/investor/pubs/affinity.htm">Don’t feel pressured to invest</a> because you were told that many other people with similar financial circumstances have invested.</p></li>
        <li><p>Don’t feel obligated to invest because the professional gave you a free gift, lunch, reduced commission fees.</p></li>
       </ul> 
       <p class="volver clearfix"><a href="#skiptarget"><span class="icon-backtotop-dwnlvl">Back to Top</span></a></p> 
      </article> 
      <article> 
       <header>
        <h2 id="item-35227">Census Related Fraud</h2>
       </header> 
       <p>The U.S. Census Bureau is the federal agency responsible for collecting data about the people and economy of the United States. It must collect some personal and demographic information from people and businesses to do this research.</p>
       <p>Some scam artists may act as if they work for the U.S. Census Bureau to collect personal information about you to use for fraud, including stealing your identity. These scam artists may send you letters that seem like official letters from the U.S. Census Bureau, or they may come to your home to try to collect information about you.</p>
       <h3>Report Census Related Fraud</h3>
       <p>If you suspect fraud, contact the Census Bureau’s <a href="https://www.census.gov/about/regions.html">regional office</a> for your state or the Census Bureau’s <a href="https://www.census.gov/about/npc.html">National Processing Center</a> immediately for verification and further guidance.</p>
       <h3>How to Protect Yourself</h3>
       <p>Follow these tips to ensure that your personal information stays safe:</p>
       <h4>Do</h4>
       <ul>
        <li><p>If you are visited at home by someone claiming to be a census worker, request to see the person’s badge. The badge should have the photograph of field representative, a Department of Commerce watermark, and an expiration date. Here are some other ways to <a href="https://www.census.gov/programs-surveys/are-you-in-a-survey/identify/census-field-representative.html">identify a legitimate census worker</a>.</p></li>
        <li><p>If you get a call, postal mail, or email that seems to be from the Census Bureau, follow these <a href="http://www.census.gov/programs-surveys/are-you-in-a-survey/fraudulent-activity-and-scams.html">tips to help you spot census scams</a>, so you don’t become a victim.</p></li>
       </ul>
       <h4>Don’t</h4>
       <ul>
        <li><p>Don’t provide information if someone claiming to be a census worker asks for your full Social Security number, bank or credit card account numbers, or your mother’s maiden name. The Census Bureau does not ask for these.</p></li>
       </ul> 
       <p class="volver clearfix"><a href="#skiptarget"><span class="icon-backtotop-dwnlvl">Back to Top</span></a></p> 
      </article> 
      <article> 
       <header>
        <h2 id="item-213719">Ponzi Schemes</h2>
       </header> 
       <p>A <a href="https://www.investor.gov/protect-your-investments/fraud/types-fraud/ponzi-scheme">Ponzi scheme</a> is a type of investment fraud that relies on money from new investors to pay “returns” to current investors. To keep the scam going, the scheme organizers must continually attract new investors and discourage current investors from cashing out. When they can’t, the scheme collapses.</p>
       <h3>Report Ponzi Schemes</h3>
       <p>Report Ponzi schemes to:</p>
       <ul>
        <li><p>The <a href="http://www.sec.gov/complaint/select.shtml">Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC)</a></p></li>
        <li><p>The <a href="http://www.finra.org/investors/investor-complaint-center">Financial Industry Regulatory Authority</a></p></li>
        <li><p>Your <a href="https://www.usa.gov/state-consumer">state's securities administrator</a></p></li>
       </ul>
       <h3>How to Protect Yourself</h3>
       <p>Keep these tips in mind to protect yourself from Ponzi schemes:</p>
       <h4><span style="font-size:16px">Do</span></h4>
       <ul>
        <li><p>Be wary of any investment that regularly pays positive returns regardless of what the overall market is doing.</p></li>
        <li><p>Avoid investments if you don’t understand them or can’t get complete information about them.</p></li>
        <li><p>Be alert to account statement errors, which may be a sign of investment fraud.</p></li>
        <li><p>Be suspicious if you don’t receive a payment or have difficulty cashing out. &nbsp;</p></li>
       </ul>
       <h4><span style="font-size:16px">Don’t</span></h4>
       <ul>
        <li><p>Don’t put your money in investments that promise big returns with little to no risk.</p></li>
        <li><p>Don’t contribute to any investment that isn’t registered with the SEC or with state regulators.</p></li>
        <li><p>Don’t get financially involved with any unlicensed investment professional or unregistered firm.</p></li>
       </ul>
