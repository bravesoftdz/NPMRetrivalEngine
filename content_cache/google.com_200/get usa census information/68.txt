           <p>Tens of millions. Millions. Hundreds of&nbsp;thousands. </p>
           <p>When trying to estimate the number of businesses in the <span class="caps">US</span>, the numbers flow thick and fast. Since our product tracks deep intelligence on <span class="caps">US</span> companies, we wanted to to know the right answer. Fortunately the <a target="_blank" href="http://census.gov/econ/susb/"><span class="caps">US</span> Census</a> and <a target="_blank" href="https://www.irs.gov/uac/tax-stats-2"><span class="caps">IRS</span> have</a> detailed data on <span class="caps">US</span> business - number of firms, branch offices, payroll, revenue etc. But it’s a lot of data. We analyzed the numbers and have condensed it into seven charts. Here are our&nbsp;findings.</p>
           <h3>Millions yes,&nbsp;but…</h3>
           <p>Overall, the <span class="caps">US</span> has <strong>29.5M</strong> employers. That’s a large number when you consider the <a target="_blank" href="http://www.census.gov/popclock/"><span class="caps">US</span> population</a> is 312M, nearly 1 employer for every 10 people. But there’s a catch. Around 23.8M of these businesses are considered “non employers” - they have no paid employees and their average revenue is around $46,000/year. These micro SMBs range from from family-run corner stores to home-based&nbsp;bloggers.</p>
           <p>Given the limited impact of these businesses on the overall <span class="caps">B2B</span> market, the actual number of <span class="caps">B2B</span> enterprises is <strong>5.72M.</strong> The charts below summarize the demographics of these&nbsp;businesses.</p>
           <h3>In sheer numbers, smaller firms&nbsp;dominate</h3>
           <p>Small firms, companies with less than 10 employees or under $1M in annual revenue, constitute the bulk of the&nbsp;businesses. </p>
           <p>Over 96 percent of companies are SMBs - with less than 50 employees; of these most of the companies are small SMBs, with less than 10&nbsp;employees.</p>

So it’s not surprising that the spread of companies by annual revenue is very similar to that of employee size. Over 75 percent of companies have less than $1M in annual revenue. On the other end of the spectrum, there are almost 3,000 companies with over a billion dollars in annual turnover - much higher than one might&nbsp;anticipate.</p>

           <h3>But larger enterprises generate most of the&nbsp;revenue</h3>
           <p>When it comes to sales however, the numbers are flipped, almost symmetrically. It’s the <a target="_blank" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pareto_principle">80/20</a> rule, except here, 4 percent of businesses - companies with more than 50 employees - generate almost 80 percent of the total&nbsp;revenue.</p>

           <h3>Local and middle-aged,&nbsp;mostly</h3>
           <p>A look into the number of office locations shows that companies with less than 100 employees have 1-2 offices, with the number steadily increasing as the employee count grows. However when companies graduate to the larger bucket, they seem to grow&nbsp;exponentially.</p>
