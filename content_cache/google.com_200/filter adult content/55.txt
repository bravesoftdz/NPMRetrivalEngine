      <p><b>Ad blocking</b> or <b>ad filtering</b> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">software</a> (or less commonly, a <a href="/wiki/Hardware_(computing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hardware (computing)">computer hardware device</a>), that can remove or alter <a href="/wiki/Advertisement" class="mw-redirect" title="Advertisement">advertising</a> content from a <a href="/wiki/Webpage" class="mw-redirect" title="Webpage">webpage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Website" title="Website">website</a>, or a <a href="/wiki/Mobile_app" title="Mobile app">mobile app</a>. Ad blockers are available for a range of computer platforms, including <a href="/wiki/Desktop_computer" title="Desktop computer">desktop</a> and <a href="/wiki/Laptop" title="Laptop">laptop</a> computers, <a href="/wiki/Tablet_computer" title="Tablet computer">tablet computers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Smartphone" title="Smartphone">smartphones</a>. A variety of methods have been used for blocking advertisements.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The benefits of this software are wide-ranging and the use of ad blocking software is increasing.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> However, many media owners (publishers) rely on advertising to fund the free content that they provide to users. Some have taken counter-measures against users who block ads on the sites they visit, for example Forbes.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technologies_and_native_countermeasures">Technologies and native countermeasures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ad_blocking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Technologies and native countermeasures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Online advertising exists in a variety of forms, including <a href="/wiki/Web_banner" title="Web banner">web banners</a>, <a href="/wiki/Image" title="Image">pictures</a>, animations, embedded audio and video, text, or <a href="/wiki/Pop-up_ad" title="Pop-up ad">pop-up windows</a>, and can employ audio and video <a href="/wiki/Autoplay" class="mw-redirect" title="Autoplay">autoplay</a>. All browsers offer some ways to remove or alter advertisements: either by targeting technologies that are used to deliver ads (such as embedded content delivered through <a href="/wiki/Browser_plug-in" class="mw-redirect" title="Browser plug-in">browser plug-ins</a> or via HTML5), targeting <a href="/wiki/URL" title="URL">URLs</a> that are the source of ads, or targeting behaviors characteristic to ads (such as the use of <a href="/wiki/HTML5" title="HTML5">HTML5</a> autoplay of both audio and video).</p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reasons_for_blocking_ads">Reasons for blocking ads</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ad_blocking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reasons for blocking ads">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>From the standpoint of an Internet user, there are various fundamental reasons why one would want to use ad blocking, in addition to not being manipulated by brands:</p> 
      <ul> 
       <li>Protecting their <a href="/wiki/Internet_privacy" title="Internet privacy">privacy</a></li> 
       <li>Protecting themselves from <a href="/wiki/Malvertising" title="Malvertising">malvertising</a></li> 
       <li>Save bandwidth (and money)</li> 
       <li>Better user experience 
        <ul> 
         <li>Less cluttered pages</li> 
         <li>Faster page loading times</li> 
         <li>Fewer distractions</li> 
        </ul> </li> 
       <li>Save battery on mobile devices</li> 
      </ul> 
      <p>Publishers and their representative trade bodies, on the other hand, argue that Internet ads provide revenue to website owners, which enable the website owners to create or otherwise purchase content for the website. Publishers claim that the prevalent use of ad blocking software and devices could adversely affect website owner revenue and thus in turn lower the availability of free content on websites.</p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits">Benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ad_blocking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>To users, the benefits of ad blocking software include quicker loading and cleaner looking web pages with fewer <a href="/wiki/Distraction" title="Distraction">distractions</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-mfit_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mfit-4">[4]</a></sup> lower resource waste (bandwidth, CPU, memory, etc.), and privacy benefits gained through the exclusion of the tracking and profiling systems of ad delivery platforms. Blocking ads can also save substantial amounts of electrical energy and lower users' power bills.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="User_experience">User experience</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ad_blocking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: User experience">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Ad blocking software may have other benefits to users' <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a>, as it decreases Internet users' exposure to advertising and marketing industries, which promote the purchase of numerous consumer products and services that are potentially harmful or unhealthy<sup id="cite_ref-auto1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and on creating the urge to buy immediately.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> With the average person seeing more than 5000 advertisements every day, and with many of these being from online sources,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and with each ad promising viewers that their lives will be improved if they buy what is being promoted<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Fast_food" title="Fast food">fast food</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soda_pop" class="mw-redirect" title="Soda pop">soda pop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Candy" title="Candy">candy</a>, expensive <a href="/wiki/Consumer_electronics" title="Consumer electronics">consumer electronics</a>) or encourage users to get into debt or <a href="/wiki/Gambling" title="Gambling">gamble</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> As well, if Internet users buy all of these items, the packaging and the containers (in the case of candy and soda pop) end up being disposed of, leading to environmental impacts of waste disposal. Advertisements are very carefully crafted to target weaknesses in human psychology;<sup id="cite_ref-auto1_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> as such, a reduction in exposure to advertisements could be beneficial for users' quality of life.</p> 
      <p>Unwanted advertising can also harm the advertisers themselves, if users become annoyed by the ads. Irritated users might make a conscious effort to avoid the goods and services of firms which are using annoying "pop-up" ads which block the Internet content the user is trying to view.<sup id="cite_ref-conferences.sigcomm.org_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-conferences.sigcomm.org-17">[17]</a></sup> For users not interested in making purchases, the blocking of ads can also save time. Any ad that appears on a website exerts a toll on the user's "attention budget", since each ad enters the user's field of view and must either be consciously ignored or closed, or dealt with in some other way. A user who is strongly focused on reading solely the content that they are seeking, likely has no desire to be diverted by advertisements that seek to sell unneeded or unwanted goods and services.<sup id="cite_ref-conferences.sigcomm.org_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-conferences.sigcomm.org-17">[17]</a></sup> In contrast, users who are actively seeking items to purchase, might appreciate advertising, in particular targeted ads.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Security">Security</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ad_blocking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Security">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Another important aspect is improving security; online advertising subjects users to a higher risk of infecting their devices with <a href="/wiki/Computer_virus" title="Computer virus">computer viruses</a> than surfing pornography sites.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In a high-profile case, malware was distributed through advertisements provided to YouTube by a malicious customer of Google's <a href="/wiki/Doubleclick" class="mw-redirect" title="Doubleclick">Doubleclick</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In August 2015, a <a href="/wiki/0-day_exploit" class="mw-redirect" title="0-day exploit">0-day exploit</a> in the Firefox browser was discovered in an advertisement on a website.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> When Forbes required users to disable ad blocking before viewing their website, those users were immediately served with pop-under malware.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Australian_Signals_Directorate" title="Australian Signals Directorate">Australian Signals Directorate</a> recommends individuals and organizations block advertisements to improve their <a href="/wiki/Information_security" title="Information security">information security</a> posture and mitigate potential <a href="/wiki/Malvertising" title="Malvertising">malvertising</a> attacks and machine compromise.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The information security firm <a href="/wiki/Webroot" title="Webroot">Webroot</a> also note employing ad blockers provide effective countermeasures against malvertising campaigns for less technically-sophisticated computer users.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Monetary">Monetary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ad_blocking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Monetary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Ad blocking can also save money for the user. If a user's personal time is worth one dollar per minute, and if unsolicited advertising adds an extra minute to the time that the user requires for reading the webpage (i.e. the user must manually identify the ads as ads, and then click to close them, or use other techniques to either deal with them, all of which tax the user's intellectual focus in some way),<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> then the user has effectively lost one dollar of time in order to deal with ads that might generate a few fractional pennies of display-ad revenue for the website owner. The problem of lost time can rapidly spiral out of control if malware accompanies the ads.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p> 
      <p>Users who pay for total transferred bandwidth ("capped" or pay-for-usage connections) including most mobile users worldwide, have a direct financial benefit from filtering an ad before it is loaded. Streaming audio and video, even if they are not presented to the user interface, can rapidly consume gigabytes of transfer especially on a faster 4G connection. Since users without a data plan often pay by the megabyte, the cost of tolerating ads can be quite high. Even fixed connections are often subject to usage limits, especially the faster connections (100Mbit/s and up) which can quickly saturate a network if filled by streaming media.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It is a known problem with most web browsers, including <a href="/wiki/Mozilla_Firefox" class="mw-redirect" title="Mozilla Firefox">Firefox</a>, that restoring sessions often plays multiple embedded ads at once.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Using an advertisement blocker stops such behaviour, and makes it easier for people to use their computers and their Internet service.</p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popularity">Popularity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ad_blocking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Popularity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

      <p>Use of mobile and desktop ad blocking software designed to remove traditional advertising grew by 41% worldwide and by 48% in the U.S. between Q2 2014 and Q2 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-30">[30]</a></sup> As of Q2 2015, 45 million Americans were using ad blockers.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-30">[30]</a></sup> In a survey research study released Q2 2016, MetaFacts reported 72 million Americans, 12.8 million adults in the UK, and 13.2 million adults in France were using ad blockers on their PCs, smartphones, or tablet computers. In March 2016, the <a href="/wiki/Internet_Advertising_Bureau" title="Internet Advertising Bureau">Internet Advertising Bureau</a> reported that UK adblocking was already at 22% among people over 18 years old.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternatives">Alternatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ad_blocking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Alternatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>As of 2015, advertisers and marketers look to involve their brands directly into the entertainment with <a href="/wiki/Native_advertising" title="Native advertising">native advertising</a> and <a href="/wiki/Product_placement" title="Product placement">product placement</a> (also known as brand integration or embedded marketing).<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> An example of product placement would be for a <a href="/wiki/Soda_pop" class="mw-redirect" title="Soda pop">soda pop</a> manufacturer to pay a <a href="/wiki/Reality_TV" class="mw-redirect" title="Reality TV">reality TV</a> show producer to have the show's cast and host appear onscreen holding cans of the soft drink. Another common product placement is for an <a href="/wiki/Automotive_industry" title="Automotive industry">automotive manufacturer</a> to give free cars to the producers of a <a href="/wiki/TV_show" class="mw-redirect" title="TV show">TV show</a>, in return for the show's producer depicting characters using these vehicles during the show.</p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods">Methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ad_blocking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>One method of filtering is simply to block (or prevent autoplay of) <a href="/wiki/Macromedia_Flash" class="mw-redirect" title="Macromedia Flash">Flash animation</a> or image loading or Windows audio and video files. This can be done in most browsers easily and also improves security and privacy. This crude technological method is refined by numerous <a href="/wiki/Browser_extension" title="Browser extension">browser extensions</a>. Every Internet browser handles this task differently, but, in general, one alters the options, preferences or application extensions to filter specific media types. An additional add-on is usually required to differentiate between ads and non-ads using the same technology, or between wanted and unwanted ads or behaviors. The more advanced ad blocking filter software allow fine-grained control of <a href="/wiki/Online_advertising" title="Online advertising">advertisements</a> through features such as <a href="/wiki/Blacklist" class="mw-redirect" title="Blacklist">blacklists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Whitelist" title="Whitelist">whitelists</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Regular_expression" title="Regular expression">regular expression</a> filters. Certain security features also have the effect of disabling some ads. Some <a href="/wiki/Antivirus_software" title="Antivirus software">antivirus software</a> can act as an ad blocker. Filtering by intermediaries such as <a href="/wiki/ISP" class="mw-redirect" title="ISP">ISP</a> providers or national governments is increasingly common.</p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Browser_integration">Browser integration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ad_blocking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Browser integration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>As of 2015, many web browsers block unsolicited <a href="/wiki/Pop-up_ad" title="Pop-up ad">pop-up ads</a> automatically. Current versions of <a href="/wiki/Konqueror" title="Konqueror">Konqueror</a><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Internet_Explorer" title="Internet Explorer">Internet Explorer</a><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> also include content filtering support. Content filtering can be added to <a href="/wiki/Firefox_browser" class="mw-redirect" title="Firefox browser">Firefox</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chromium_browser" class="mw-redirect" title="Chromium browser">Chromium-based browsers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Opera_browser" class="mw-redirect" title="Opera browser">Opera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Safari_browser" class="mw-redirect" title="Safari browser">Safari</a> and other browsers with extensions such as <a href="/wiki/AdBlock" title="AdBlock">AdBlock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adblock_Plus" title="Adblock Plus">Adblock Plus</a> and <a href="/wiki/UBlock_Origin" title="UBlock Origin">uBlock Origin</a>, and a number of sources provide regularly updated filter lists. Adblock Plus (provided by the German software house Eyeo GmbH) is included in the freeware browser <a href="/wiki/Maxthon" title="Maxthon">Maxthon</a> from the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="People's Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a> by default.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Another method for filtering advertisements uses <a href="/wiki/Cascading_Style_Sheets" title="Cascading Style Sheets">Cascading Style Sheets</a> (CSS) rules to hide specific <a href="/wiki/HTML" title="HTML">HTML</a> and <a href="/wiki/XHTML" title="XHTML">XHTML</a> <a href="/wiki/HTML_element" title="HTML element">elements</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. In 2017, Google has announced their plan for creating an in-built ad blocker for Chrome browser. However, they have confirmed that they will only block certain ads from other companies.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="External_programs">External programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ad_blocking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: External programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>A number of external software applications offer ad filtering as a primary or additional feature. A traditional solution is to customize an <a href="/wiki/HTTP_proxy" class="mw-redirect" title="HTTP proxy">HTTP proxy</a> (or web proxy) to filter content. These programs work by caching and filtering content before it is displayed in a user's browser. This provides an opportunity to remove not only ads but also content which may be offensive, inappropriate, or even malicious (<a href="/wiki/Drive-by_download" title="Drive-by download">Drive-by download</a>). Popular proxy software which blocks content effectively include <a href="/wiki/Netnanny" class="mw-redirect" title="Netnanny">Netnanny</a>, <a href="/wiki/Privoxy" title="Privoxy">Privoxy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Squid_(software)" title="Squid (software)">Squid</a>, and some <a href="/wiki/Content-control_software" title="Content-control software">content-control software</a>. The main advantage of the method is freedom from implementation limitations (browser, working techniques) and centralization of control (the proxy can be used by many users). Proxies are very good at filtering ads, but they have several limitations compared to browser based solutions. For proxies, it is difficult to filter <a href="/wiki/Transport_Layer_Security" title="Transport Layer Security">Transport Layer Security</a> (SSL) (https://) traffic and full webpage context is not available to the filter. As well, proxies find it difficult to filter <a href="/wiki/JavaScript" title="JavaScript">JavaScript</a>-generated ad content.</p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hosts_file_and_DNS_manipulation">Hosts file and DNS manipulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ad_blocking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Hosts file and DNS manipulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
       Further information: 
       <a href="/wiki/Hosts_(file)#Extended_applications" title="Hosts (file)">Hosts (file) §&nbsp;Extended applications</a>
      </div> 
      <p>Most <a href="/wiki/Operating_system" title="Operating system">operating systems</a>, even those which are aware of the <a href="/wiki/Domain_Name_System" title="Domain Name System">Domain Name System</a> (DNS), still offer <a href="/wiki/Backward_compatibility" title="Backward compatibility">backward compatibility</a> with a locally administered list of foreign hosts. This configuration, for historical reasons, is stored in a flat text file that by default contains very few hostnames and their associated <a href="/wiki/IP_address" title="IP address">IP addresses</a>. Editing this hosts file is simple and effective because most DNS clients will read the local hosts file before querying a remote <a href="/wiki/DNS_server" class="mw-redirect" title="DNS server">DNS server</a>. Storing <a href="/wiki/Black_hole_(networking)" title="Black hole (networking)">black-hole</a> entries in the hosts file prevents the browser from accessing an ad server by manipulating the name resolution of the ad server to a local or nonexistent IP address (127.0.0.1 or 0.0.0.0 are typically used for IPv4 addresses). While simple to implement, these methods can be circumvented by advertisers, either by hard-coding the IP address of the server that hosts the ads (this, in its turn, can be worked around by changing the local routing table by using for example <a href="/wiki/Iptables" title="Iptables">iptables</a> or other blocking firewalls), or by loading the advertisements from the same server that serves the main content; blocking name resolution of this server would also block the useful content of the site.</p> 
      <p>Using a <a href="/wiki/DNS_sinkhole" title="DNS sinkhole">DNS sinkhole</a> by manipulating the hosts file exploits the fact that most operating systems store a file with IP address, domain name pairs which is consulted by most browsers before using a DNS server to look up a domain name. By assigning the <a href="/wiki/Loopback_address" class="mw-redirect" title="Loopback address">loopback address</a> to each known <a href="/wiki/Ad_server" class="mw-redirect" title="Ad server">ad server</a>, the user directs traffic intended to reach each <a href="/wiki/Ad_server" class="mw-redirect" title="Ad server">ad server</a> to the local machine or to a virtual black hole of <a href="/wiki//dev/null" class="mw-redirect" title="/dev/null">/dev/null</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bit_bucket" title="Bit bucket">bit bucket</a>.</p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="DNS_cache">DNS cache</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ad_blocking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: DNS cache">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>This method operates by filtering and changing records of a <a href="/wiki/DNS_cache" class="mw-redirect" title="DNS cache">DNS cache</a>. On most operating systems the domain name resolution always goes via DNS cache. By changing records within the cache or preventing records from entering the cache, programs are allowed or prevented from accessing domain names. The external programs monitor internal DNS cache and import DNS records from a file.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> As a part of the domain name resolution process, a DNS cache lookup is performed before contacting a DNS server. Thus its records take precedence over DNS server queries. Unlike the method of modifying a Hosts file, this method is more flexible as it uses more comprehensive data available from DNS cache records.</p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="DNS_filtering">DNS filtering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ad_blocking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: DNS filtering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>The filtering and selective blocking of DNS traffic can be performed by a DNS firewall which is configured to block DNS name resolution based on name patterns. A DNS firewall, such as <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.verigio.com/products/dns-firewall/">Verigio DNS Firewall</a>, can also block access to IP addresses for names not resolved via DNS. Thus prevent display of advertisements from servers accessed directly using their IP addresses.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><br> Advertising can be blocked by using a DNS server which is configured to block access to domains or hostnames which are known to serve ads by spoofing the address.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Users can choose to use an already modified DNS server or set up a dedicated device such as a <a href="/wiki/Raspberry_Pi" title="Raspberry Pi">Raspberry Pi</a> themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Manipulating DNS is a widely employed method to manipulate what the end user sees from the Internet but can also be deployed locally for personal purposes. China runs its own <a href="/wiki/Root_name_server" title="Root name server">root DNS</a> and the EU has considered the same. <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> has required their <a href="/wiki/Google_Public_DNS" title="Google Public DNS">Google Public DNS</a> be used for some applications on its <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a> devices. Accordingly, DNS addresses / domains used for advertising may be extremely vulnerable to a broad form of ad substitution whereby a domain that serves ads is entirely swapped out with one serving more local ads to some subset of users. This is especially likely in countries, notably <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, where advertisers often refuse to pay for clicks or page views. DNS-level blocking of domains for non-commercial reasons is already common in China.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hardware_devices">Hardware devices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ad_blocking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Hardware devices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Devices such as AdTrap use hardware to block Internet advertising.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Based on reviews of AdTrap, this device uses a <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a> <a href="/wiki/Kernel_(operating_system)" title="Kernel (operating system)">Kernel</a> running a version of PrivProxy to block ads from <a href="/wiki/Video_streaming" class="mw-redirect" title="Video streaming">video streaming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Music_streaming" class="mw-redirect" title="Music streaming">music streaming</a>, and any Internet browser.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Another such solution is provided for network level ad blocking for telcos by Israeli startup Shine.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_external_parties_and_internet_providers">By external parties and internet providers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ad_blocking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: By external parties and internet providers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>Internet providers, especially mobile operators, frequently offer proxies designed to reduce network traffic. Even when not targeted specifically at ad filtering, these proxy-based arrangements will block many types of advertisements that are too large or bandwidth-consuming, or that are otherwise deemed unsuited for the specific internet connection or target device. Many internet operators block some form of advertisements while at the same time injecting their own ads promoting their services and specials.</p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_consequences_for_online_business">Economic consequences for online business</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ad_blocking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Economic consequences for online business">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Some content providers have argued that widespread ad blocking results in decreased revenue to a website sustained by advertisements,<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/E-commerce" title="E-commerce">e-commerce</a>-based businesses, where this blocking can be detected. Some<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (January 2017)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> have argued that since advertisers are ultimately paying for ads to increase their own revenues, eliminating ad blocking would only dilute the value per impression and drive down the price of advertising, arguing that like <a href="/wiki/Click_fraud" title="Click fraud">click fraud</a>, impressions served to users who use ad blockers are of little to no value to advertisers. Consequently, they argue, eliminating ad blocking would not increase overall ad revenue to content providers in the long run.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p> 
      <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publisher_countermeasures_and_justifications">Publisher countermeasures and justifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ad_blocking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Publisher countermeasures and justifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
      <p>Some websites have taken counter-measures against ad blocking software, such as attempting to detect the presence of ad blockers and informing users of their views, or outright preventing users from accessing the content unless they disable the ad blocking software. There have been several arguments supporting<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> and opposing<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> the assertion that blocking ads is wrong.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p> 
      <p>Some publisher companies have taken steps to protect their rights to conduct their business according to prevailing law.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-53">[53]</a></sup> It has been suggested that in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, the practice of websites scanning for ad blocking software may run afoul of the <a href="/wiki/Directive_on_Privacy_and_Electronic_Communications" class="mw-redirect" title="Directive on Privacy and Electronic Communications">E-Privacy Directive</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-thedrum-euprivacyadblock_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thedrum-euprivacyadblock-54">[54]</a></sup> This claim was further validated by IAB Europe's guidelines released in June 2016 stating that there indeed may be a legal issue in ad blocker detection.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> While some anti-blocking stakeholders have tried to refute this<sup id="cite_ref-euprivacyadblock-debunk_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-euprivacyadblock-debunk-56">[56]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-euprivacyadblock-digiday_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-euprivacyadblock-digiday-57">[57]</a></sup> it seems safe to assume that Publishers should follow the guidelines provided by the main Publisher lobby IAB. The joint effort announced by IAB Sweden prior to IAB Europe's guideline on the matter never materialized, and would have most likely been found against European anti-competition laws if it did.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (August 2017)">original research?</span></a></i>]</sup></p> 
      <p>In August 2017, a vendor of such counter-measures issued a takedown notice under the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Digital_Millennium_Copyright_Act" title="Digital Millennium Copyright Act">Digital Millennium Copyright Act</a>, to demand the removal of a <a href="/wiki/Domain_name" title="Domain name">domain name</a> associated with their service from an ad-blocking filter list. The vendor argued that the domain constituted a component of a <a href="/wiki/Technological_protection_measures" class="mw-redirect" title="Technological protection measures">technological protection measure</a>, thus making it a violation of <a href="/wiki/Anti-circumvention" title="Anti-circumvention">anti-circumvention</a> law to frustrate access to it. .<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></p>
