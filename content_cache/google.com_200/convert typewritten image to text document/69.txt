     <p>Writing only requires words, a bit of punctuation, and perhaps some paragraph breaks to organize thoughts. Headings, italicized text, images, fancy quotes, footnotes, bullet points and more are just decoration.</p> 
     <p>Decoration is important, though, something you want to be <em>just right</em>. If your curtains are shorter than your windows, or your pictures are all hung crooked, the entire room will look off.</p> 
     <p>That's the problem with text formatting. Add bold text and bullet points and handsome headings to a Word document, then copy and paste it into your blog, and odds are the shine will have quickly faded.</p> 
     <p><a href="https://daringfireball.net/projects/markdown/">Markdown</a> fixes that, with simple formatting that looks the same everywhere. Here's how to use the world's most popular text formatting language—and some great apps to help you craft and decorate your next great novel.</p> 
     <p class="related-content">Originally published Jan 21, 2016; updated Aug 21, 2017 with new pricing and features for markdown apps.<br></p> 
     <hr> 
     <ul> 
      <li><a href="#history">What is Markdown?</a></li> 
      <li><a href="#basics">Markdown Basics</a></li> 
      <li><a href="#advanced">Advanced Markdown: Lists, Tables and More</a></li> 
      <li><a href="#apps">The Best Markdown Writing Apps</a></li> 
      <li><a href="#tools">What Can I do With Markdown?</a></li> 
     </ul>

     <p><a id="history"></a></p> 
     <h2>What is Markdown?</h2> 
     <div class="figure widen center shadow"> 
      <img src="https://zapier.cachefly.net/storage/photos/4c0a9c4a27eb834e6d9e8513d1accfe1.png" alt="what is markdown"> 
     </div> 
     <p>Imagine typing on a mechanical typewriter, with only letters and punctuation at your fingertips. There's no italics, no color options, no larger typeface for headers. How do you emphasize words, set apart quotes, and both make your document nicer looking and easier to read at the same time?</p> 
     <p>The simplest way would be to repurpose some special characters. Perhaps use a hash symbol (yes, what the kids these days call a hashtag) for headers, an underscore for italics, and asterisk for strong, bold words.</p> 
     <p>With that, you'd have reinvented a basic version of Markdown.</p> 
     <p>Markdown was created in 2004 by John Gruber and Aaron Swartz as a way to format text for the web using simple characters including asterisks, underscores, and brackets. "The overriding design goal for Markdown’s formatting syntax is to make it as readable as possible," writes Gruber on Markdown's main page. Format your text with a few symbols, and it's still perfectly readable—both by humans and by programs which could turn the text into even more readable PDFs, websites, and printed books.</p>
     <p class="pullquote">"The overriding design goal for Markdown’s formatting syntax is to make it as readable as possible."
      <attribution>
       - 
       <a href="https://daringfireball.net/projects/markdown/">John Gruber</a>, Markdown co-creator
      </attribution></p>
     <p>Formatting text with Markdown is far more precise than the rich text formatting Word offers, while far simpler to write—and read—than HTML code. Paste Markdown formatted text into another app, and it <em>always</em> looks the exact same—something you couldn't say for formatted text copied from Word.</p> 
     <p>Markdown the best of the plain and formatted worlds, an ideal way to write in a world where we each have our own favorite writing apps and blogging platforms.</p> 
     <p>It's the one simple type of coding <em>everyone</em> should learn to use. So let's get started.</p> 
     <p><a id="basics"></a></p> 
     <h2>How to Format Text in Markdown</h2> 
     <div class="figure center shadow"> 
      <img src="https://zapier.cachefly.net/storage/photos/3642d21716b50985a9ae85498d2c2ddc.gif" alt="Markdown GIF"> 
     </div> 
     <p>Ready to add some formatting to your text? Here are the basics:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><strong>Italics</strong>: Add one asterisk or underscore around your text <code>*like this*</code> or <code>_this_</code></li> 
      <li><strong>Bold</strong>: Add two asterisks or underscores around your text <code>**like this**</code> or <code>__this__</code></li> 
      <li><strong>Bold <em>and</em> Italic</strong>: Add three asterisks or underscores around your text <code>***like this***</code> or <code>___this___</code></li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>You can even mix it up. Want a whole sentence in italics, with one word in bold?</p> 
     <p><code>*This **should** work*.</code></p> 
     <p>Got that? Now, let's get a bit fancier.</p> 
     <p>Sentences and paragraphs don't need anything special. Just make sure to leave a blank line between each paragraph, and you'll be good. Then, to add fancier things to your document:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li><strong>Links</strong>: Add brackets around the linked text, then add a parenthesis, your link, and a close parenthesis, like this: <code>[Zapier](https://zapier.com/)</code></li> 
      <li><strong>Image</strong>: Type an exclamation mark, then an open bracket, then add alt text to describe your image, then close the bracket and add a parenthesis, paste a link to your image, then close the parentheses, like this: <code>![Zapier Logo](logo.png)</code></li> 
      <li><strong>Quotes</strong>: Add a greater-than sign in front of each line of quoted text, like this: <code>&gt; Just do it!</code></li> 
      <li><strong>Code</strong>: Add 4 spaces before each line of code, or a backtick before and after the code, like this: <code>`&lt;a href="https://zapier.com/"&gt;Zapier&lt;/a&gt;`</code></li> 
      <li><strong>Strikethrough</strong>: Add two tildes around the text <code>~~like this~~</code> (though this may not work in all Markdown apps)</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p class="related-content">Want to use a backtick in your Markdown text? Either escape it by including a backslash first, like <code>\`</code>, or put three backticks around the backtick to display it in code.<br></p> 
     <p>With all that stuff, you might want to break your document into sections with <strong>Headings</strong>. Markdown uses standard HTML-style headings, but you add them with a hash or pound symbol. For the largest headings, or H1 in HTML, add one hash symbol and a space in front of a line of text—or add 2 hash symbols without a space between them for H2, 3 for H3, and so on, like this:</p> 
     <div class="codehilite">
      <pre><span></span># Our important Story

A long time ago...

## The first section

Some more text...
</pre>
     </div> 
     <p>Ok. Looks like you've got the basics down. Try writing your own story with a bit of Markdown formatting, and you should quickly get something like this:</p> 
     <div class="figure widen shadow"> 
      <img src="https://zapier.cachefly.net/storage/photos/4073ac07987f8783a87cdffba02f85e4.png" alt="markdown text"> 
     </div> 
     <p>Let's add a bit more to that.</p> 
     <p><a id="advanced"></a></p> 
     <h2>Advanced Markdown</h2> 
     <p>There's only two more things you'll likely want to add to your Markdown document: lists and tables.</p> 
     <p><strong>Lists</strong> are simple. For <strong>Unordered Lists</strong>, just add a dash, asterisk, or plus symbol before each line in your list, like this:</p> 
     <div class="codehilite">
      <pre><span></span>- Something
- Another thing
- Yet one more thing
</pre>
     </div> 
     <p><strong>Ordered Lists</strong> work the same, but with numbers and periods instead of dashes, like this:</p> 
     <div class="codehilite">
      <pre><span></span>1. First thing
2. Second thing
3. Third thing
</pre>
     </div> 
     <p>Or you could mix them, and add nested lists (in most Markdown apps, anyhow) just by adding two spaces before the nested items, like this:</p> 
     <div class="codehilite">
      <pre><span></span>1. First thing
  - Something
  - Another thing
2. Second thing
3. Third thing
</pre>
     </div> 
     <p><strong>Tables</strong> are the most tricky thing in Markdown, and they're also only supported in some Markdown apps. Essentially, to make a table, add a pipe character—or this: <code>|</code>—before and after every cell in the table, and add a row of dashes to separate your header cell. To make everything look nicer, use tabs to separate text instead of tabs. Here's how it should look:</p> 
     <div class="codehilite">
      <pre><span></span>|   Food    |   Price   |
|   -----   |   -----   |
|   Eggs    |   $2.99   |
|   Bacon   |   $4.99   |
|   Bread   |   $1.99   |
</pre>
     </div> 
     <p>With a bit of tweaking, you should be able to get your lists and tables to look something like this:</p> 
     <div class="figure widen shadow"> 
      <img src="https://zapier.cachefly.net/storage/photos/36d4b165bdc28765c0a5c909d00c393f.png" alt="table and list in markdown"> 
     </div> 
     <p>And that's it. With those handful of characters and tricks, you'll be able to format anything you want with Markdown. Want to embed a GIF or Video? Just upload the file to your site, then use the image embed. Want to embed a YouTube video, form, or any other web element? Just paste the embed code into your document, and it'll render just fine on most websites.</p> 
     <p>You're now ready to start writing in Markdown. Want a new app to help you out?</p>

     <p>For the nice, typewriter-like feel most Markdown apps bring, use a monospaced typeface like <a href="http://quoteunquoteapps.com/courierprime/">Courier Prime</a>, along an off-white background color and a grey color for your text. Then, remember to save the finished document in <code>.txt</code> format, so you can open it anywhere without having to rely on Word or another word processor app.</p> 
     <p class="related-content">For longform writing, check out our detailed comparison of <a href="https://zapier.com/blog/ulysses-scrivener/">Scrivener versus Ulysses</a> to see which is best for your writing needs.<br></p> 
     <p><a id="tools"></a></p> 
     <h2>What to do with Markdown Text?</h2> 
     <p>You've written something amazing, something you're ready to share with the world. You could just copy your Markdown-formatted text and share it or print it out, but it'd look a tiny bit odd to most people.</p> 
     <p>Instead, you should turn it into beautifully formatted documents, books, websites, presentations, and more. Here's how.</p> 
     <h3>Print</h3> 
     <div class="figure widen center shadow"> 
      <img src="https://zapier.cachefly.net/storage/photos/a958eee9b50410ad785355fedf8b48e8.png" alt="Markdown publishing from Ulysses"> 
      <div class="caption">
       A PDF export from Ulysses
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>Writing anything lengthy takes a number of steps. Once you've written your text, it'll need edited, formatted into a print book or brochure, tweaked to look great in a PDF or a mobile-ready eBook, then distributed to the world.</p> 
     <p>Rich text quickly becomes a problem, as your text formatting slowly changes from each different app in the publishing workflow. Markdown, instead, keeps your text consistent—and makes formatting as easy as writing some CSS.</p> 
     <p>All you'll need is a Markdown writing app, and an export tool to turn Markdown into eBooks, PDFs, or anything else you want. Or you might just need the writing tool: iA Writer and Byword include simple formatting to export text as a PDF or a print document, while Ulysses and Scrivener include customizable export templates to publish Markdown as eBooks and more.</p> 
     <p>If you want a tool just for turning Markdown into beautiful documents, <a href="http://pandoc.org/getting-started.html">Pandoc</a> is one of the most powerful Markdown export tools. It can be tricky to master, though, and only runs in Terminal. For a simpler way to export Markdown, <a href="http://marked2app.com/">Marked</a> is a great option with customizable PDF and eBook templates. Or, you can use <a href="https://zapier.com/help/formatter">Zapier's Formatter</a> tool to convert Markdown text to HTML, then send it to any of your apps for safekeeping or publication.</p> 
     <p>If you're writing a book, just upload your Markdown files to <a href="http://leanpub.com/">LeanPub</a> and it'll turn them into print-ready PDFs and Kindle and iBooks-ready eBooks, and even let you sell your book right its site.</p> 
     <p class="related-content">Want to turn your Markdown text into Rich Text or HTML? Just check your writing app's <em>Save As</em> menu. Then you can paste the Rich Text into any other app, send it in an email, or do anything else with it you want.<br></p> 
     <h3>Publish</h3> 
     <div class="figure widen center shadow"> 
      <img src="https://zapier.cachefly.net/storage/photos/f7cc65cb0d1b3a43bd1b170e923b5c9c.png" alt="Markdown in WordPress"> 
      <div class="caption">
       You can format Wordpress blog posts using Markdown
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>Blogging is where Markdown shines brightest. It started out with a plugin for <a href="https://movabletype.org/documentation/author/text-formatting.html">Movable Type</a>, and since has spread to almost every new blogging platform.</p> 
     <p><a href="https://www.tumblr.com/docs/en/posting">Tumblr</a> was one of the first blog platforms to support Markdown. Just click the gear icon when writing a post, and select "Markdown" under the Text Editor options to use Markdown to format your posts. In <a href="https://support.squarespace.com/hc/en-us/articles/205813788-Using-the-Markdown-Block">Squarespace</a>, just use the Markdown block when adding text to your site—and you can set it as your default text block if you'd like.</p> 
     <p>The new blogging platform <a href="http://support.ghost.org/markdown-guide/">Ghost</a> is designed around Markdown; write your post with Markdown formatting, and preview the final version in the right column at the same time. Flat-file CMS apps like <a href="http://getkirby.com/">Kirby</a> and <a href="https://jekyllrb.com/">Jekyll</a> are also designed around Markdown-formatted text files.</p> 
     <p>WordPress now supports Markdown, too. It's built into WordPress.com—just <a href="https://en.support.wordpress.com/markdown/">tick the Markdown box in your blog's Writing settings</a>. On a self-hosted WordPress site, you can add Markdown support with a plugin like <a href="https://wordpress.org/plugins/wp-markdown/">WP-Markdown</a> which converts Markdown text into HTML before publishing your blog post.</p> 
     <p>Medium's known for having an easy-to-use editor, though it doesn't support Markdown right now. Instead, though, you can use some of the most popular Markdown apps—including upcoming new versions of Byword and iA Writer—to <a href="https://medium.com/the-story/medium-s-publishing-api-31b9f05fada1">publish Markdown-formatted text directly on Medium</a>.</p> 
     <h3>Present</h3> 
     <div class="figure widen center shadow"> 
      <img src="https://zapier.cachefly.net/storage/photos/035c351355fa87af4afede7c98b8710a.png" alt="Deckset and iA Writer"> 
      <div class="caption">
       Deckset can turn Markdown text and images into a presentation
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>PowerPoint is overkill for most presentations. If your presentation is just text and images, you could just write your notes in Markdown and turn them directly into a presentation.</p> 
     <p><a href="http://www.decksetapp.com/">Deckset</a> is one of the most popular Markdown presentation apps. Drag a Markdown text file into the app, then pick from a few templates and you'll have a presentation in seconds. <a href="http://slidify.org/">Slidify</a> is a similar app for Windows, while <a href="http://www.texts.io/">Texts</a>, one of the writing apps mentioned above, lets you export your documents as presentations too.</p> 
     <p>Alternately, you can turn Markdown into a web-ready presentation using toolkits like <a href="http://remarkjs.com/#1">Remark.js</a> and <a href="http://jdan.github.io/cleaver/">Cleaver</a>. Your new presentation could be shown from a browser—perhaps using a <a href="https://zapier.com/blog/chromecast-business-apps/">Chromecast</a>—or you could just upload it to your website as a quick way to publish a presentation.</p> 
     <hr> 
     <p>When every word matters, it's <em>words</em> that should have the full focus—not formatting. Markdown quickly adds the most basic formatting to your text, and lets you trust that the finished work will look exactly like you intended.</p> 
     <p>After all, that's what really matters: writing down the words you've always wanted to say, in the way you wanted them to appear.</p> 
     <p>It might take time to become fluent at Markdown, just as it's difficult to <a href="http://www.slate.com/articles/technology/technology/2011/01/space_invaders.html">drop double-spacing</a>. But it's a worthwhile investment for simpler, more consistent writing for the web—and the future.</p>
