       <h3 class="imgixJS-contentTitle">Getting Started</h3> 
       <p>If you don't already have an imgix account, <a href="https://webapp.imgix.com/sign-up">it’s free to sign up</a>. imgix.js makes use of the <a href="https://docs.imgix.com/apis/url">imgix URL API</a>, and require images to be served through an <a href="https://docs.imgix.com/setup/creating-sources" target="_blank">imgix Source</a>.</p> 
       <p>imgix.js makes heavy use of the <code>srcset</code> and <code>sizes</code> attributes available on <code>&lt;img&gt;</code> and <code>&lt;picture&gt;</code> elements. These allow modern browsers to deliver a responsive experience to their users. By generating comprehensive <code>srcset</code> rules, you can deliver pixel-perfect experiences to all browsers and devices.</p> 
       <p>Because imgix renders images on the fly, you don’t need to worry about manually generating a new image for each size. For a primer on how <code>srcset</code> and <code>sizes</code> work, read <a href="https://ericportis.com/posts/2014/srcset-sizes/">“Srcset and sizes”</a> by Eric Portis.</p> 
      </div>

       <h3 class="imgixJS-contentTitle">Using imgix.js with <code>srcset</code> for resolution switching</h3> 
       <p>When people think responsive images, they usually think “resolution switching.” Resolution switching is serving a different resolution image to different devices. The content, crop, and composition of the image remains the same, but the resolution changes.</p> 
       <p>imgix is a service uniquely equipped to handle this case. All renders are generated on the fly and cached at edge locations around the globe. With imgix.js, implementing resolution switching is simple:</p> 
       <div class="imgixJS-codeExample"> 
        <pre class="highlight html"><code><span class="nt">&lt;img</span> <span class="na">ix-src=</span><span class="s">"https://assets.imgix.net/coffee.jpg?fit=crop&amp;amp;crop=faces"</span>
     <span class="na">sizes=</span><span class="s">"(min-width: 768px) 540px, 100vw"</span>
     <span class="na">alt=</span><span class="s">"A woman drinking a cup of coffee at Sightglass"</span><span class="nt">&gt;</span>
</code></pre> 
       </div> 
       <p>Upon initialization, imgix.js will augment any image element with the <code>ix-src</code> attribute to generate an exhaustive <code>srcset</code> definition. Here’s what you will see under the hood if you inspect the element:</p> 
       <div class="imgixJS-codeExample"> 
        <pre class="highlight html"><code><span class="nt">&lt;img</span> <span class="na">ix-src=</span><span class="s">"https://assets.imgix.net/coffee.jpg?fit=crop&amp;amp;crop=faces"</span>
     <span class="na">alt=</span><span class="s">"A woman drinking a cup of coffee at Sightglass"</span>
     <span class="na">sizes=</span><span class="s">"(min-width: 768px) 540px, 100vw"</span>
     <span class="na">srcset=</span><span class="s">" https://assets.imgix.net/unsplash/coffee.jpg?w=100&amp;amp;h=67&amp;amp;fit=crop&amp;amp;crop=faces 100w, https://assets.imgix.net/unsplash/coffee.jpg?w=200&amp;amp;h=150&amp;amp;fit=crop&amp;amp;crop=faces 200w, … https://assets.imgix.net/unsplash/coffee.jpg?w=2560&amp;amp;h=1920&amp;amp;fit=crop&amp;amp;crop=faces 2560w "</span>
     <span class="na">src=</span><span class="s">"https://assets.imgix.net/unsplash/coffee.jpg?w=400&amp;amp;h=300&amp;amp;fit=crop&amp;amp;crop=faces"</span>
     <span class="na">ix-initialized=</span><span class="s">"ix-initialized"</span><span class="nt">&gt;</span>
</code></pre> 
       </div> 
       <p>The resulting image will be loaded in at 540 logical pixels wide on tablets and desktops, thanks to the <code>(min-width: 768px) 540px</code> rule. It will be loaded at 100% viewport width (<code>100vw</code>) on mobile screens. Modern browsers will select an appropriate image from the local cache if available. If no appropriate image is available, it will request a new image at the targeted <code>w</code> that it needs.</p> 
       <p>Browsers running on devices with high-density displays will select the image most appropriate for their resolution. In this example, an iPhone 6S will select the <code>750w</code> URL from the <code>srcset</code> attribute. This is because the iPhone 6S is 375 logical pixels wide, but has a screen density of 2x. Thus, <code>375 × 2 = 750</code>.</p> 
       <img ix-src="https://assets.imgix.net/unsplash/moon.jpg?fit=crop&amp;crop=entropy&amp;w=400&amp;h=300" class="imgixJS-imageExample" sizes="(min-width: 1440px) 812px, (min-width: 1200px) 670px, (min-width: 768px) 540px, calc(100vw - 48px)" alt="A photo of the Moon"> 
       <p>The result is that just the right image is loaded for each device and browser. imgix.js is able to easily handle resolution switching by generating <code>srcset</code> attributes.</p>

       <h3 class="imgixJS-contentTitle">Simplifying Configuration</h3> 
       <p>imgix.js provides the ability to greatly simplify how you define your images and their operations on your site. This is done with the <code>ix-path</code> and <code>ix-params</code> attributes on <code>&lt;img&gt;</code> elements.</p> 
       <div class="imgixJS-codeExample"> 
        <pre class="highlight html"><code><span class="nt">&lt;img</span>
  <span class="na">ix-path=</span><span class="s">"coffee.jpg"</span>
  <span class="na">ix-params=</span><span class="s">'{ "w": 400, "h": 300, "fit": "crop", "crop": "faces" }'</span>
  <span class="na">ix-host=</span><span class="s">"assets.imgix.net"</span>
  <span class="na">sizes=</span><span class="s">"(min-width: 768px) 540px, 100vw"</span>
  <span class="na">alt=</span><span class="s">"A woman drinking a cup of coffee at Sightglass"</span>
<span class="nt">&gt;</span>
</code></pre>

The definition of <code>ix-host</code> can also be defined globally in a <code>&lt;meta&gt;</code> element, so that all images on the site use the same host.        <h3 class="imgixJS-contentTitle">Using imgix.js with <code>&lt;picture&gt;</code> for art direction</h3> 
       <p>imgix.js can also be used for art direction in responsive images by using the <code>&lt;picture&gt;</code> element. Art direction in responsive images allows you to have more creative control over the images at different breakpoints, such as being able to specify different crops at different breakpoints.</p> 
       <p>imgix.js is able to exhaustively generate <code>srcset</code> attributes on <code>&lt;source&gt;</code> elements contained within a <code>&lt;picture&gt;</code> element.</p> 
       <p>For this example, we will specify 2 different sources: one wide (16:9) crop for the desktop and one square (1:1) crop for mobile devices.</p> 
       <div class="imgixJS-codeExample"> 
        <pre class="highlight html"><code><span class="nt">&lt;picture&gt;</span>
    <span class="c">&lt;!-- Wide crop --&gt;</span>
    <span class="nt">&lt;source</span>
        <span class="na">media=</span><span class="s">"(min-width: 768px)"</span>
        <span class="na">sizes=</span><span class="s">"640px"</span>
        <span class="na">ix-path=</span><span class="s">"unsplash/astronaut.jpg"</span>
        <span class="na">ix-params=</span><span class="s">'{ "w": 1600, "h": 900, "fit": "crop", "crop": "middle" }'</span>
    <span class="nt">&gt;</span>
    <span class="c">&lt;!-- Square crop --&gt;</span>
    <span class="nt">&lt;source</span>
        <span class="na">sizes=</span><span class="s">"calc(100vw - 48px)"</span>
        <span class="na">ix-path=</span><span class="s">"unsplash/astronaut.jpg"</span>
        <span class="na">ix-params=</span><span class="s">'{ "w": 300, "h": 300, "fp-x": 0.49, "fp-y": 0.4, "fp-z": 3, "crop": "focalpoint", "fit": "crop" }'</span>
    <span class="nt">&gt;</span>
    <span class="nt">&lt;img</span> <span class="na">ix-path=</span><span class="s">"unsplash/astronaut.jpg"</span><span class="nt">&gt;</span>
<span class="nt">&lt;/picture&gt;</span>
</code></pre> 
       </div> 
       <p>This code allows for different crops at different viewport sizes. At viewports wider than <code>768px</code>, our image receives a crop at a 16×9 aspect ratio. Smaller than <code>768px</code>, we give the image a square crop to show just the astronaut.</p> 
       <p>Try resizing the browser window to see this effect in action. Because the contents of the 2 images are different, the browser will request a new image to download the crop it doesn’t have.</p> 
       <picture class="imgixJS-imageExample"> 
        <source media="(min-width: 768px)" sizes="640px" ix-path="unsplash/astronaut.jpg" ix-params="{
                            &quot;w&quot;: 1600,
                            &quot;h&quot;: 900,
                            &quot;fit&quot;: &quot;crop&quot;,
                            &quot;crop&quot;: &quot;middle&quot;
                            }"> 
        <source sizes="calc(100vw - 48px)" ix-path="unsplash/astronaut.jpg" ix-params="{
                            &quot;w&quot;: 300,
                            &quot;h&quot;: 300,
                            &quot;fp-x&quot;: 0.49,
                            &quot;fp-y&quot;: 0.4,
                            &quot;fp-z&quot;: 3,
                            &quot;crop&quot;: &quot;focalpoint&quot;,
                            &quot;fit&quot;: &quot;crop&quot;
                            }"> 
        <img ix-path="unsplash/astronaut.jpg"> 
       </picture> 
       <p>As with our previous examples, the exhaustively generated <code>srcset</code> attributes allow each browser to select the exact size needed for proper display. This is all done without binding event listeners to the <code>window</code>, which means the experiences will always be very smooth or "jank-free."</p>
