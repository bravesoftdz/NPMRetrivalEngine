           <h1 class="headline"> <span id="introduction">Overview</span> </h1> 
           <div class="author">
            by 
            <a href="/users/thekitty"> <span class="name">Mike Barela</span> </a> 
           </div> 
          </div> 
          <div class="page-content"> 
           <div class="build-embed"> 
            <div class="build-video">
             <iframe width="459" height="344" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/kzgYpn55ijg?feature=oembed" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen></iframe>
            </div> 
           </div> 
           <div class="row-fluid build-text"> 
            <p>Talking computers were on fire 20 to 30 years ago. &nbsp;Movies like War Games and TV series like Knight Rider featured electronics&nbsp;speaking to their human operators. Speak-n-Spell machines taught a generation of children. Then the magic was out of the bottle and the focus drifted to other technologies.</p> 
            <p>Today, we have a new explosion of voice enabled devices. &nbsp;They announced this week the Amazon Echo will read Kindle books &nbsp;You will see more devices speaking with the growth of the Internet of Things. &nbsp;Special speech systhesis chips of old are no longer required. &nbsp;The smallest of today's Linux computers often has the capabity to output speech. &nbsp;This includes the Raspberry Pi line of single board computers.</p> 
            <p>From the Raspberry Pi Zero to the A+/B+ to the Raspberry Pi 2, all have the capacity to run free software to turn text to speech.</p> 
            <p>This tutorial will show you how to have your Pi use the free software packages Festival and its derivative Flite to output voice.</p> 
           </div> 
           <div class="row-fluid build-text"> 
            <h2> <a href="#festival" class="anchor-link"><span class="fa fa-link"></span></a><span id="festival" class="anchor-link-target"></span>Festival</h2> 
            <p>Festival, written by&nbsp;The Centre for Speech Technology Research in the UK, offers a framework for building speech synthesis systems. It offers full text to speech through a number APIs: from shell level, via&nbsp;a command interpreter, as a C++ library, from Java, and an Emacs editor interface. Festival is multi-lingual (currently British English, American English, and Spanish. Other groups work to release new languages for the system. &nbsp;Festival is in the package manager for the Raspberry Pi making it very easy to install.</p> 
            <h2> <a href="#flite" class="anchor-link"><span class="fa fa-link"></span></a><span id="flite" class="anchor-link-target"></span>Flite</h2> 
            <p>Flite is a lighter version of Festival built specifically for embedded systems. &nbsp;It has commands that make it easier to use than Festival on the command line. &nbsp;It runs faster than Festival. &nbsp;Unless you have the need for Festival's complex scripting language or phoneme handling, Flite is the go-to program. &nbsp;Flite is also in the package manager.</p> 
            <p>There are other speech programs for Linux, including <a title="eSpeak" href="http://espeak.sourceforge.net/" target="_blank">eSpeak</a>. &nbsp;If you find that Festival / Flite does not meet your needs, then check out eSpeak or other packages.</p>
