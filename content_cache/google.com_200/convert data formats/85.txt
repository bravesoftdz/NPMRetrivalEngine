        <h1 class="entry-title">Moment.JS Shows the Wrong&nbsp;Date!</h1> 
       </header>
       <!-- .entry-header --> 
       <div class="entry-content"> 
        <p>I have been answering some&nbsp;Stack Overflow questions lately, and it seems like quite a lot of them all boil down to someone saying that Moment.JS is showing the wrong date.</p> 
        <p>Here are some of the most common reasons why Moment might not be showing what you expect, and the ways to get it to do what you want:</p> 
        <h2 id="consoleDump">The console shows the wrong date when I dump&nbsp;a Moment object</h2> 
        <p>I might run code that looks something like this:</p> 
        <p><img data-attachment-id="531" data-permalink="https://maggiepint.com/2016/05/14/moment-js-shows-the-wrong-date/momentconsoleutc/" data-orig-file="https://maggiepint.files.wordpress.com/2016/05/momentconsoleutc.png?w=1108" data-orig-size="487,203" data-comments-opened="1" data-image-meta="{&quot;aperture&quot;:&quot;0&quot;,&quot;credit&quot;:&quot;&quot;,&quot;camera&quot;:&quot;&quot;,&quot;caption&quot;:&quot;&quot;,&quot;created_timestamp&quot;:&quot;0&quot;,&quot;copyright&quot;:&quot;&quot;,&quot;focal_length&quot;:&quot;0&quot;,&quot;iso&quot;:&quot;0&quot;,&quot;shutter_speed&quot;:&quot;0&quot;,&quot;title&quot;:&quot;&quot;,&quot;orientation&quot;:&quot;0&quot;}" data-image-title="MomentConsoleUTC" data-image-description="" data-medium-file="https://maggiepint.files.wordpress.com/2016/05/momentconsoleutc.png?w=1108?w=300" data-large-file="https://maggiepint.files.wordpress.com/2016/05/momentconsoleutc.png?w=1108?w=487" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-531" src="https://maggiepint.files.wordpress.com/2016/05/momentconsoleutc.png?w=1108" alt="MomentConsoleUTC" srcset="https://maggiepint.files.wordpress.com/2016/05/momentconsoleutc.png 487w, https://maggiepint.files.wordpress.com/2016/05/momentconsoleutc.png?w=150 150w, https://maggiepint.files.wordpress.com/2016/05/momentconsoleutc.png?w=300 300w" sizes="(max-width: 487px) 100vw, 487px"></p> 
        <p>As you can see, I parsed January 1, but am seeing December 31 in the console.</p> 
        <p>When you dump a Moment object to the console, you see <code>Object.prototype.toString()</code> being called on the <code>_d</code> variable, which is the built-in JavaScript date object that Moment wraps. In most JavaScript implementations, that will cause that object to display in local time. I parsed a UTC time though, so I’m seeing the wrong result in the console. If I had used Moment Timezone, I would also be seeing a wrong value in the console.</p> 
        <p>This is easy to work around. Ignore the value of <code>_d</code> – it is not useful information. Instead, rely on <code>.format()</code> to get the correct result:</p> 
        <pre class="brush: plain; title: ; notranslate" title="">
moment.utc('2016-01-01').format()
2016-01-01T00:00:00Z
</pre> 
        <h2 id="formatWrong">.format() Shows a Date I Didn’t Parse</h2> 
        <p>Most often, when <code>.format()</code> isn’t giving the expected result, it is because the date was parsed with an offset, and the wrong Moment constructor function was used. This is a little bit of a complicated statement, but let’s break it down.</p> 
        <p>First, the date format causing confusion is usually ISO 8601 format with offset. That format looks like this:</p> 
        <p><code>2016-05-14T08:33:14-03:00</code></p> 
        <p>This indicates that the date in question is May 14 2016 at 8:33 AM in local time, and that the local time of this date is offset three hours behind UTC.</p> 
        <p>You may also see an ISO8601 formatted date that looks like this:</p> 
        <p><code>2016-05-14T13:37:09Z</code></p> 
        <p>Note that the <code>z</code> in the date above indicates that it is in UTC, or has an offset of <code>+00:00</code>.</p> 
        <p>This is a very helpful format in that it reflects both local time, and global time in the same time stamp. It can get people into trouble when making moments though.</p> 
        <p>Moment offers four ways to construct a moment object, and all of them will use this offset information to achieve different results.</p> 
        <p>The moment constructor functions are as follows:</p> 
        <ul> 
         <li><code>moment()</code></li> 
         <li><code>moment.utc()</code></li> 
         <li><code>moment.parseZone()</code></li> 
         <li><code>moment.tz()</code> (this only works with the Moment TimeZone add on)</li> 
        </ul> 
        <h3 id="defaultConstructor">The moment() Function </h3> 
        <p>When you use the default moment constructor, it will convert the date from the provided offset to the environment’s local time. Right now my local time is <code>-05:00</code>. As such, if I take my <code>-03:00</code> date from above, and parse it with moment, I get the following:</p> 
        <pre class="brush: plain; title: ; notranslate" title="">
moment('2016-05-14T08:33:14-03:00').format()
&amp;quot;2016-05-14T06:33:14-05:00&amp;quot;
</pre> 
        <p>As you can see, the time has been shifted from <code>08:33:14</code> to <code>06:33:14</code> to reflect the offset change.</p> 
        <p>It is important to note that the default moment constructor will use information from the browser to keep the moment in the user’s local time, even when offsets change due to DST. If I add six months to this moment, my local offset will be <code>-06:00</code>. This will be properly handled:</p> 
        <pre class="brush: plain; title: ; notranslate" title="">
moment('2016-05-14T08:33:14-03:00').add(6, 'months').format()
2016-11-14T06:33:14-06:00
</pre> 
        <h3 id="utcConstructor">The moment.utc() Function </h3> 
        <p>When you use the moment UTC constructor, it will convert the date from the offset provided to UTC. Using the same date string:</p> 
        <pre class="brush: plain; title: ; notranslate" title="">
moment.utc('2016-05-14T08:33:14-03:00').format()
&amp;quot;2016-05-14T11:33:14Z&amp;quot;
</pre> 
        <p>As you can see, this time the date has been shifted forward three hours, from <code>08:33:14</code> to <code>11:33:14</code> to be in line with UTC.</p> 
        <h3 id="zoneConstructor">The moment.parseZone() Function </h3> 
        <p>The <code>.parseZone()</code> function will parse a moment with the offset fixed to the provided offset.</p> 
        <p>It is very important to remember that this function is mis-named. When you use <code>.parseZone()</code> you are parsing the moment to have a fixed offset. A fixed offset is NOT a time zone. Most time zones will have more than one offset at various points in the year due to DST. Also, most time zones have changed their offset over time. Any math performed with a moment in fixed offset mode may yield unexpected results. The use cases for a fixed offset moment are limited. See the <a href="http://stackoverflow.com/tags/timezone/info">Stack Overflow timezone tag</a> for more information on time zones vs offsets.</p> 
        <p>Using my same time, I get the following result with <code>.parseZone()</code>:</p> 
        <pre class="brush: plain; title: ; notranslate" title="">
moment.parseZone('2016-05-14T08:33:14-03:00').format()
2016-05-14T08:33:14-03:00
</pre> 
        <p>As you can see, the time and offset are unchanged.</p> 
        <h3 id="timeZone">The moment.tz() Function </h3> 
        <p>The <code>moment.tz()</code> function will parse a given time with offset and convert it to the time zone provided. It is useful when you need to interpret a time in a time zone other than UTC, or the user’s local time.</p> 
        <p>Note that this functionality does not work without the Moment TimeZone add on library being included in your project. The Moment TimeZone library uses the IANA time zones to provide time zone support. A list of IANA time zone identifiers and offsets can be found <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_tz_database_time_zones">here</a>.</p> 
        <p>If you only need to work in UTC, and the user’s local time, do NOT bring in the Moment TimeZone library. It only causes extra code to be sent to the browser. Of course if you are in Node, feel free to bring it in everywhere just in case.</p> 
        <p>Using the same date and time as before, I will specify the time be parsed to Europe/Berlin. This time zone is UTC +2 at the time of year specified:</p> 
        <pre class="brush: plain; title: ; notranslate" title="">
moment.tz('2016-05-14T08:33:14-03:00', 'Europe/Berlin').format()
2016-05-14T13:33:14+02:00
</pre> 
        <p>As you can see, the time has been shifted five hours forward, to move from <code>-03:00</code> to <code>+02:00</code>. The resultant time is <code>13:33</code>. This moment will be time zone aware, so if you were to add six months to it, at which point Berlin will have an offset of <code>-01:00</code>, the moment will correctly change offsets:</p> 
        <pre class="brush: plain; title: ; notranslate" title="">
moment.tz('2016-05-14T08:33:14-03:00', 'Europe/Berlin').add(6, 'months').format()
2016-11-14T13:33:14+01:00
</pre> 
        <h2 id="doNotWant">But I Don’t WANT the Offset</h2> 
        <p>A common complaint about these functions is that people just don’t want the offset to be used. They wish for the date to be interpreted as local time.</p> 
        <p>If you are thinking this, please use caution. When you get a date with an offset, you are getting valuable information about both local time, and the point on the global timeline. Throwing this information away may not be helpful down the road.</p> 
        <p>If you are sure that you do not want the offset information, the best practice would be to have the system that you are getting the date from stop sending the offset. Any other choice may be misinterpreted later.</p> 
        <p>As developers though, we live in reality. We can’t always change the data that is being sent to us. If you need to ignore the offset, you have a few options.</p> 
        <p>If the offset is <code>z</code>, so all dates are being sent in UTC, just parse in UTC mode. No change will be made to the time.</p> 
        <pre class="brush: plain; title: ; notranslate" title="">
moment.utc('2016-05-14T08:33:14Z').format('LLL')
May 14, 2016 8:33 AM
</pre> 
        <p>If the offset is some other number, and you wish to ignore it, you can use <code>.parseZone()</code>, and simply not display the offset information:</p> 
        <pre class="brush: plain; title: ; notranslate" title="">
moment.parseZone('2016-05-14T08:33:14-03:00').format('LLL')
May 14, 2016 8:33 AM
</pre> 
        <p>Remember though, both of the above methods will not handle DST transitions in the user’s local time. Thus, mathematical operations can have unexpected results.</p> 
        <p>If you want to interpret the date as the user’s local time, and be able to safely do math with it, you can specify a format that has no <code>Z</code> token, and thus ignores the offset:</p> 
        <pre class="brush: plain; title: ; notranslate" title="">
moment('2016-05-14T08:33:14-03:00', 'YYYY-MM-DDTHH:mm:ss').format()
2016-05-14T08:33:14-05:00
</pre> 
        <p>As you can see, the time has been interpreted as local and has my offset. Note that this will not work in strict parsing mode, as there will be left over training characters. It will result in an invalid date.</p>
