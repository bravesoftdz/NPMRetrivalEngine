        <h2>Most developers exploring the Augmented Reality space become fixated on the first SDK they find.</h2> 
        <p>And that's an easy thing to do, because the first one most people run across will usually be one of the big ones. There was Metaio, which was spectacular - I don't think any other single solution comes close to its feature set, so it was quite an upset when Apple purchased it and took it dark last year. &nbsp;A lot of people flocked to Vuforia, then,&nbsp;and others, but many SDKs for Augmented Reality, both commercial and open source, remain available and aren't as widely known.</p> 
        <h3>What do we mean by Augmented Reality?</h3> 
        <p>While I tend, elsewhere, to use the term "Extended Reality" to refer to the confluence of interrelated terms and disciplines made up by Augmented, Mixed and Virtual Reality, I'm putting that aside for this article. For the sake of discussion, the term "Augmented Reality" in this article is used to broadly refer to the range of experiences wherein graphics, imagery and data is inserted into the real world through various digital means, typically tracked to an object or feature in the world, or more generally inserted spatially. I understand that many prefer a strict definition of "Augmented Reality" with&nbsp;a distinction from "Mixed Reality" but I choose to accept that "AR" has come to thoroughly dominate the choice of&nbsp;terms in common use, even in cases that are technically regarded as "Mixed Reality." It should be noted that none of the SDKs, products or services that I will discuss in this article call themselves "Mixed Reality" SDKs - even though we'd have to admit that they all fit that term.</p> 
        <h3>Why we'll focus on Unity-compatible SDKs</h3> 
        <p>Well, for one, I know of nearly&nbsp;50 different SDKs&nbsp;for developing Augmented Reality applications. Writing a comprehensive overview of the <em>entire </em>AR software development&nbsp;landscape is simply infeasible. That's a thick technical book, not an article. I'm also ruling out anything that requires very specific hardware. For that reason, we won't be discussing Hololens (except where we mention support for Hololens in another SDK) or Tango, RealSense or Kinect hardware-dependent SDKs.</p> 
        <p>So for this article, I'm focusing on those SDKs which provide native Unity support and work with&nbsp;commodity hardware. There's no doubt: at the moment, Unity is the winning platform to develop augmented reality applications on. It's pretty big in VR, too, owing to the widespread native support it's received from many VR and AR vendors. When Microsoft began describing how developers would create applications for Hololens, it became apparent that the only way they intended development to happen, at that stage, was in Unity. When Google released their Cardboard SDK, they provided Unity support. When they released the Tango SDK, they provided a Unity package. For Intel's RealSense, they directly supported Unity. There are certainly other options for developers - but nearly all of them require rolling your own support to get these various SDKs onto another interactive engine.</p> 
        <p>As a result, those Augmented Reality SDKs which directly support Unity are of the greatest interest to most commercial AR developers today - and what I've found is that there are quite a few more of these SDKs than most aspiring AR developers are aware of. &nbsp;(There are also a ton of others which don't provide native Unity support which I'll briefly cover at the end of this article, so if you're more interested in rolling your own support, or plan to use it in Unreal or on the web, I'll provide links and descriptions there.) In most cases these SDKs will have a C# interface. In a couple of cases, JavaScript may be available where mentioned.</p> 
        <p>Let's get started!</p> 
        <p><img class="center" src="//:0" width="480" height="640" data-li-src="https://media.licdn.com/mpr/mpr/shrinknp_800_800/AAEAAQAAAAAAAAilAAAAJGMyNmJmYTc2LTgyMmMtNGZjNC04MDVmLWRhOGI1NDE2ODY3Ng.jpg"></p> 
        <p>&nbsp;1. The ARToolkit SDK from DAQRI</p> 
        <p><a href="http://daqri.com/" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">DAQRI</a> is a sleeping giant of the Augmented Reality world (<a href="https://www.linkedin.com/company/1958425" target="_blank">see them here</a> on LinkedIn). Already famous for the still-unreleased DAQRI Smart Helmet, and their numerous acquisitions of startlingly futuristic tech centered around everything from mind-reading headbands to holographic headset technology, they're almost more of a well-funded thinktank than a traditional tech company. They acquired ARToolworks in 2015 and along with it, the ARToolkit SDK which they promptly re-released as a fully open-source project, releasing all of the features previously available only in the "Pro" version to the open source community.</p> 
        <p class="center"><img class="center" src="//:0" width="640" height="427" data-li-src="https://media.licdn.com/mpr/mpr/shrinknp_800_800/AAEAAQAAAAAAAAdUAAAAJGEzNjM3OTY0LTRjOWMtNGI5Ny05ZjAwLTBiMDQxNjllYzRhMg.jpg"><em>The DAQRI Smart Helmet</em></p> 
        <p>Of special interest is that ARToolkit was originally developed in 1999 -&nbsp;a decade and a half&nbsp;before the first developer releases of commercial AR headsets. ARToolkit was originally developed by <a href="https://www.linkedin.com/in/hirokazu-kato-72641912" target="_blank">Hirokazu Kato</a> at NAIST (<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nara_Institute_of_Science_and_Technology" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Nara Institute of Science and Technology</a>&nbsp;- perhaps the most prestigious Japanese post-graduate research university) and later released by the University of Washington's famous HIT Lab. If you're not familiar with the Human Interface Technology Lab&nbsp;already (and its often-related "sister lab" the Human Photonics Lab), they're one of&nbsp;the more&nbsp;influential academic groups in the past 20 years of Augmented and Mixed Reality, responsible for key research in everything from&nbsp;Virtual Retinal Display technology (rumored to be the underlying tech for Magic Leap's mythical headset) to&nbsp;research into shared virtual spaces. It's almost impossible to review the literature on Extended Reality&nbsp;without encountering notable papers out of this research group.</p> 
        <p>Thanks to DAQRI's OpenSource release of ARToolkit, all of the features described below are available for free on a variety of platforms.</p> 
        <p><em><strong>ARToolkit provides support for three general categories of tracker:</strong></em></p> 
        <ul> 
         <li><strong>Natural Feature Tracking (NFT)</strong><br>NFT is a sort of gold standard for feature tracking - it supports freeform 2d images which may not have a clearly defined and consistent&nbsp;outside edge. Examples include anything from a drink coaster to a&nbsp;roadside billboard.</li> 
         <li><strong>Traditional template square marker</strong><br>These markers are generally a fairly simple icon with a mandatory solid black border around the periphery. These are best thought of as "designed" markers rather than images that you may already exist organically.</li> 
         <li><strong>2D Barcode Markers<br></strong>These markers are predefined (in the SDK itself) and are typically highly optimized for rapid detection and solid tracking in variable lighting conditions. Because they're so highly optimized, it's typically possible to have more of them detected and tracked simultaneously, and they'll be rapidly detected under circumstances that other types of tracker might not be detected at all. These look more like fiducial markers or low-resolution QR Codes than other types of marker, so they'll typically be obvious where you might want something more discreet.</li> 
        </ul> 
        <p><em><strong>There are a number of features that are somewhat unique to ARToolkit.</strong></em></p> 
        <ul> 
         <li><strong>Multiple camera support</strong><br>While the most obvious example of multiple camera support would be for stereo cameras, this does not seem to be the limit for ARToolkit. Multiple cameras are supported in other configurations as well. The presence of overlapping areas, such as found&nbsp;in a stereo camera arrangement, provides extremely robust pose estimation for the camera array.</li> 
         <li><strong>One of comparatively few SDKs that supports Windows Phone<br></strong>Windows Phone hasn't gotten as much love from the AR developer community as its competing architectures, but ARToolkit provides support.</li> 
         <li><strong>Very robust tracking for markers over a range of distances<br></strong>Training ARToolkit for Natural Feature Tracking gives it a notion of what features will stand out over varying distances - something fewer SDKs take into account. It's not uncommon for an images features to blur together at a distance and what worked close by to become less meaningful at a distance. Likewise, the opposite is true - that features that are trackable at a distance become a distinct array of their own smaller features as you approach. ARToolkit takes this into account.</li> 
         <li><strong>JavaScript-based version for integration into web frameworks</strong><br>I'm still surprised when I see this, even though it's fairly common. ARToolkit has a companion JavaScript SDK known as&nbsp;JSARToolKit. Don't get confused - there's also another JavaScript AR SDK that is also called&nbsp;JSARToolKit - so make sure you're looking at <a href="https://github.com/artoolkit/jsartoolkit5" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">this one</a>, not <a href="https://github.com/kig/JSARToolKit" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">this one</a>! (We'll talk about that second one below).</li> 
        </ul> 
        <p><strong>Extra Super-Special Bonus<br></strong>Thanks to the Open Source nature of the ARToolkit SDK (it's released under LGPLv3), it's the only AR library I'm aware of that currently has publicly available Unreal Engine support, contributed by one of its users - so if you're dead set on using Unreal for AR, DAQRI's ARToolkit might demand&nbsp;your first look.</p> 
        <p>You can <a href="http://artoolkit.org/" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">download the ARToolkit SDK here</a>.</p> 
        <p><img class="center" src="//:0" width="480" height="640" data-li-src="https://media.licdn.com/mpr/mpr/shrinknp_800_800/AAEAAQAAAAAAAAdKAAAAJGY2ZWU2YjliLWI3ZjktNGZmNi1iZTNkLTkxMGJmNTc3ODM5Mg.jpg"></p> 
        <h3>2. The CraftAR SDK from Catchoom</h3> 
        <p>A spinoff of Telefonica R&amp;D (a large, and one of the first, high tech R&amp;D centers&nbsp;located in Spain), <a href="https://www.linkedin.com/company/catchoom" target="_blank">Catchoom</a> is&nbsp;an award-winning image and object recognition provider. CraftAR is designed&nbsp;to capture&nbsp;distant or partially occluded targets in addition to those in more ideal settings, doing so against both an on-device database and a cloud-based set of targets.</p> 
        <p>For some reason, Catchoom has decided to implement a per-target cost for their cloud platform which to me seems like a strange licensing scheme. Instead of being based on a total impressions model (which still&nbsp;plays into it to a lesser degree) they're charging based on "collections" and "targets". There *is* something to be said for the computational weight of comparing a set of image features against a larger set, but this still feels off to me. I'd love to hear what clients are opting for this plan.</p> 
        <ul> 
         <li>20 Images and 1000 Cloud-based scans: Free, Time-unlimited</li> 
         <li>Cloud Starter: 1 Collection, 100 Images, 100k Cloud-based Scans/month, $119-$169/month ($119 if prepaid for the year)</li> 
         <li>Cloud Plus: Unlimited Collections, 1000 Images, 150k Cloud-based Scans/month, $289-$349/month ($289 if prepaid for the year)</li> 
         <li>Cloud Pro: Custom setups available</li> 
         <li>On-Device Image Recognition. Unlimited apps, $2890</li> 
         <li>On-Device Augmented Reality. Unlimited apps, $2290</li> 
        </ul> 
        <p>On-device image recognition and on-device AR come in at a better rate since there is&nbsp;no particular target or application&nbsp;limit, but their pricing may be too steep for shops that may only intend to release a single app (versus other SDKs).</p> 
        <p><img class="center" src="//:0" width="640" height="432" data-li-src="https://media.licdn.com/mpr/mpr/shrinknp_800_800/AAEAAQAAAAAAAAjJAAAAJGNlOTViOGZjLTJkMTQtNGI1Ni05NDkwLTRiNWY5MGFjNTNhMA.png"></p> 
        <p class="center"><em>A suggested use case for CraftAR from Catchoom</em></p> 
        <p>Catchoom employs a distributed infrastructure, with servers dispatched regionally, to reduce ping times and better manage bandwidth so that cloud recognition is as fast as possible. This is really their strong suit - providing responsive cloud-based recognition and I suspect that if this is your primary use case, this is why you'd most want to give them a go. Their on-device license fees only make sense if you *know* you're going to be creating at least a half dozen mobile augmented reality apps - if not, you should look elsewhere as there are better options for less.</p> 
        <p><em><strong>CraftAR provides the following types of target:</strong></em></p> 
        <ul> 
         <li><strong>Cloud Image Recognition<br></strong>Catchoom handles both image recognition (IR) and augmented reality (AR) using related SDKs, so it makes sense to discuss both here. It is able to recognize images, including those of "real life" objects that may have a small 3d presence - for instance, the front of a car stereo or the like. Depending on how many objects you want to be able to recognize, your monthly fees will increase.</li> 
         <li><strong>On-Device Image Recognition<br></strong>The option to store the targets on the device exists, as well, providing a self-contained app that doesn't need network access (and, conversely, must be updated to recognize more targets or generate different behaviors).</li> 
         <li><strong>Cloud-based Augmented Reality<br></strong>Similar to Catchoom's IR behavior, their cloud-based AR solution is able to provide recognition for a range of elements in a cloud repository and to return pose data for those 2d images. All of CraftAR's targets are free-form Natural Feature Tracking (NFT) Targets.&nbsp;</li> 
         <li><strong>On-Device Augmented Reality<br></strong>And of course, on-device NFT targets are an option as well.</li> 
         <li><strong>Hybrid Cloud/Device AR<br></strong>The CraftAR SDK has an interesting compromise state that allows the device to download the target profiles and to update them periodically rather than requiring an always-on connection to support cloud-based AR. This can substantially reduce ping times and ease&nbsp;network congestion, ideal for situations where you may have many people accessing the app at one time, such as for trade shows and other live events that could experience massive bursts in traffic.</li> 
        </ul> 
        <p>It's also worth noting&nbsp;that CraftAR has support for native iOS and Android SDKs, as well as Cordova and Unity plugin support for Augmented Reality. Catchoom's Image Recognition API supports an even wider range with&nbsp;Javascript, Python, PHP and a Cordova Plugin in addition to native mobile SDKs.</p> 
        <p>Being limited to iOS and Android is a bit of a shock at this stage of the AR game, especially when Android support doesn't include Digital Eyewear like the Epson Moverio line, but if Android and iOS mobile device support&nbsp;is what you need, it's here. With the bonus of a closely-aligned Image Recognition library, Catchoom can be a one-stop-shop for a lot of contextual-awareness applications such as educational and training applications and plant monitoring software.</p> 
        <p>You can download CraftAR<a href="http://catchoom.com/product/craftar/augmented-reality-sdk/" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"> from here</a>.</p> 
        <p><img class="center" src="//:0" width="480" height="640" data-li-src="https://media.licdn.com/mpr/mpr/shrinknp_800_800/AAEAAQAAAAAAAAkxAAAAJDJiOTAyYTRjLTA4NTgtNGFjMC1iY2FiLTExM2ZlNmY5NzBmNw.jpg"></p> 
        <p>&nbsp;</p> 
        <h3>3. Beyond Reality's In2AR SDK</h3> 
        <p><a href="https://www.linkedin.com/company/1326192" target="_blank">Beyond Reality</a>&nbsp;is a Dutch company that calls themselves "a creative company, creating new technologies or looking for new ways to implement existing technologies." To that end, they created their own AR engine called IN2AR and have been aggressively involved in the Extended Reality creative space, while marketing the IN2AR SDK for commercial&nbsp;consumption.</p> 
        <p class="center"><img class="center" src="//:0" width="600" height="417" data-li-src="https://media.licdn.com/mpr/mpr/shrinknp_800_800/AAEAAQAAAAAAAAieAAAAJDY5YjJmNGVlLTY2N2EtNDgxMy04ZDk4LTgyNzgyZTlmODNhZg.jpg"><em>An IN2AR Screenshot from Unity</em></p> 
        <p><strong>Types of Target:</strong></p> 
        <ul> 
         <li><strong>Reference image recognition<br></strong>The IN2AR SDK supports Natural Feature Tracking as do most of the other SDKs on our list, with some up-front training for that image.</li> 
         <li><strong>Object Tracking?<br></strong>I'd seen some verbiage that sounded like IN2AR might&nbsp;support tracking of complex objects&nbsp;- and their support forum has one or two comments from others asking about it, so I wasn't completely alone in misreading it. I don't think they're being deliberately misleading anywhere, but IN2AR does not appear to support complex objects. Slightly curved objects, like a beer bottle label, are supported per their feedback on the support forum - but don't expect it to be able to recognize a complex object like a carburetor, for instance.</li> 
        </ul> 
        <p><strong>Pricing Structure / License:</strong></p> 
        <ul> 
         <li><strong>Free Trial License</strong><br>Predefined markers are provided with the unitypackage<br>Users may register and create markers and marker collections on the site. Unregistered target collections will apply a watermark.</li> 
         <li><strong>Priced by number of targets:</strong> 
          <ul> 
           <li>1 to 4: €999</li> 
           <li>1 to 5: €1150</li> 
           <li>1 to 6: €1300</li> 
           <li>1 to 50: €1450</li> 
          </ul> </li> 
        </ul> 
        <p>Beyond Reality seem to have a great product here, but their "per target" pricing model feels like the way a creative studio might bill for app development, not the way a SAAS provider would bill for a scalable service. I hope they'll update their pricing model soon to something more dynamic, sensible and subscription-based, but I'm not holding my breath. I suspect they may be more interested in having their own engine and producing creative content than they are at being a software company, though it's certainly possible that their pricing model is based on providing excellent customer support - at which point, it might even be regarded as reasonable.</p> 
        <p>Still, at the price points they indicate, a relatively simple AR app that I developed has 78 unique targets - each of which presents two different pieces of data depending on orientation. That 78 target number isn't even covered in their pricing scheme which covers up to 50 - and would run somewhere beyond&nbsp;€1450 - or over $1600 - in engine licensing alone. There are plenty of other options that come in well below that, so it's hard to imagine how someone selects&nbsp;IN2AR at that price point unless they couldn't find another AR solution.</p> 
        <p>And that's part of the problem with where we are today with Augmented and Mixed Reality: few developers&nbsp;are making bank off of it right now. There's still a lot to sort out, including how we monetize "spatial content." It's one thing if you're selling a game and can appeal to game prices, but for truly original experiences, such as ways to visualize contextual data in the real world, the rules have yet to be written. The companies most rapidly embracing AR at the enterprise level are companies with millions to burn. That's an awkward way&nbsp;to set prices, but it feels like what's driving pricing for a number of AR SDK&nbsp;providers&nbsp;in 2016.</p> 
        <p>You can download IN2AR <a href="http://www.in2ar.com/" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">from here</a>.</p> 
        <p><img class="center" src="//:0" width="480" height="640" data-li-src="https://media.licdn.com/mpr/mpr/shrinknp_800_800/AAEAAQAAAAAAAAfmAAAAJDJmNGE5ZTJlLWZmMzctNDc2NC05ZDc3LTdiOTg0NmI1MTAyYw.jpg"></p> 
        <h3>4. The Kudan AR SDK (from Kudan!)</h3> 
        <p><a href="https://www.linkedin.com/company/2054087" target="_blank">Kudan </a>as a company was founded in 2011 in Bristol, UK, expressly to deliver this really great and unique AR SDK. I'm not able to mark any digital eyewear support because it doesn't officially support any particular eyewear, but it's designed to be flexible enough to be calibrated for other devices meaning it's reasonable to get it working on the Moverio glasses, even if there's no direct support.</p> 
        <p>Probably the most unique thing about the Kudan SDK, and the one feature that gives it an edge over every other Augmented Reality SDK that I'm aware of, is that it has very robust single-camera SLAM.</p> 
        <p>SLAM is a computer vision technology that allows a device to track its position while simultaneously mapping its environment - and Kudan has one of the most readily deployable solutions. (The now-dark "Metaio" - acquired by Apple - also had robust single-camera SLAM support but is no longer publicly available, as did the Pointcloud SDK&nbsp;from 13th Lab which was acquired by Facebook/Oculus and which also went dark).</p> 
        <p class="center"><img class="center" src="//:0" width="600" height="338" data-li-src="https://media.licdn.com/mpr/mpr/shrinknp_800_800/AAEAAQAAAAAAAAiyAAAAJGQxNmE2NGJjLTQyMzgtNGZhYi1hZWU3LTI5YTlkMjhkYjliOQ.jpg"><em>Kudan's SDK can return&nbsp;camera pose data for spatial tracking in mobile VR</em></p> 
        <p>SLAM is a great way to convey accurate spatial information to a mobile device not just for Augmented and Mixed Reality but for Virtual Reality as well. Vastly superior to the simple rotational tracking for Cardboard/Daydream applications, this can replicate the kind of roomscale (or larger) tracking of hardware kits&nbsp;like the HTC Vive's lighthouse controllers.</p> 
        <p>The Kudan SDK's tracking behavior for targets is flexible and technically&nbsp;limitless. Target preparation happens through local tools on the developer side (rather than online / in the cloud as many other SDKs) and they impose no limits on the number of targets that can be tracked. Additionally, targets may be defined in the application at runtime.</p> 
        <p>My biggest concern about Kudan as an Augmented Reality SDK is that every other SDK that incorporated SLAM has been acquired and silenced as it was presumably wrapped into someone else's future offerings. Customers who were using Metaio were able to run out their contracts, but if their apps required SLAM (rather than just AR tracking) they switched to Kudan or closed down unless they could get by with Vuforia's less-robust "extended tracking."</p> 
        <p>The £1,000 per app price tag is steep by comparison to some other solutions if all you need is marker recognition, but with the market cornered for mobile SLAM solutions, it's automatically the best price for robust extended tracking. If you need a mobile app with real spatial awareness, Kudan is your best solution unless you roll your own.</p> 
        <p>You can download Kudan <a href="https://www.kudan.eu/" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">from here</a>.</p> 
        <p><img class="center" src="//:0" width="480" height="640" data-li-src="https://media.licdn.com/mpr/mpr/shrinknp_800_800/AAEAAQAAAAAAAAktAAAAJGI4MDRkNjM1LWQ5MzgtNDUyNy04YzZmLTg5YjVjZTVkMWJmZg.jpg"></p> 
        <h3>5. The Maxst AR SDK (Max Star?) from Maxst Co</h3> 
        <p>Founded in 2010, <a href="https://www.linkedin.com/company/6378072" target="_blank">Maxst</a> is based in Seoul, Korea, and has been developing augmented reality adapted services and projects with the goal of achieving better-than-best performance, as exemplified in their name "Maxst" - like "Maximum-est."</p> 
        <p>As a newer SDK, Maxst AR focuses specifically on Natural Feature Tracking, and provides a fairly wide range of deployment platforms, including the Moverio smartglasses.</p> 
        <p><img class="center" src="//:0" width="640" height="369" data-li-src="https://media.licdn.com/mpr/mpr/shrinknp_800_800/AAEAAQAAAAAAAAfgAAAAJDZmNTY0MzFmLWVmZGMtNGM3MS04NWQ0LTQ1ZmZhYThhYmQ1Yg.png"></p> 
        <p class="center"><em>A good range of platforms and a free entry-level pricetag make Max ST AR attractive</em></p> 
        <p>Maxst AR supports up to 200 target images per "channel" and there is no charge for target prep or handling. The free version displays a watermark but has no restrictions on commercial use. Additionally, their $999 licensing fee allows for unlimited targets and unlimited applications with no ongoing fees, with the ability to build apps for iOS, Android, Windows, MacOS X and for the Epson Moverio smartglasses.</p> 
        <p>Possibly the most awkward downside is that the Maxst AR SDK is currently limited to Unity's 32-bit version. They indicate that they will be supporting 64-bit Unity "soon". This may affect some larger projects but for most users it will be no more complicated than downloading the 32-bit version.</p> 
        <p>Give this one a try if the platforms match your needs - registration and target setup is easy and the unrestricted free version will give you a quick test for performance on your target device(s).&nbsp;</p> 
        <p>You can pick up the Maxst AR SDK <a href="https://developer.maxst.com/GetTheSDK" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">on this page</a>.</p> 
        <p><img class="center" src="//:0" width="480" height="640" data-li-src="https://media.licdn.com/mpr/mpr/shrinknp_800_800/AAEAAQAAAAAAAAi8AAAAJGI0ODJiNDI0LTIyM2YtNGZjMy1hYmEyLWJjZjJlMjlhZGUyNA.jpg"></p> 
        <h3>6. The Robocortex Rox AR SDK</h3> 
        <p>Robocortex is a French technology company specializing in Augmented Reality software and tools and providing services to industrial clients at industrial-size scale. Their Rox AR SDK started development as an ESM (Efficient second-order minimization - see below for what that means) computer vision SDK from a French national institute dedicated to digital research, "INRIA" who transferred the tech to Robocortex for maintenance and commercialization.</p> 
        <p><img class="center" src="//:0" width="416" height="312" data-li-src="https://media.licdn.com/mpr/mpr/shrinknp_800_800/AAEAAQAAAAAAAAh5AAAAJDY1ZDI5YzU0LTcyNDEtNDJjNS1hZGE1LWFlMzczMDJkOThjZQ.png"></p> 
        <p class="center"><em>An example of a Rox AR SDK project.</em></p> 
        <p>Very much focused on&nbsp;natural features&nbsp;and full object tracking, combining sensor fusion from the device IMU to improve tracking performance, the Rox AR SDK provides robust tracking and SLAM-like "driftless" mosaicking.&nbsp;</p> 
        <p>With a free evaluation license (you'll have to email them - visit<a href="http://robocortex.com/index.php/sdk" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"> their site here</a>) and a&nbsp;750€&nbsp;professional production license, they're one of the more accessible solutions and easy enough to try out. I find the "send us an email" approach to licensing to be a bit unusual in the Augmented Reality world, but it's fairly typical for software from specialized industrial software markets. Robocortex certainly straddles those two worlds.</p> 
        <p>The Robocortex solution is quite different from most of the other SDKs listed here. While the approach in most Augmented Reality SDKs relies on sparse feature detection (the breaking down of a potentially complex target into a much smaller number of recognizable features) Rox uses an approach they call "Efficient Second-order Approximation Method" or ESM which allows analysis of&nbsp;direct methods and dense data in realtime.</p> 
        <p>Reading their documentation feels more like reading an academic paper than most of the other solutions here, and that makes sense considering where their algorithms originated. You may want to be prepared for that going in - but I found it refreshingly informative. You can really dig into the SDK - there's a lot of functionality there, far more than you're likely used to seeing.</p> 
        <p>This means, though, that integration with Unity is much less drag-and-drop than you'll find from most of the rest on this list. Unity compatibility is provided through a C# wrapper to the main SDK in C.&nbsp;While most other AR SDKs&nbsp;provide a unitypackage with some prefabs that can get basic functionality into your application in just a short while, you may find yourself taking a bit longer with Rox. The results promise&nbsp;to be considerably more robust, however. This is an AR system that could be adapted to missile guidance and autonomous vehicles, directly from what they provide. It's powerful. If you need an AR solution that can predict the motion of targets as they move behind foreground obstructions, and as they drift far enough away that little of their original detail is available to the camera sensor, Rox is your solution.</p> 
        <p>While they only list iOS, Android, Windows and Linux as supported operating systems, they indicate in their product brochure that additional platforms are available on demand. After speaking with them, they saw no reason that the current release shouldn't operate on Hololens and were quick to offer me a 7 day trial license to try it out. I suspect compiling it for OSX wouldn't be out of the question, either, but is not immediately supported.</p> 
        <p>Check out the Rox AR SDK <a href="http://www.robocortex.com/index.php/sdk?view=featured" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">on this page</a>.</p> 
        <p><img class="center" src="//:0" width="480" height="640" data-li-src="https://media.licdn.com/mpr/mpr/shrinknp_800_800/AAEAAQAAAAAAAAlVAAAAJGU2ZGJhMTE5LThmMDMtNDM2ZC05YmZmLWJhM2FhNmM0MjE2NA.jpg"></p> 
        <h3>7. The Vuforia SDK from PTC</h3> 
        <p>And last, but certainly not least, is perhaps the most well-known&nbsp;of the commercially available Augmented Reality SDKs.&nbsp;</p> 
        <p>Vuforia was originally developed by Qualcomm and optimized for their chipsets (aiming primarily at&nbsp;mobile performance). After the disappearance of Metaio subsequent to&nbsp;the Apple acquisition, Vuforia quickly took its place as the centerpiece of most AR conversation in app development.</p> 
        <p><img class="center" src="//:0" width="600" height="360" data-li-src="https://media.licdn.com/mpr/mpr/shrinknp_800_800/AAEAAQAAAAAAAAfyAAAAJGE1NjMxZjQ3LThmZjktNGQ2MC05YTFlLTI1OTg1NDJmOTBmYw.jpg"></p> 
        <h3>Target Types</h3> 
        <ul> 
         <li>Complex Objects (scanned using an Android app that develops an internal model of the object in question)</li> 
         <li>User-defined Images (both locally stored and “cloud-based”)</li> 
         <li>Cylinders (with user-defined wrap-around imagery)</li> 
         <li>Text (100,000 words or a custom vocabulary)</li> 
         <li>Boxes (Simple cubes and rectangular boxes&nbsp;with unique user-defined side images)</li> 
         <li>Frame markers (QR-code like, limited number of unique ones, extremely fast recognition in challenging lighting conditions)</li> 
        </ul> 
        <h3>Pricing Structure &amp; Licensing</h3> 
        <ul> 
         <li>Free and Commercial SDK options. Closed-source.</li> 
         <li>Ranges from $0 (for a watermarked “starter” license) to $499 (one time fee) for “Classic” license (per application, no watermark, no support for cloud recognition)</li> 
         <li>Cloud recognition tiers are subscription based and range from $99 to $999 a month depending on recognition volume.</li> 
        </ul> 
        <h3>Special Features</h3> 
        <ul> 
         <li>Extremely varied range of marker types</li> 
         <li>Aggressive development</li> 
         <li>Early HoloLens support available (beta)</li> 
         <li>Triggers - areas of markers that when covered (for example - touching with a finger) can trigger events</li> 
        </ul> 
        <h3>Concerns</h3> 
        <ul> 
         <li>$499 fee per-application could be steep for beginning developers, especially now when AR app development isn’t a robustly moneymaking venture yet. Would be nice to have a general developer license that allowed multiple apps under one umbrella at around that pricepoint.</li> 
         <li>Always-on watermarks for the starter license. Prior versions displayed the watermark the first time the application was launched in a particular day which was much preferable. Demonstrating apps in development is pretty ugly with watermarks applied constantly.</li> 
        </ul> 
        <p>&nbsp;Vuforia is available <a href="https://www.vuforia.com/" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">on its own website</a>.</p>

It's powerful. If you need an AR solution that can predict the motion of targets as they move behind foreground obstructions, and as they drift far enough away that little of their original detail is available to the camera sensor, Rox is your solution.\u003c/p\u003e \n\u003cp\u003eWhile they only list iOS, Android, Windows and Linux as supported operating systems, they indicate in their product brochure that additional platforms are available on demand.
