         <div class="firstPar">
          <p> <i>When in a restaurant while on holiday in the US, I was asked by the waitress if I wanted to pay the bill in pounds instead of dollars. </i> </p>
         </div>
         <div class="secondPar">
          <p> <i>But when I was handed the card terminal, I could see the sterling price was much more expensive than if I'd been offered the exchange rate charged by my bank. Was I really being charged more?</i> </p>
         </div>
         <div class="thirdPar">
          <p align="right"> <strong>EE, via email</strong> </p>
         </div>
         <div class="fourthPar">
          <p> You'll pay a 5pc markup by paying in pounds at foreign shops, restaurants and hotels instead of opting for the local currency thanks to a little-known option known as "dynamic currency conversion" or DCC. </p>
         </div>
         <div class="fifthPar">
          <p> The add-on is completely legal and operated by companies that profit by converting your bill to sterling using a poor exchange rate. This lets them pocket a fee on every transaction. </p>
         </div>
         <div class="body">
          <p> You might also face bank charges for using your debit or credit card in a foreign shop; these will normally apply whether you pay in sterling or the local currency. </p>
          <p> A 5pc markup would add £50 in avoidable charges to holiday spending of £1,000 – on top of the charges from your card provider. </p>
          <p> Shops may also take a cut from the DCC "service", so it is in their interests to persuade holidaymakers to pay in their home currency. But by law they should give you the option of paying in the local currency. </p>
          <p></p>
          <h3 class="was-h3"> Is it ever cheaper to pay in pounds? </h3>
          <p></p>
          <p> Jennifer Conneely of the Dynamic Currency Conversion Forum, an industry body representing five operators that profit from DCC, said there were several "myths" surrounding the option. </p>
          <p> She said: "We're often misrepresented in the media as being high-cost. But it is possible to save money on small-value transactions." </p>
          <p> Buying a £3 coffee, for example, could cost 6p to 15p in DCC fees but could cost £1.50 in bank fees, if your bank imposes a minimum exchange fee for foreign transactions. </p>
          <p> But it is rare for a bank to charge so much, and borrowers can sign up for a fee-free debit or credit card to avoid holiday exchange fees completely. </p>
          <p> Ms Conneely said: "People use us because it's reassuring to leave a shop and know the final price you've paid. If you're not familiar with the foreign currency, you may not know how much you have spent." </p>
          <p></p>
          <h3 class="was-h3"> What's the best way to pay in foreign currency? </h3>
          <p></p>
          <p> You can avoid bank fees when buying abroad by signing up for a low-cost credit or debit card. </p>
          <p> Halifax's Clarity MasterCard and the Post Office Platinum credit card are both free and don't charge for foreign purchases, although both will charge interest from day one if you use them at a cashpoint. </p>
          <p> They use Mastercard's <a target="_blank" href="https://www.mastercard.com/global/currencyconversion/">exchange rates</a>. Visa's <a target="_blank" href="http://www.visaeurope.com/making-payments/exchange-rates">rates</a> tend to be slightly poorer. </p>
          <p> <i>– <a href="mailto:kate.palmer@telegraph.co.uk">kate.palmer@telegraph.co.uk</a> </i> </p>
