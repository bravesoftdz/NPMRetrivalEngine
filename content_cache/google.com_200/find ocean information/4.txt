   <div class="blurb" id="waves"> 
    <p class="center text">about ocean waves</p> 
    <p class="center text"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Significant_wave_height">Significant Wave Height</a> is the average height of the highest 1/3 of waves at a particular point in the ocean. There's a <a href="http://www.vos.noaa.gov/MWL/apr_06/waves.shtml">great writeup here</a> describing what this means.</p> 
    <p class="center text"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency">Peak Wave Period</a> is the (inverse) frequency of the most energetic waves passing through a particular point, whether wind generated or swells. Certainly, there are many more groups of waves moving through an area, each in different directions, but trying to show them all rapidly becomes complex. Instead, we show the one wave group contributing the most energy. This has the effect, though, of creating "boundaries" between regions of ocean where the #1 wave group suddenly switches to second place. Often these boundaries represent swell fronts, but other times they are just artifacts of the ranking mechanism.</p> 
   </div> 
   <div class="blurb" id="co2"> 
    <p class="center text">about CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations<br>for dates earlier than 2017-01-24 04:30 UTC</p> 
    <p class="center text">While implementing the visualization of CO<sub>2</sub> surface concentration, I noticed the <a href="http://gmao.gsfc.nasa.gov/GEOS/">NASA GEOS-5 model</a> reports a global mean concentration that differs significantly from widely reported numbers. For example, from the run at 2015-11-23 00:00 UTC, the global mean is only 368 ppmv whereas CO<sub>2</sub> observatories <a href="http://www.esrl.noaa.gov/gmd/ccgg/trends/weekly.html">report concentrations closer to 400 ppmv</a>. GEOS-5 was constructed in the 2000s, so perhaps the model does not account for accumulation of atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> over time? This is simply speculation. I am just not certain.</p> 
    <p class="center text">To bring the GEOS-5 results closer to contemporary numbers, I have added a uniform offset of +32 ppmv, increasing the global mean to 400 ppmv. This is not scientifically valid, but it does allow the visualization to become illustrative of the discussion occurring today around atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub>. Without question, I would welcome a more rigorous approach or an explanation why the GEOS-5 model produces the data that it does.</p> 
    <p class="center text">From 2017-01-24 04:30 UTC, this adjustment is no longer necessary because GEOS-5 appears to have been upgraded.</p>

   <div class="blurb" id="aot"> 
    <p class="center text">about aerosols and extinction</p> 
    <p class="center text">An <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerosol">aerosol</a> is air containing particles. Common particles are dust, smoke, soot, and water droplets (clouds). These particles affect sunlight primarily through absorption and scattering, which combine to reduce the amount of light reaching the ground. This loss of light as it passes through the atmosphere is called <a href="http://www-rohan.sdsu.edu/~aty/explain/extinction/extintro.html">extinction</a>.</p> 
    <p class="center text">One common measure of extinction is <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_depth">aerosol optical thickness</a> (AOT), which is (the log of) the ratio between the power of incoming light and the power of transmitted light. This helps us understand how "thick" the air is with particulates.</p>
