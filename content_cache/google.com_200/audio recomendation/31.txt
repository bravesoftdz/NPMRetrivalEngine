                <h3>Choose speaker systems by room size</h3> 
                <p>Most of the time, the home audio conversation starts with <a class="link" target="_blank" href="//www.newegg.com/Home-Audio-Speakers/SubCategory/ID-494">speaker recommendations</a>. If you are a brand loyalist--say you want to find deals on Bose speakers, or discounts on a Klipsch surround sound system--thats easily done using the site tools in the left navigation.                 <p>More important than brand, however, is choosing speakers that fit your home theater dimensions. Bigger is not always better for speakers, especially in a small room due to the physics of soundwaves.</p> 
                <p>Do not try for massive subwoofer systems where a <a class="link" target="_blank" href="//www.newegg.com/Wireless-Streaming-Audio/SubCategory/ID-3371?Tid=26718">little wireless Bluetooth speaker</a> is appropriate.</p> 
                <p>Vendors do not make fitting speakers to a room an exact science. In fact, it might seem that brands deliberately muddy the water for sizing your stereo and speakers.</p> 
                <p>Follow these common sense rules when picking the best home audio speakers for your room and you will be on the right track:</p> 
                <ul class="article-list-style-disc"> 
                 <li>Tower speaker height should not exceed half of your ceiling height. You have eight-foot ceilings? Speakers over four feet are overkill. In fact, unless youre doing pro audio a four foot speaker is probably the largest you should consider.</li> 
                 <li>If you want big bass, you need a big space. The physics of sound is such that a 30 Hz frequency (a bone-rattling low tone) sounds best at 37 feet away. A 60 Hz low-end is more appropriate for listening from 15-20 feet.</li> 
                 <li>Use <b><a class="link" target="_blank" href="//www.newegg.com/Stereo-Shelf-Systems/SubCategory/ID-644">bookshelf speakers for music</a></b>; this isnt a hard rule, but in general, most genres dont require ultra-low frequencies subwoofer unless you want to violate a noise ordinance.</li> 
                 <li>Subwoofers seal the deal for a movie-theater experience. Bass is for explosions and dinosaurs. If youre going to spring for 5.1 or 6.1 surround sound its a good idea to invest a little extra into the subwoofer. You want to feel low frequencies in your solar plexus.</li> 
                </ul>

                <p>In a surround sound setup, a box-like stereo receiver sends unique signals to two or more speakers, with an onboard amplifier providing power to the speakers.</p> 
                <p>The quarterback of the home audio system, a good stereo receiver orchestrates sonic frequencies and volume levels for the home audio system to create the effect of sitting right in the middle of an action film. This refinement of audio signal is called equalization, or (EQ).</p> 
                <p><a class="link" target="_blank" href="//www.newegg.com/Receivers/SubCategory/ID-488">Home surround sound receivers</a> offer precise EQ settings that fine-tune audio best suit the media and the room. The more speakers you have, the more precise you can get.</p> 
                <p>A surround sound numbering system quantifies various speaker setups and configurations:</p> 
                <ul class="article-list-style-disc"> 
                 <li>2.0 - A stereo setup with two speakers, a left and a right channel, situated in front of the viewer.</li> 
                 <li>3.0 - Stereo setup plus a dedicated, powered subwoofer, also called a LFE (low-frequency effects) bass channel.</li> 
                 <li>5.1 - Standard surround sound. Five channels total; four speakers placed around the perimeter plus a LFE.</li> 
                 <li>6.1 - Adds a third channel called the back surround speaker. It is located behind the viewer in most cases and enhances the effect of sonic movement. It is commonplace for studios to release 6.1 DVD and Blu-ray titles nowadays. They are compatible with 5.1 systems.</li>

                <p>Before you buy home audio or home theater equipment to incorporate into an existing setup, be aware of how Ohms, impedance, and sensitivity relate to each other. You need them to avoid buying audio equipment inappropriate for your space, or worse, fry your amplifier with the wrong speakers.</p> 
                <p>Two ironclad rules for home speaker systems:</p> 
                <ul class="article-list-style-disc"> 
                 <li>A stereo receiver and speakers must have the same impedance rating--this is measured in Ohms. The weird part is that high-end loudspeakers have lower ratings for ohms (4-6 ohms) than standard home audio speakers (usually 8 ohms). Mismatched Ohm ratings will feed too much energy into the stereos amplifier and cause damage.</li> 
                 <li>Sensitivity and decibel ratings vary by room size and are difficult to pin down in a meaningful way.</li> 
                </ul> 
                <p>We'll explain this without getting too deep into the physics.</p> 
                <ul class="article-list-style-disc"> 
                 <li>Impedance measures the strength of the regulation of power flow. Mismatched energy flow is bad news for amplifiers which are especially sensitive to excessive energywhich is what happens if paired with speakers with lower impedance.</li> 
                 <li>Decibels (dB) and sensitivity ratings depict how much electrical power (Watts) speakers use to achieve a loud, clear level of volume; a 90 decibel rating is solidly high-end.</li> 
                </ul> 
                <p>The catch is that sensitivity is inherently subjective, and very much dependent on room size, shape, and dozens of factors affecting acoustics.</p> 
                <p>In theory, sensitivity is measured like so:</p> 
                <p><i>One sensitivity unit equals one decibel of sound, produced by one watt of electricity, heard from a distance of one meter.</i></p> 
                <p>This looks nice and linear, but in reality sound is not that neat.</p> 
                <p>There are so many room variables that come into play that throw this off kilter. Add amplifier voltage to the mix suddenly you're dealing with Ohms law: (Power (watts) = Voltage (V) x Current (I) or Power = V/R (impedance in Ohms).</p> 
                <p>We will spare you the twelfth grade physics lesson in the interest of time and sanity.</p> 
                <p>Anyhow, room size and acoustics dramatically determine how sound is experienced. Probably more than voltage. Thanks to solid-state home audio equipment, voltage is more or less uniform. The downside is that your unique space makes sensitivity metrics a dreadful moving target.</p> 
                <h3>Buying speakers without listening?</h3> 
                <p>If decibels and sensitivity are finicky measures, how is it possible to buy speakers online before you hear them?</p> 
                <p>The good news is that sensitivity provides a yardstick for online home audio system shoppers if you know your room.</p> 
                <p>Finding the best sounding home audio literally requires calculus. But, if you know a few of the variables it helps you understand what you should be installing for your home theater.</p> 
                <p>So before you buy Klipsch, Polk, Bose, whatever--first ask yourself two questions:</p> 
                <ul class="article-list-style-disc"> 
                 <li>At how many decibels is this audio intended to be experienced?</li> 
                 <li>How large is my space?</li> 
                </ul> 
                <h3>Think of decibels on this scale:</h3> 
                <ul class="article-list-style-disc"> 
                 <li>Careless Whisper: 15-25 dB</li> 
                 <li>Background chatter: 40-60 dB</li> 
                 <li>Normal conversation: 65-70 dB</li> 
                 <li>Philharmonic playing fortissimo: 105 dB</li> 
                 <li>Metal concert: 120+ dB</li> 
                 <li>Pain threshold: 130 dB</li> 
                 <li>Airport tarmac: 140-180 dB</li> 
                </ul> 
                <h3>How does wattage fit in?</h3> 
                <p>Lets say you listen to the audio 32 feet from the speaker. It's a big rec room. Earlier you discovered that a 32-watt amplifier pushes 87 decibels, optimally, for your room. You want to make it louder, so youre checking out stereos with higher watts.</p> 
                <p>Here is how much more Wattage it takes to move the needle volume-wise:</p> 
                <h3>Watts to decibels at 32 feet</h3> 
                <ul class="article-list-style-disc"> 
                 <li>1 x (32 Watts) : 87 dB</li> 
                 <li>2 x (32 Watts) : 90 dB</li> 
                 <li>4 x (32 Watts) : 93 dB</li> 
                 <li>10 x (32 Watts) : 97 dB</li> 
                 <li>20 x (32 Watts) : 100 dB</li> 
                 <li>40 x (32 Watts) : 103 dB</li> 
                 <li>100 x (32 Watts) :107 dB</li> 
                 <li>200 x (32 Watts) :110 dB</li> 
                 <li>400 x (32 Watts) : 113 dB</li> 
                </ul>

                <p>Installing in-wall speakers is popular for smaller home theater setups to save space. In-wall speakers are designed to blend into the room; for extra style points select speakers with grilles that match the paint of the home theater walls.</p> 
                <p>On-wall speakers you see the speaker chassis and it require less cutting and drilling for installs.</p> 
                <p>If you plan on a DIY installation of <a class="link" target="_blank" href="//www.newegg.com/Home-Audio-Speakers/SubCategory/ID-494">in-wall speakers</a>, note the specific recommendations for placement for the speakers. It may vary by brand, but in general:</p> 
                <ul class="article-list-style-disc"> 
                 <li>View at 8-12 feet from forward-facing speakers</li> 
                 <li>Place left and right channels 6-10 feet apart; at least 18 inches from the corner of the room.</li> 
                 <li>Place speakers at ear height; be consistent with the height for each channel.</li> 
                 <li>Vendors include a cut-out template for each speaker; hang all templates before cutting the walls. Usually you need masking tape around the perimeter.</li> 
                </ul> 
                <h3>DIY Tips for Mounting In-Wall or In-Ceiling Speakers</h3> 
                <p>Installing wall speakers is relatively straightforward if you have some experience with home improvement.</p> 
                <p>Use a stud finder to locate the studs or joists in the walls. On-wall speakers are mounted on the stud; in-wall speakers need to fit between them. A drywall saw cuts the larger chunks of the wall. Use a utility knife for smaller, detailed cutting.</p> 
                <p>Always shut off the power before cutting into walls. Be cautious about drilling into a pipe or electrical conduit; a good stud finder does not negate human error.</p> 
                <p>First drill a small pilot hole and probe the inside of the wall with a firm wire before cutting out speaker fittings. Large in-wall speakers might require several pilot holes.</p> 
                <p>In-wall speakers fasten with screw-on or bolt-on brackets that hold them steady in the wall; follow the directions.</p> 
                <p>After the install, stuff the cavity around the speaker with insulation. Make sure you are consistent with the density for every speaker.</p> 
                <p>If the speakers have tone controls leave the grille off until you EQ them to your liking.</p>
